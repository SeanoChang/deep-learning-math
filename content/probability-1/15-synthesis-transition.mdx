## The Logical Architecture

The course follows a strict logical chain. Each layer builds on the previous one.

| Layer | Objects | Key Results |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Events** | Sample space $\Omega$, $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}$, probability $P$ | Axioms, inclusion-exclusion, independence |
| **2. Random Variables** | Measurable functions $X : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ | CDF, PMF/PDF, joint distributions |
| **3. Expectation** | $E[X] = \sum x \, P(X=x)$ or $\int x f(x) dx$ | Linearity, indicator trick, variance |
| **4. Conditioning** | $P(A \mid B)$, $E[X \mid Y]$ | Bayes, tower property, total variance |
| **5. Limit Theorems** | $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$, $Z_n \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$ | WLLN, CLT |

<Callout type="info">
**The unifying theme.** At every layer, we approximate something complex by something tractable. Events are approximated by unions, integrals by sums, conditional distributions by observed data, and entire distributions by the Normal curve. The course is the story of making these approximations precise.
</Callout>

## Capstone Proof Chain 1: Markov to Chebyshev to WLLN

This is the most important proof chain in the course. Each step is one line.

**Markov's inequality.** For $X \geq 0$ and $a > 0$: $P(X \geq a) \leq E[X]/a$. (Proof: $E[X] \geq a \cdot P(X \geq a)$.)

**Chebyshev's inequality.** Apply Markov to $(X - \mu)^2$ with threshold $\varepsilon^2$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) = P((X-\mu)^2 \geq \varepsilon^2) \leq \frac{E[(X-\mu)^2]}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\varepsilon^2}.$$

**WLLN.** Apply Chebyshev to $\bar{X}_n$ (mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2/n$):

$$P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) \leq \frac{\sigma^2/n}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \to 0.$$

Three lines. Three theorems. The entire chain from the definition of expectation to the convergence of averages.

## Capstone Proof Chain 2: Conditioning and Computation

The conditioning machinery provides a systematic strategy for computing expectations and variances of complex random variables.

**Step 1 (Indicator trick).** Express counting random variables as sums of indicators: $X = \sum_{i} \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$. Then $E[X] = \sum_i P(A_i)$ by linearity.

**Step 2 (Tower property).** For hierarchical models, condition on the intermediate variable: $E[X] = E[E[X \mid N]]$.

**Step 3 (Total variance).** Decompose variance into explained and unexplained components: $\text{Var}(X) = E[\text{Var}(X \mid N)] + \text{Var}(E[X \mid N])$.

**Example (revisited).** Let $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ and given $N$, let $X \sim \text{Binomial}(N, p)$.

- $E[X \mid N] = Np$, so $E[X] = E[Np] = p\lambda$.
- $\text{Var}(X \mid N) = Np(1-p)$ and $E[X \mid N] = Np$.
- $\text{Var}(X) = E[Np(1-p)] + \text{Var}(Np) = \lambda p(1-p) + p^2 \lambda = \lambda p$.

This is the variance of $\text{Poisson}(\lambda p)$, and indeed $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda p)$ (provable via MGFs: the thinning property).

## Capstone Proof Chain 3: MGFs and Distribution Identification

The MGF machinery converts the hard problem of finding the distribution of a sum into algebra.

**Strategy.** To identify the distribution of $S = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ (independent):

1. Compute $M_{X_i}(t)$.
2. Form $M_S(t) = \prod M_{X_i}(t)$.
3. Recognize $M_S(t)$ as the MGF of a known family.
4. Invoke uniqueness.

**Applications proven this semester:**

- $\text{Bernoulli}(p) + \cdots + \text{Bernoulli}(p) \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$
- $\text{Poisson}(\lambda) + \text{Poisson}(\mu) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda + \mu)$
- $N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2) + N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2) \sim N(\mu_1 + \mu_2, \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2)$

## The Transition to Probability II

<Callout type="definition">
**What changes.** In Probability I, we worked with discrete and continuous random variables on concrete sample spaces. Probability II replaces this with a fully abstract framework based on measure theory.
</Callout>

### From Events to $\sigma$-Algebras

In Probability I, we treated $\sigma$-algebras informally (the collection of "reasonable" events). In Probability II, they become central:

- A **$\sigma$-algebra** $\mathcal{F}$ on $\Omega$ is a collection of subsets closed under complementation and countable unions.
- A **probability measure** $P : \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$ satisfies countable additivity.
- A **random variable** $X : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is **$\mathcal{F}$-measurable** if $\{X \leq x\} \in \mathcal{F}$ for all $x$.

The Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $\mathbb{R}$ is generated by open intervals and is the natural domain for "ordinary" random variables.

### From Conditional Expectation to the Radon-Nikodym Theorem

In Probability I, $E[X \mid Y = y]$ is defined by a formula (sum or integral). This breaks in general: what if $Y$ is neither discrete nor continuous? Probability II defines $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ for any sub-$\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ as the unique (a.s.) $\mathcal{G}$-measurable random variable satisfying:

$$E[\mathbf{1}_G \cdot E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]] = E[\mathbf{1}_G \cdot X] \quad \text{for all } G \in \mathcal{G}.$$

Existence is guaranteed by the **Radon-Nikodym theorem**. This abstract definition subsumes all our concrete formulas and extends to settings where they fail.

### From WLLN to SLLN and Martingales

The Weak Law (convergence in probability) is upgraded to the **Strong Law of Large Numbers**: $P(\bar{X}_n \to \mu) = 1$ (almost sure convergence). The proof uses more sophisticated tools (Borel-Cantelli lemmas, or martingale theory).

**Martingales** generalize the idea of a "fair game": a sequence $(M_n)$ adapted to a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_n)$ with $E[M_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = M_n$. The optional stopping theorem, martingale convergence theorem, and their applications are central to Probability II.

### From CLT to Characteristic Functions

The MGF heuristic for the CLT becomes rigorous when we replace MGFs by **characteristic functions** $\varphi_X(t) = E[e^{itX}]$, which always exist (since $|e^{itX}| = 1$). **L\'evy's continuity theorem** provides the bridge: pointwise convergence of characteristic functions implies convergence in distribution.

<Callout type="tip">
**The big picture.** Probability II does not invalidate anything from Probability I. Rather, it provides a framework that:

1. **Unifies** discrete and continuous cases under one theory,
2. **Extends** to settings that are neither (e.g., singular distributions),
3. **Strengthens** results (a.s. convergence vs. convergence in probability),
4. **Enables** new tools (martingales, ergodic theory, stochastic processes).
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem title="Exercise 15.1 (Proof Reconstruction)">
Without referring to notes, write out the complete proof of the WLLN from first principles. Your proof should include:

(a) The computation of $E[\bar{X}_n]$ and $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n)$,

(b) The statement and one-line proof of Markov's inequality,

(c) The derivation of Chebyshev from Markov,

(d) The application of Chebyshev to $\bar{X}_n$ to conclude convergence in probability.
</Problem>

<Solution>
**Setup.** Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = \mu$, $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty$. Let $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$.

**(a)** $E[\bar{X}_n] = \frac{1}{n}\sum E[X_i] = \mu$. Since the $X_i$ are independent: $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum \text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2/n$.

**(b) Markov's inequality.** For $Y \geq 0$ and $a > 0$: $Y \geq a \cdot \mathbf{1}_{Y \geq a}$, so $E[Y] \geq a \cdot P(Y \geq a)$, giving $P(Y \geq a) \leq E[Y]/a$.

**(c) Chebyshev from Markov.** Apply Markov to $Y = (\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 \geq 0$ with $a = \varepsilon^2$:

$$P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) = P((\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 \geq \varepsilon^2) \leq \frac{E[(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2]}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n)}{\varepsilon^2}.$$

**(d)** Substituting $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \sigma^2/n$:

$$P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 0.$$

Since this holds for every $\varepsilon > 0$, $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem title="Exercise 15.2 (Conceptual Synthesis)">
For each of the following results, identify (i) the key hypothesis, (ii) the technique used, and (iii) what fails when the hypothesis is removed.

(a) Markov's inequality.

(b) Chebyshev's inequality.

(c) The MGF factorization theorem.

(d) The CLT.
</Problem>

<Solution>
**(a) Markov.** (i) $X \geq 0$. (ii) Bounding $X \geq a \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X \geq a}$ and taking expectations. (iii) Without $X \geq 0$: let $X = -100$ a.s., then $P(X \geq 1) = 0$ but $E[X]/1 = -100 < 0$, so the bound $P \leq E[X]/a$ is vacuously true but the proof breaks (the inequality $X \geq a \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X \geq a}$ fails for negative $X$).

**(b) Chebyshev.** (i) Finite variance $\sigma^2 < \infty$. (ii) Apply Markov to $(X - \mu)^2$. (iii) Without finite variance (e.g., Cauchy distribution): $\text{Var}(X) = \infty$, so the bound $\sigma^2/k^2 = \infty$ is useless.

**(c) MGF factorization.** (i) Independence of $X$ and $Y$. (ii) $E[e^{tX}e^{tY}] = E[e^{tX}]E[e^{tY}]$ from independence. (iii) Without independence: if $Y = X$, then $M_{X+Y}(t) = M_{2X}(t) = M_X(2t) \neq M_X(t)^2$ in general.

**(d) CLT.** (i) i.i.d. with finite mean and variance. (ii) MGF/characteristic function convergence under $\sqrt{n}$ normalization. (iii) Without finite variance (e.g., i.i.d. Cauchy): $\bar{X}_n$ has the same Cauchy distribution for all $n$ (no convergence to Normal; the average is as variable as a single observation). $\square$
</Solution>
