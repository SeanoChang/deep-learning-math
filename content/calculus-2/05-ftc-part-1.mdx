# The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part I

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is one of the great achievements of mathematics. It connects
the two central operations of calculus -- differentiation and integration -- which were developed
independently. Part I says that integration followed by differentiation recovers the original function:
if $f$ is continuous, then the "area function" $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt$ is differentiable with $F'(x) = f(x)$.

## The Integral Function

<Callout type="definition" title="The Integral Function">
Let $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be integrable. The **integral function** (or **accumulation function**) of $f$ is

$$F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt, \qquad x \in [a, b].$$

Note: $F(a) = 0$ and $F(b) = \int_a^b f$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="Geometric Interpretation">
$F(x)$ measures the signed area under the graph of $f$ from $a$ to $x$. As $x$ increases, $F(x)$ accumulates more area. Where $f > 0$, $F$ is increasing; where $f < 0$, $F$ is decreasing. Where $f = 0$, $F$ has a horizontal tangent.
</Callout>

## Continuity of the Integral Function

Before proving differentiability, we establish a weaker but important property: the integral function is always continuous, even when $f$ is merely integrable (not necessarily continuous).

<Callout type="theorem" title="Continuity of the Integral Function">
If $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is integrable (hence bounded), then $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt$ is continuous on $[a, b]$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Since $f$ is integrable on $[a, b]$, it is bounded: there exists $M > 0$ with $|f(t)| \leq M$ for all $t \in [a, b]$.

Let $x, y \in [a, b]$ with $x < y$. By additivity of the integral:

$$F(y) - F(x) = \int_a^y f - \int_a^x f = \int_x^y f.$$

By the integral bound theorem and $-M \leq f(t) \leq M$:

$$|F(y) - F(x)| = \left|\int_x^y f\right| \leq \int_x^y |f| \leq M(y - x) = M|y - x|.$$

Given $\epsilon > 0$, choose $\delta = \epsilon / M$. Then $|y - x| < \delta$ implies $|F(y) - F(x)| \leq M \cdot \delta = \epsilon$.

In fact, $F$ is **Lipschitz continuous** with Lipschitz constant $M$, which is stronger than mere continuity. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Lipschitz Is Stronger Than Continuous">
We proved $|F(y) - F(x)| \leq M|y - x|$ -- the function $F$ cannot change faster than rate $M$. Lipschitz continuity implies uniform continuity, which implies continuity. The bound $M$ on $f$ directly controls how fast the accumulated area can change.
</Callout>

## The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part I

<Callout type="theorem" title="FTC Part I">
Let $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be integrable, and suppose $f$ is continuous at a point $c \in (a, b)$. Then the integral function $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt$ is differentiable at $c$ and

$$F'(c) = f(c).$$

In particular, if $f$ is continuous on all of $[a, b]$, then $F$ is differentiable on $(a, b)$ and $F' = f$ on $(a, b)$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** We must show

$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{F(c + h) - F(c)}{h} = f(c).$$

By additivity, $F(c + h) - F(c) = \int_c^{c+h} f(t) \, dt$. (We take $h > 0$; the case $h < 0$ is analogous.) Therefore

$$\frac{F(c+h) - F(c)}{h} = \frac{1}{h} \int_c^{c+h} f(t) \, dt.$$

Since $\int_c^{c+h} f(c) \, dt = f(c) \cdot h$, we can write

$$\frac{F(c+h) - F(c)}{h} - f(c) = \frac{1}{h} \int_c^{c+h} (f(t) - f(c)) \, dt.$$

Now we use continuity of $f$ at $c$. Let $\epsilon > 0$. Choose $\delta > 0$ such that $|t - c| < \delta \implies |f(t) - f(c)| < \epsilon$. For $0 < h < \delta$ and $t \in [c, c+h]$, we have $|t - c| \leq h < \delta$, so $|f(t) - f(c)| < \epsilon$. Therefore

$$\left|\frac{F(c+h) - F(c)}{h} - f(c)\right| = \frac{1}{h} \left|\int_c^{c+h} (f(t) - f(c)) \, dt\right| \leq \frac{1}{h} \int_c^{c+h} |f(t) - f(c)| \, dt < \frac{1}{h} \cdot \epsilon \cdot h = \epsilon.$$

Since this holds for all $0 < h < \delta$, and the argument for $h < 0$ is symmetric, we conclude

$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{F(c+h) - F(c)}{h} = f(c). \quad \blacksquare$$

<Callout type="warning" title="Continuity of $f$ Is Essential">
The hypothesis that $f$ is continuous at $c$ cannot be dropped. If $f$ has a jump discontinuity at $c$, the integral function $F$ is still continuous at $c$ (we proved this above), but $F$ need not be differentiable there. For example, if $f$ is the Heaviside step function, $F$ has a corner at the jump point.
</Callout>

## Alternative Proof via Squeeze by Local Extrema

Here is the classical "squeeze" version of the proof, which is equivalent but perhaps more intuitive.

**Proof (Squeeze version).** Let $h > 0$ (the case $h < 0$ is analogous). Since $f$ is continuous at $c$ and $[c, c+h]$ is compact, $f$ attains its minimum and maximum on $[c, c+h]$:

$$m_h = \min_{t \in [c, c+h]} f(t), \qquad M_h = \max_{t \in [c, c+h]} f(t).$$

By the integral bound:

$$m_h \cdot h \leq \int_c^{c+h} f(t) \, dt \leq M_h \cdot h.$$

Dividing by $h > 0$:

$$m_h \leq \frac{F(c+h) - F(c)}{h} \leq M_h.$$

As $h \to 0^+$, the points where $f$ attains $m_h$ and $M_h$ are squeezed toward $c$. Since $f$ is continuous at $c$, both $m_h \to f(c)$ and $M_h \to f(c)$. By the Squeeze Theorem:

$$\lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{F(c+h) - F(c)}{h} = f(c). \quad \blacksquare$$

## Applications and Consequences

<Callout type="theorem" title="Existence of Antiderivatives">
If $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous, then $f$ has an antiderivative, namely $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt$.
</Callout>

This is a pure existence result: it guarantees that every continuous function has an antiderivative. It does not tell us how to find a closed-form expression for it. Many integrable functions (like $e^{-x^2}$) have antiderivatives that cannot be expressed in terms of elementary functions.

<Callout type="info" title="What FTC I Does NOT Say">
FTC Part I does NOT say that $\int_a^b f = G(b) - G(a)$ for an antiderivative $G$. That is the content of **FTC Part II** (the Evaluation Theorem), which we will prove in the next lesson. Part I is about differentiating integrals; Part II is about evaluating them.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem title="Prove FTC Part I">
Let $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be integrable and continuous at $c \in (a, b)$. Define $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt$. Prove that $F'(c) = f(c)$. Your proof should clearly indicate where continuity of $f$ at $c$ is used.

<Solution>
**Proof.** We show $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{F(c+h) - F(c)}{h} = f(c)$.

By additivity of the integral, $F(c+h) - F(c) = \int_c^{c+h} f(t) \, dt$. We write

$$\frac{F(c+h) - F(c)}{h} - f(c) = \frac{1}{h}\int_c^{c+h} f(t) \, dt - f(c) = \frac{1}{h}\int_c^{c+h}(f(t) - f(c)) \, dt$$

using the fact that $\frac{1}{h}\int_c^{c+h} f(c) \, dt = f(c)$.

**Here we use continuity of $f$ at $c$:** Given $\epsilon > 0$, choose $\delta > 0$ so that $|t - c| < \delta \implies |f(t) - f(c)| < \epsilon$. For $0 < |h| < \delta$, every $t$ between $c$ and $c + h$ satisfies $|t - c| \leq |h| < \delta$, so $|f(t) - f(c)| < \epsilon$. Thus

$$\left|\frac{F(c+h) - F(c)}{h} - f(c)\right| \leq \frac{1}{|h|}\int_{\min(c, c+h)}^{\max(c, c+h)} |f(t) - f(c)| \, dt < \frac{1}{|h|} \cdot \epsilon \cdot |h| = \epsilon.$$

Since $\epsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, $F'(c) = f(c)$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

<Problem>
**Exercise 5.3 (Counterexample).** Show that FTC Part I fails without the continuity hypothesis. Define $f \colon [0, 2] \to \mathbb{R}$ by

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } 0 \leq x \leq 1, \\ 1 & \text{if } 1 < x \leq 2. \end{cases}$$

This is the Heaviside step function (shifted to $x = 1$). Show that $f$ is integrable, compute $F(x) = \int_0^x f(t)\,dt$ explicitly, and prove that $F$ is **not differentiable** at $x = 1$. Conclude that continuity of $f$ at $c$ is essential for $F'(c) = f(c)$.

*Hint:* Show the left and right derivatives of $F$ at $x = 1$ differ.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* **Integrability.** The function $f$ is monotone (nondecreasing) on $[0, 2]$, so it is Riemann integrable by the monotone integrability theorem.

**Computing $F$.** For $0 \leq x \leq 1$: $F(x) = \int_0^x 0\,dt = 0$.

For $1 < x \leq 2$: $F(x) = \int_0^1 0\,dt + \int_1^x 1\,dt = 0 + (x - 1) = x - 1$.

So $F(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 \leq x \leq 1, \\ x - 1 & 1 < x \leq 2. \end{cases}$

Note $F$ is continuous at $x = 1$ (both sides give $0$), consistent with our theorem that the integral function is always continuous.

**Non-differentiability at $x = 1$.** The left derivative:

$$\lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{F(1+h) - F(1)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{0 - 0}{h} = 0.$$

The right derivative:

$$\lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{F(1+h) - F(1)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{(1+h-1) - 0}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{h}{h} = 1.$$

Since the left and right derivatives differ ($0 \neq 1$), $F$ is not differentiable at $x = 1$. The graph of $F$ has a "corner" at this point.

**Conclusion.** At $x = 1$, $f$ is discontinuous (jump from $0$ to $1$), and $F'(1)$ does not exist. FTC I guarantees $F'(c) = f(c)$ only at points where $f$ is continuous. The jump discontinuity of $f$ produces a corner in $F$, breaking differentiability. $\square$
</Solution>

## Numerical Verification: $F'(x) = f(x)$

<PythonPlayground

    return np.cos(t**2)

def F_numerical(x, n=10000):
    """Compute integral from 0 to x using the trapezoidal rule."""
    if abs(x) < 1e-15:
        return 0.0
    t = np.linspace(0, x, n + 1)
    y = f(t)
    return np.trapz(y, t)

    return (F_numerical(x + h) - F_numerical(x - h)) / (2 * h)

<Callout type="tip" title="Key Takeaway">
FTC Part I tells us that integration is, in a precise sense, the inverse of differentiation: if we integrate $f$ and then differentiate, we recover $f$ (at points of continuity). The converse direction -- evaluating an integral by finding an antiderivative -- is the subject of FTC Part II.
</Callout>
