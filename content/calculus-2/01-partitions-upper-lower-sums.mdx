# Partitions, Upper Sums, and Lower Sums

The Riemann integral gives a rigorous foundation for the notion of "area under a curve." Rather than
beginning with the familiar limit-of-Riemann-sums approach, we develop the Darboux formulation, which
is equivalent but cleaner for theoretical work. The key idea: approximate the area from above and below
using step functions, then ask when these approximations agree.

## Partitions of an Interval

<Callout type="definition" title="Partition">
A **partition** of a closed interval $[a, b]$ is a finite set of points

$$P = \{x_0, x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}$$

such that $a = x_0 < x_1 < x_2 < \cdots < x_n = b$. The intervals $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$ are the **subintervals** of $P$, and $\Delta x_i = x_i - x_{i-1}$ is the **width** of the $i$-th subinterval. The **mesh** (or **norm**) of $P$ is $\|P\| = \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} \Delta x_i$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="definition" title="Refinement">
A partition $P'$ is a **refinement** of $P$ if $P \subseteq P'$ (that is, $P'$ contains all the points of $P$ and possibly additional ones). Given two partitions $P$ and $Q$, their **common refinement** is $P \cup Q$.
</Callout>

## Upper and Lower Darboux Sums

<Callout type="definition" title="Upper and Lower Sums">
Let $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be bounded, and let $P = \{x_0, \ldots, x_n\}$ be a partition of $[a, b]$. Define

$$M_i = \sup\{f(x) : x \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]\}, \qquad m_i = \inf\{f(x) : x \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]\}.$$

The **upper Darboux sum** and **lower Darboux sum** of $f$ with respect to $P$ are

$$U(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} M_i \, \Delta x_i, \qquad L(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i \, \Delta x_i.$$
</Callout>

Since $m_i \leq M_i$ for each $i$, we always have $L(f, P) \leq U(f, P)$.

<Callout type="info" title="Geometric Intuition">
Think of $U(f, P)$ as the total area of rectangles that **enclose** the graph from above on each subinterval, and $L(f, P)$ as rectangles that fit **inside** the graph from below. The true area (integral) must lie between these two values.
</Callout>

## The Refinement Inequality

The fundamental property of Darboux sums is that refining a partition brings upper and lower sums closer together.

<Callout type="theorem" title="Refinement Inequality">
Let $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be bounded. If $P'$ is a refinement of $P$, then

$$L(f, P) \leq L(f, P') \leq U(f, P') \leq U(f, P).$$
</Callout>

**Proof.** It suffices to prove the result when $P'$ is obtained from $P$ by adding a single point $t$, since any refinement is built by adding points one at a time.

Suppose $t \in (x_{k-1}, x_k)$ for some $k$. The partition $P'$ splits the subinterval $[x_{k-1}, x_k]$ into $[x_{k-1}, t]$ and $[t, x_k]$. For the upper sums, let

$$M_k = \sup_{[x_{k-1}, x_k]} f, \quad M_k' = \sup_{[x_{k-1}, t]} f, \quad M_k'' = \sup_{[t, x_k]} f.$$

Since $[x_{k-1}, t] \subseteq [x_{k-1}, x_k]$ and $[t, x_k] \subseteq [x_{k-1}, x_k]$, we have $M_k' \leq M_k$ and $M_k'' \leq M_k$. Therefore

$$M_k'(t - x_{k-1}) + M_k''(x_k - t) \leq M_k(t - x_{k-1}) + M_k(x_k - t) = M_k \, \Delta x_k.$$

All other subintervals are unchanged, so $U(f, P') \leq U(f, P)$. An analogous argument (using infima and reversing the inequality) shows $L(f, P) \leq L(f, P')$. The middle inequality $L(f, P') \leq U(f, P')$ holds because $m_i \leq M_i$ on every subinterval. $\blacksquare$

## Lower Sums Never Exceed Upper Sums

<Callout type="theorem" title="Any Lower Sum Bounded by Any Upper Sum">
Let $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be bounded. For **any** two partitions $P$ and $Q$ of $[a, b]$,

$$L(f, P) \leq U(f, Q).$$
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $R = P \cup Q$ be the common refinement. Then $R$ refines both $P$ and $Q$, so by the refinement inequality:

$$L(f, P) \leq L(f, R) \leq U(f, R) \leq U(f, Q). \quad \blacksquare$$

<Callout type="tip" title="Why This Matters">
This result is what makes the Darboux integral well-defined. It says the set of all lower sums is bounded above (by any upper sum), and the set of all upper sums is bounded below (by any lower sum). So their supremum and infimum exist.
</Callout>

## Upper and Lower Integrals

<Callout type="definition" title="Upper and Lower Integrals">
Let $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be bounded. The **lower integral** and **upper integral** of $f$ on $[a, b]$ are

$$\underline{\int_a^b} f = \sup_P L(f, P), \qquad \overline{\int_a^b} f = \inf_P U(f, P),$$

where the supremum and infimum are taken over all partitions $P$ of $[a, b]$.
</Callout>

By the theorem above, every lower sum is at most every upper sum, so

$$\underline{\int_a^b} f \leq \overline{\int_a^b} f.$$

## Riemann Integrability

<Callout type="definition" title="Riemann Integrable (Darboux Formulation)">
A bounded function $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is **Riemann integrable** (or **Darboux integrable**) on $[a, b]$ if

$$\underline{\int_a^b} f = \overline{\int_a^b} f.$$

When this holds, the common value is denoted $\displaystyle \int_a^b f$ and is called the **Riemann integral** of $f$ on $[a, b]$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning" title="Boundedness Is Necessary">
The definition requires $f$ to be bounded on $[a, b]$. If $f$ is unbounded, the suprema and infima $M_i, m_i$ may be $\pm \infty$, and the Darboux sums are not well-defined real numbers. Unbounded "integrals" require the theory of improper integrals.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem title="Prove the Refinement Inequality for Lower Sums">
Let $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be bounded and let $P' = P \cup \{t\}$ where $t \in (x_{k-1}, x_k)$. Prove that $L(f, P) \leq L(f, P')$ by showing that the contribution from the split subintervals is at least $m_k \, \Delta x_k$.

<Solution>
**Proof.** Let $m_k = \inf_{[x_{k-1}, x_k]} f$, $m_k' = \inf_{[x_{k-1}, t]} f$, and $m_k'' = \inf_{[t, x_k]} f$.

Since $[x_{k-1}, t] \subseteq [x_{k-1}, x_k]$, the infimum over the smaller set is at least as large: $m_k' \geq m_k$. Similarly $m_k'' \geq m_k$. Therefore

$$m_k'(t - x_{k-1}) + m_k''(x_k - t) \geq m_k(t - x_{k-1}) + m_k(x_k - t) = m_k \, \Delta x_k.$$

Since all other terms in $L(f, P')$ and $L(f, P)$ are identical, we conclude $L(f, P') \geq L(f, P)$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

## Computing Darboux Sums Numerically

<PythonPlayground
  title="Upper and Lower Sums for f(x) = x^2"
  defaultCode={`import numpy as np

def f(x):
    return x**2

a, b = 0, 2

# Try different numbers of subintervals
for n in [2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256]:
    # Uniform partition
    x = np.linspace(a, b, n + 1)

    upper_sum = 0.0
    lower_sum = 0.0
    for i in range(n):
        # Sample many points in [x[i], x[i+1]] to approximate sup and inf
        samples = np.linspace(x[i], x[i+1], 1000)
        values = f(samples)
        M_i = np.max(values)
        m_i = np.min(values)
        dx = x[i+1] - x[i]
        upper_sum += M_i * dx
        lower_sum += m_i * dx

    print(f"n={n:>3}: L(f,P) = {lower_sum:.6f}, U(f,P) = {upper_sum:.6f}, "
          f"gap = {upper_sum - lower_sum:.6f}")

print(f"\\nExact integral of x^2 from 0 to 2: {2**3/3:.6f}")
print("Notice: both sums converge to 8/3 as n grows!")`}

<Problem>
**Exercise 1.4 (Counterexample).** Consider the Dirichlet function $D \colon [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $D(x) = 1$ if $x \in \mathbb{Q}$ and $D(x) = 0$ if $x \notin \mathbb{Q}$. This function is bounded. Show that $D$ is **not** Riemann integrable by computing $U(D, P)$ and $L(D, P)$ for an arbitrary partition $P$ of $[0, 1]$.

*Hint:* On every subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, both rationals and irrationals are dense.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* Let $P = \{x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ be any partition of $[0, 1]$. On each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, the rationals are dense, so there exists $q \in \mathbb{Q} \cap [x_{i-1}, x_i]$ with $D(q) = 1$. Hence $M_i = \sup_{[x_{i-1}, x_i]} D = 1$. Similarly, the irrationals are dense, so there exists $r \notin \mathbb{Q}$ with $D(r) = 0$. Hence $m_i = \inf_{[x_{i-1}, x_i]} D = 0$.

Therefore:

$$U(D, P) = \sum_{i=1}^n 1 \cdot \Delta x_i = 1, \qquad L(D, P) = \sum_{i=1}^n 0 \cdot \Delta x_i = 0.$$

For every partition $P$, $U(D, P) - L(D, P) = 1$. The $\varepsilon$-partition criterion fails for any $\varepsilon < 1$, so $D$ is not Riemann integrable.

This shows that boundedness alone does not imply integrability. The obstruction is that the oscillation $\omega_i = 1$ on every subinterval, regardless of the partition. $\square$
</Solution>
