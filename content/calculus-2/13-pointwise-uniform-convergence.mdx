# Pointwise vs Uniform Convergence

We now confront one of the most important ideas in analysis: the distinction between pointwise and uniform convergence of function sequences. The difference comes down to the order of quantifiers, and getting it wrong leads to false theorems.

## Pointwise Convergence

<Callout type="definition">
**Definition 13.1 (Pointwise Convergence).** Let $f_n : A \to \mathbb{R}$ be a sequence of functions. We say $(f_n)$ **converges pointwise** to $f : A \to \mathbb{R}$ if for every $x \in A$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} f_n(x) = f(x).$$

Written with quantifiers:

$$\forall x \in A,\; \forall \varepsilon > 0,\; \exists N \in \mathbb{N},\; \forall n \geq N,\; |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon.$$
</Callout>

The crucial feature: $N$ may depend on both $\varepsilon$ **and** $x$.

## Uniform Convergence

<Callout type="definition">
**Definition 13.2 (Uniform Convergence).** The sequence $(f_n)$ **converges uniformly** to $f$ on $A$ if:

$$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\; \exists N \in \mathbb{N},\; \forall n \geq N,\; \forall x \in A,\; |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon.$$

Equivalently, $\sup_{x \in A} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**The quantifier swap.** Compare the two definitions:

- **Pointwise:** $\forall x\; \forall \varepsilon\; \exists N\; \forall n \geq N$: $|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$
- **Uniform:** $\forall \varepsilon\; \exists N\; \forall n \geq N\; \forall x$: $|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$

In uniform convergence, $N$ depends only on $\varepsilon$, not on $x$. A single $N$ works for ALL points simultaneously. This is strictly stronger.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 13.3.** Uniform convergence implies pointwise convergence. The converse is false.
</Callout>

*Proof.* If $(f_n) \to f$ uniformly, then the $N$ from the uniform definition works for each individual $x$, so we also have pointwise convergence. The converse fails by the following canonical example. $\square$

## Canonical Example: $f_n(x) = x^n$ on $[0, 1]$

<Callout type="definition">
**Example 13.4.** Define $f_n : [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R}$ by $f_n(x) = x^n$.
</Callout>

**Pointwise limit.** For each fixed $x \in [0, 1)$, $x^n \to 0$. At $x = 1$, $1^n = 1 \to 1$. So the pointwise limit is:

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } 0 \leq x < 1, \\ 1 & \text{if } x = 1. \end{cases}$$

**Not uniformly convergent.** Each $f_n$ is continuous on $[0, 1]$, but the pointwise limit $f$ is discontinuous at $x = 1$. As we will prove below, uniform convergence preserves continuity. Since the limit is discontinuous, the convergence cannot be uniform.

We can verify directly: $\sup_{x \in [0,1]} |f_n(x) - f(x)| = \sup_{x \in [0,1)} x^n = 1$ (the supremum approaches 1 from below but is never attained). In fact, for any $x_n = (1 - 1/n)$, we have $f_n(x_n) = (1 - 1/n)^n \to 1/e \neq 0$. So the sup does not go to zero.

## Second Example: $f_n(x) = x / (1 + nx^2)$

<Callout type="info">
**Example 13.5.** Let $f_n(x) = \frac{x}{1 + nx^2}$ on $\mathbb{R}$.

For $x = 0$: $f_n(0) = 0$ for all $n$. For $x \neq 0$: $f_n(x) = \frac{x}{1 + nx^2} \to 0$ since the denominator grows without bound.

So $f_n \to 0$ pointwise. Is this uniform?

The maximum of $|f_n|$ occurs at $x = 1/\sqrt{n}$ (by calculus), giving $f_n(1/\sqrt{n}) = \frac{1/\sqrt{n}}{1 + 1} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{n}}$. So $\sup |f_n(x)| = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{n}} \to 0$.

The convergence IS uniform on $\mathbb{R}$, despite the "moving bump." The bump height shrinks to zero.
</Callout>

## The Cauchy Criterion for Uniform Convergence

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 13.6 (Cauchy Criterion).** A sequence $(f_n)$ converges uniformly on $A$ if and only if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N$ such that

$$\sup_{x \in A} |f_n(x) - f_m(x)| < \varepsilon \quad \text{for all } n, m \geq N.$$
</Callout>

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) If $f_n \to f$ uniformly, then for $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N$ with $\sup |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon/2$ for $n \geq N$. For $n, m \geq N$:

$$|f_n(x) - f_m(x)| \leq |f_n(x) - f(x)| + |f(x) - f_m(x)| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon$$

uniformly in $x$.

($\Leftarrow$) For each $x$, $(f_n(x))$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb{R}$, hence converges to some $f(x)$. Fix $n \geq N$ and let $m \to \infty$ in $|f_n(x) - f_m(x)| < \varepsilon$ to get $|f_n(x) - f(x)| \leq \varepsilon$ for all $x$. Since $\varepsilon$ is arbitrary, $f_n \to f$ uniformly. $\square$

## Uniform Convergence Preserves Continuity

This is the theorem that makes uniform convergence indispensable.

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 13.7 (Uniform Limit of Continuous Functions is Continuous).** Let $f_n : A \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous for each $n$, and suppose $f_n \to f$ uniformly on $A$. Then $f$ is continuous on $A$.
</Callout>

*Proof.* Let $c \in A$ and $\varepsilon > 0$. We use the $\varepsilon/3$ trick.

**Step 1.** Since $f_n \to f$ uniformly, there exists $N$ such that $|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon/3$ for all $x \in A$ and all $n \geq N$. Fix such an $n$.

**Step 2.** Since $f_n$ is continuous at $c$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|x - c| < \delta$ implies $|f_n(x) - f_n(c)| < \varepsilon/3$.

**Step 3.** For $|x - c| < \delta$:

$$|f(x) - f(c)| \leq \underbrace{|f(x) - f_n(x)|}_{< \varepsilon/3} + \underbrace{|f_n(x) - f_n(c)|}_{< \varepsilon/3} + \underbrace{|f_n(c) - f(c)|}_{< \varepsilon/3} < \varepsilon.$$

Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, $f$ is continuous at $c$. Since $c$ was arbitrary, $f$ is continuous on $A$. $\square$

<Callout type="warning">
**Contrapositive in action.** This theorem gives us a powerful tool for showing convergence is NOT uniform: if each $f_n$ is continuous but the pointwise limit $f$ is discontinuous, then $f_n \not\to f$ uniformly. This is exactly how we proved $f_n(x) = x^n$ does not converge uniformly on $[0,1]$.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem>
**Exercise 13.1.** Prove Theorem 13.7 (uniform limit of continuous functions is continuous) from scratch, clearly identifying where each hypothesis (uniform convergence, continuity of $f_n$) is used.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* Let $c \in A$ and let $\varepsilon > 0$ be given. We must find $\delta > 0$ such that $|x - c| < \delta$ and $x \in A$ implies $|f(x) - f(c)| < \varepsilon$.

**Using uniform convergence:** Since $f_n \to f$ uniformly on $A$, applied with $\varepsilon/3 > 0$, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \geq N$ and all $x \in A$:

$$|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \frac{\varepsilon}{3}. \tag{1}$$

Fix $n = N$ (any $n \geq N$ would work).

**Using continuity of $f_N$:** Since $f_N$ is continuous at $c$, applied with $\varepsilon/3 > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $x \in A$ with $|x - c| < \delta$:

$$|f_N(x) - f_N(c)| < \frac{\varepsilon}{3}. \tag{2}$$

**Combining via triangle inequality:** For $x \in A$ with $|x - c| < \delta$:

$$|f(x) - f(c)| \leq |f(x) - f_N(x)| + |f_N(x) - f_N(c)| + |f_N(c) - f(c)|.$$

By (1) with $x = x$: the first term is $< \varepsilon/3$.
By (2): the second term is $< \varepsilon/3$.
By (1) with $x = c$: the third term is $< \varepsilon/3$.

Therefore $|f(x) - f(c)| < \varepsilon$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 13.2.** Show that $f_n(x) = x^n$ converges pointwise but not uniformly on $[0, 1]$. Use the contrapositive of Theorem 13.7.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.*

**Pointwise convergence.** For $x \in [0, 1)$: $|x| < 1$, so $x^n \to 0$. For $x = 1$: $1^n = 1 \to 1$. Thus $f_n \to f$ pointwise, where $f(x) = 0$ for $x \in [0,1)$ and $f(1) = 1$.

**Not uniform (via Theorem 13.7).** Each $f_n(x) = x^n$ is continuous on $[0,1]$ (it is a polynomial). However, the pointwise limit $f$ is discontinuous at $x = 1$:

$$\lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x) = 0 \neq 1 = f(1).$$

By the contrapositive of Theorem 13.7 (if the uniform limit of continuous functions is continuous, then if the limit is discontinuous, the convergence is not uniform), the convergence $f_n \to f$ is not uniform on $[0,1]$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 13.3 (Counterexample).** Construct a sequence of continuous functions $f_n \colon [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R}$ that converges pointwise to a **discontinuous** limit, thereby showing that pointwise convergence does not preserve continuity.

Use the sequence $f_n(x) = x^n$ and:
1. Prove the pointwise limit $f(x) = 0$ for $x \in [0,1)$ and $f(1) = 1$ is discontinuous at $x = 1$.
2. Prove the convergence is not uniform by computing $\sup_{x \in [0,1]} |f_n(x) - f(x)|$ and showing it does not converge to $0$.
3. Explain why this does not contradict Theorem 13.7 (which requires uniform convergence).

*Hint:* Evaluate $f_n$ at $x_n = (1 - 1/n)$ to bound the sup-norm from below.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.*

**(1) Pointwise limit is discontinuous.** For $x \in [0, 1)$: $f_n(x) = x^n \to 0$ since $|x| < 1$. For $x = 1$: $f_n(1) = 1^n = 1 \to 1$. So

$$f(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} x^n = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 \leq x < 1, \\ 1 & x = 1. \end{cases}$$

This is discontinuous at $x = 1$: $\lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x) = 0 \neq 1 = f(1)$.

**(2) Convergence is not uniform.** The sup-norm is:

$$\|f_n - f\|_{\infty} = \sup_{x \in [0,1]} |f_n(x) - f(x)| = \sup_{x \in [0,1)} x^n$$

(since at $x = 1$, $|f_n(1) - f(1)| = |1 - 1| = 0$).

For any $n$, evaluate at $x_n = (1 - 1/n) \in [0, 1)$:

$$|f_n(x_n) - f(x_n)| = \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \to \frac{1}{e} \approx 0.3679.$$

Since $\|f_n - f\|_{\infty} \geq (1 - 1/n)^n \to 1/e > 0$, the sup-norm does not converge to $0$, so the convergence is not uniform.

**(3) No contradiction with Theorem 13.7.** Theorem 13.7 states: if $f_n \to f$ *uniformly* and each $f_n$ is continuous, then $f$ is continuous. The hypothesis is uniform convergence. Since we just proved the convergence is only pointwise (not uniform), the theorem's hypothesis is not met, and no conclusion about continuity of $f$ can be drawn. The discontinuous limit is consistent with the theorem -- it is a consequence of the convergence being merely pointwise. $\square$
</Solution>
