# FTC Part II, Substitution, and Integration by Parts

The First Fundamental Theorem (FTC I) told us that every continuous function has an antiderivative: the
accumulation function $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$ satisfies $F'(x) = f(x)$. We now prove the converse
direction -- the **Evaluation Theorem** (FTC II) -- and develop the two most important techniques for
computing integrals: substitution and integration by parts.

## The Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 6.1 (FTC Part II — Evaluation Theorem).** Let $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous, and suppose $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$ (i.e., $F$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$, and $F'(x) = f(x)$ for all $x \in (a,b)$). Then

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a).$$
</Callout>

*Proof.* By FTC I, the function $G(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$ is an antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$, i.e., $G'(x) = f(x)$ for all $x \in (a,b)$.

Since $F$ and $G$ are both antiderivatives of $f$ on $(a,b)$, the function $H = F - G$ satisfies $H'(x) = 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$. By the Mean Value Theorem, $H$ is constant on $[a,b]$: for any $x, y \in [a,b]$ with $x < y$, there exists $c \in (x,y)$ such that $H(y) - H(x) = H'(c)(y - x) = 0$.

Therefore $F(x) - G(x) = C$ for some constant $C$, for all $x \in [a,b]$.

Now $G(a) = \int_a^a f(t)\,dt = 0$, so $C = F(a) - G(a) = F(a)$. Hence $G(x) = F(x) - F(a)$ for all $x \in [a,b]$. Evaluating at $x = b$:

$$\int_a^b f(t)\,dt = G(b) = F(b) - F(a). \quad \blacksquare$$

<Callout type="info">
**Remark.** The proof crucially uses two facts: (1) FTC I guarantees an antiderivative exists, and (2) the Mean Value Theorem implies any two antiderivatives differ by a constant. Without the MVT, we could not bridge from $H'(x) = 0$ to $H$ being constant.
</Callout>

### A Classical Application

As an immediate application, we can now evaluate definite integrals without computing Riemann sums:

$$\int_0^1 x^2\,dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{3} - 0 = \frac{1}{3}.$$

## The Substitution Theorem

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 6.2 (Change of Variables / Substitution).** Let $g : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuously differentiable (i.e., $g' $ exists and is continuous on $[a,b]$), and let $f$ be continuous on an interval containing the range of $g$. Then

$$\int_a^b f(g(t)) \cdot g'(t)\,dt = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u)\,du.$$
</Callout>

*Proof.* Since $f$ is continuous, by FTC I it has an antiderivative $F$ with $F' = f$. Consider the composite function $H(t) = F(g(t))$. By the chain rule:

$$H'(t) = F'(g(t)) \cdot g'(t) = f(g(t)) \cdot g'(t).$$

The right-hand side is continuous on $[a,b]$ (as a product of continuous functions), so by FTC II:

$$\int_a^b f(g(t)) \cdot g'(t)\,dt = H(b) - H(a) = F(g(b)) - F(g(a)).$$

Applying FTC II again to the right-hand side:

$$\int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u)\,du = F(g(b)) - F(g(a)).$$

The two expressions are equal. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip">
**When to substitute.** Look for an integrand of the form $f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$. Setting $u = g(x)$, $du = g'(x)\,dx$ converts the integral into $\int f(u)\,du$, which may be simpler. The theorem guarantees this is valid when $g$ is $C^1$ and $f$ is continuous.
</Callout>

### Example: Substitution in Action

Compute $\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin(x) \cos(x)\,dx$.

Set $u = \sin(x)$, so $du = \cos(x)\,dx$. When $x = 0$, $u = 0$; when $x = \pi/2$, $u = 1$. Then:

$$\int_0^{\pi/2} \sin(x)\cos(x)\,dx = \int_0^1 u\,du = \left[\frac{u^2}{2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2}.$$

## Integration by Parts

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 6.3 (Integration by Parts).** Let $f, g : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuously differentiable. Then

$$\int_a^b f(x) g'(x)\,dx = \bigl[f(x)g(x)\bigr]_a^b - \int_a^b f'(x) g(x)\,dx.$$
</Callout>

*Proof.* By the product rule for derivatives:

$$(f \cdot g)'(x) = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x).$$

Since $f, g$ are $C^1$, both $f'g$ and $fg'$ are continuous. Integrating both sides from $a$ to $b$ and applying FTC II:

$$f(b)g(b) - f(a)g(a) = \int_a^b f'(x)g(x)\,dx + \int_a^b f(x)g'(x)\,dx.$$

Rearranging gives the result. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="definition">
**Notation.** In the traditional shorthand, we write $u = f(x)$, $dv = g'(x)\,dx$, $du = f'(x)\,dx$, $v = g(x)$, and the formula becomes:

$$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du.$$
</Callout>

### Example: Integration by Parts

Compute $\int_0^1 x e^x\,dx$.

Let $u = x$, $dv = e^x\,dx$. Then $du = dx$, $v = e^x$.

$$\int_0^1 x e^x\,dx = \bigl[xe^x\bigr]_0^1 - \int_0^1 e^x\,dx = e - \bigl[e^x\bigr]_0^1 = e - (e - 1) = 1.$$

## Exercises

<Problem>
**Exercise 6.1.** Prove FTC II directly using a telescoping argument. That is, given a partition $P = \{x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ of $[a,b]$, write $F(b) - F(a)$ as a telescoping sum, apply the Mean Value Theorem on each subinterval, and show the sum equals a Riemann sum for $f$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* Let $P = \{a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = b\}$ be any partition. Then:

$$F(b) - F(a) = \sum_{k=1}^n \bigl[F(x_k) - F(x_{k-1})\bigr].$$

By the Mean Value Theorem, for each $k$ there exists $c_k \in (x_{k-1}, x_k)$ such that

$$F(x_k) - F(x_{k-1}) = F'(c_k)(x_k - x_{k-1}) = f(c_k)\Delta x_k.$$

Therefore $F(b) - F(a) = \sum_{k=1}^n f(c_k)\Delta x_k$. This is a Riemann sum for $f$ on $[a,b]$ with sample points $c_k$.

Since $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ (hence Riemann integrable), as $\|P\| \to 0$ all Riemann sums converge to $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$. But the left-hand side $F(b) - F(a)$ is independent of the partition, so:

$$F(b) - F(a) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx. \quad \blacksquare$$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 6.2.** Using integration by parts twice, derive the reduction formula:

$$\int e^x \sin(x)\,dx = \frac{e^x(\sin x - \cos x)}{2} + C.$$
</Problem>

<Solution>
Apply IBP with $u = \sin x$, $dv = e^x\,dx$, so $du = \cos x\,dx$, $v = e^x$:

$$I = \int e^x \sin x\,dx = e^x \sin x - \int e^x \cos x\,dx.$$

Apply IBP again to $\int e^x \cos x\,dx$ with $u = \cos x$, $dv = e^x\,dx$:

$$\int e^x \cos x\,dx = e^x \cos x + \int e^x \sin x\,dx = e^x \cos x + I.$$

Substituting back:

$$I = e^x \sin x - e^x \cos x - I.$$

Therefore $2I = e^x(\sin x - \cos x)$, giving $I = \frac{e^x(\sin x - \cos x)}{2} + C$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 6.3 (Counterexample).** Show that the substitution formula can produce an incorrect result if the hypothesis that $g'$ is continuous is violated. Consider the function $g \colon [-1, 1] \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $g(t) = t^2 \sin(1/t)$ for $t \neq 0$ and $g(0) = 0$. Show that $g$ is differentiable everywhere on $[-1, 1]$ but $g'$ is **not** continuous at $t = 0$. Then explain why the substitution theorem (Theorem 6.2) does not apply with this $g$.

*Hint:* Compute $g'(0)$ from the definition and $g'(t)$ for $t \neq 0$ using the product/chain rule. Show $\lim_{t \to 0} g'(t)$ does not exist.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* **$g$ is differentiable at $t = 0$:**

$$g'(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{g(h) - g(0)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h^2 \sin(1/h)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} h \sin(1/h) = 0$$

since $|h \sin(1/h)| \leq |h| \to 0$.

**$g'$ for $t \neq 0$:** By the product and chain rules:

$$g'(t) = 2t \sin(1/t) + t^2 \cdot \cos(1/t) \cdot (-1/t^2) = 2t\sin(1/t) - \cos(1/t).$$

**$g'$ is not continuous at $t = 0$:** We have $g'(0) = 0$, but

$$\lim_{t \to 0} g'(t) = \lim_{t \to 0} \bigl[2t\sin(1/t) - \cos(1/t)\bigr].$$

The term $2t\sin(1/t) \to 0$, but $\cos(1/t)$ oscillates between $-1$ and $1$ as $t \to 0$. So $\lim_{t \to 0} g'(t)$ does not exist, and in particular $g'$ is not continuous at $0$.

**Why the substitution theorem fails:** Theorem 6.2 requires $g$ to be **continuously differentiable** ($C^1$), meaning $g'$ exists and is continuous. The proof uses the fact that $f(g(t)) \cdot g'(t)$ is continuous (hence Riemann integrable) to apply FTC II. With $g'$ discontinuous, the composite $f(g(t)) \cdot g'(t)$ may fail to be Riemann integrable, and the chain rule argument that $H'(t) = f(g(t))g'(t)$ may not yield an integrable function to which FTC II applies.

This does not mean the substitution always gives a wrong answer for non-$C^1$ functions -- it may still work in special cases. But the theorem's guarantee is lost. $\square$
</Solution>
