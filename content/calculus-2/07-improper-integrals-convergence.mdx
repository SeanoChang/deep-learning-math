# Improper Integrals and Convergence

Up to now, the Riemann integral $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ has been defined only for bounded functions on bounded, closed intervals. We now extend integration to two important cases where this framework breaks down: (1) integration over unbounded intervals, and (2) integration of functions with singularities. These **improper integrals** require the machinery of limits, and the question of convergence versus divergence becomes central.

## Type I: Unbounded Intervals

<Callout type="definition">
**Definition 7.1 (Improper integral, Type I).** Let $f$ be Riemann integrable on $[a, R]$ for every $R > a$. We define

$$\int_a^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = \lim_{R \to \infty} \int_a^R f(x)\,dx,$$

provided this limit exists (as a finite real number). If the limit exists, we say the improper integral **converges**; otherwise it **diverges**.

Analogously, $\int_{-\infty}^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{R \to -\infty} \int_R^b f(x)\,dx$, and $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = \int_{-\infty}^c f(x)\,dx + \int_c^{\infty} f(x)\,dx$ for any $c$, provided both integrals converge.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Caution.** The definition of $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx$ requires *both* one-sided limits to exist independently. The symmetric limit $\lim_{R \to \infty}\int_{-R}^R f(x)\,dx$ (the **Cauchy principal value**) may exist even when the improper integral diverges. For example, $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\,dx$ diverges, but the principal value is $0$.
</Callout>

## The $p$-Integral on $[1, \infty)$

The family $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p}\,dx$ is the fundamental benchmark for convergence.

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 7.2 (p-integral criterion).** For $p \in \mathbb{R}$,

$$\int_1^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p}\,dx \quad \begin{cases} \text{converges} & \text{if } p > 1, \\[4pt] \text{diverges} & \text{if } p \leq 1. \end{cases}$$

*Proof.* **Case $p \neq 1$:** An antiderivative of $x^{-p}$ is $\frac{x^{1-p}}{1-p}$. So

$$\int_1^R \frac{dx}{x^p} = \frac{R^{1-p} - 1}{1-p}.$$

If $p > 1$, then $1 - p < 0$, so $R^{1-p} \to 0$ as $R \to \infty$, giving $\int_1^{\infty} x^{-p}\,dx = \frac{1}{p-1}$.

If $p < 1$, then $1 - p > 0$, so $R^{1-p} \to \infty$, and the integral diverges.

**Case $p = 1$:** $\int_1^R \frac{dx}{x} = \ln R \to \infty$ as $R \to \infty$. Diverges. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## Type II: Singularities

<Callout type="definition">
**Definition 7.3 (Improper integral, Type II).** Let $f$ be Riemann integrable on $[a + \epsilon, b]$ for every $\epsilon > 0$, and suppose $f$ is unbounded near $a$. We define

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0^+} \int_{a+\epsilon}^b f(x)\,dx,$$

provided the limit exists. Analogous definitions hold for singularities at $b$ or at interior points.
</Callout>

### Example: Singularity at $x = 0$

$$\int_0^1 \frac{dx}{\sqrt{x}} = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0^+} \int_\epsilon^1 x^{-1/2}\,dx = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0^+} \bigl[2\sqrt{x}\bigr]_\epsilon^1 = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0^+} (2 - 2\sqrt{\epsilon}) = 2.$$

This converges. In contrast, $\int_0^1 \frac{dx}{x} = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0^+} [-\ln \epsilon] = \infty$ diverges.

## Comparison Tests

When we cannot find an antiderivative, we establish convergence by comparison to known integrals.

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 7.4 (Comparison Test for Improper Integrals).** Let $f, g$ be continuous on $[a, \infty)$ with $0 \leq f(x) \leq g(x)$ for all $x \geq a$.

1. If $\int_a^{\infty} g(x)\,dx$ converges, then $\int_a^{\infty} f(x)\,dx$ converges.
2. If $\int_a^{\infty} f(x)\,dx$ diverges, then $\int_a^{\infty} g(x)\,dx$ diverges.

*Proof of (1).* Define $F(R) = \int_a^R f(x)\,dx$. Since $f \geq 0$, $F$ is nondecreasing. Since $f(x) \leq g(x)$:

$$F(R) = \int_a^R f(x)\,dx \leq \int_a^R g(x)\,dx \leq \int_a^{\infty} g(x)\,dx = M < \infty.$$

So $F$ is nondecreasing and bounded above by $M$. By the Monotone Convergence Theorem for sequences (applied to $F(n)$ and then extended to the continuous parameter), $\lim_{R \to \infty} F(R)$ exists and is finite. $\blacksquare$

Part (2) is the contrapositive of (1).
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 7.5 (Limit Comparison Test).** Let $f, g$ be continuous and positive on $[a, \infty)$. If

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = L, \qquad 0 < L < \infty,$$

then $\int_a^{\infty} f(x)\,dx$ and $\int_a^{\infty} g(x)\,dx$ either both converge or both diverge.

*Proof.* Since $f(x)/g(x) \to L$ with $L > 0$, choose $\epsilon = L/2$. There exists $N$ such that for $x \geq N$:

$$\frac{L}{2} < \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} < \frac{3L}{2},$$

so $\frac{L}{2}g(x) < f(x) < \frac{3L}{2}g(x)$ for $x \geq N$. The result follows from the Comparison Test applied in both directions. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## Absolute Convergence

<Callout type="definition">
**Definition 7.6.** The improper integral $\int_a^{\infty} f(x)\,dx$ **converges absolutely** if $\int_a^{\infty} |f(x)|\,dx$ converges.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 7.7.** Absolute convergence implies convergence: if $\int_a^{\infty} |f(x)|\,dx$ converges, then $\int_a^{\infty} f(x)\,dx$ converges.

*Proof.* Note $0 \leq f(x) + |f(x)| \leq 2|f(x)|$. By the Comparison Test, $\int_a^{\infty}(f(x) + |f(x)|)\,dx$ converges. Then

$$\int_a^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = \int_a^{\infty}(f(x) + |f(x)|)\,dx - \int_a^{\infty}|f(x)|\,dx$$

is the difference of two convergent integrals, hence convergent. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**Conditional convergence.** An integral can converge without converging absolutely. The classic example is $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx$, which converges (by Dirichlet's test) but does not converge absolutely.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem>
**Exercise 7.1.** Prove the Comparison Test (Theorem 7.4) in full detail, carefully justifying the passage from the monotone sequence $F(n)$ to the continuous limit $\lim_{R \to \infty} F(R)$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* Let $F(R) = \int_a^R f(x)\,dx$ for $R \geq a$. Since $f \geq 0$, if $R_1 < R_2$ then

$$F(R_2) = F(R_1) + \int_{R_1}^{R_2} f(x)\,dx \geq F(R_1),$$

so $F$ is nondecreasing. By hypothesis, $F(R) \leq \int_a^R g(x)\,dx \leq \int_a^{\infty} g(x)\,dx = M$ for all $R$.

The sequence $(F(n))_{n \geq \lceil a \rceil}$ is nondecreasing and bounded above by $M$, so by the Monotone Convergence Theorem for sequences, $\ell = \lim_{n \to \infty} F(n)$ exists with $\ell \leq M$.

For arbitrary $R > a$, let $n = \lfloor R \rfloor$. Then $n \leq R < n + 1$, so

$$F(n) \leq F(R) \leq F(n+1).$$

Since both $F(n)$ and $F(n+1)$ converge to $\ell$, by the Squeeze Theorem $\lim_{R \to \infty} F(R) = \ell$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 7.2.** Show that $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{1}{1 + x^3}\,dx$ converges using the Limit Comparison Test with $g(x) = 1/x^3$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
We compute:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/(1+x^3)}{1/x^3} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^3}{1 + x^3} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{1/x^3 + 1} = 1.$$

Since $L = 1 \in (0, \infty)$ and $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^3}\,dx$ converges ($p = 3 > 1$), the Limit Comparison Test implies $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{1}{1+x^3}\,dx$ converges. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 7.3 (Counterexample).** Show that $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx$ converges **conditionally**: it converges, but it does not converge absolutely (i.e., $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{|\sin x|}{x}\,dx = \infty$).

*Hint:* For convergence, use integration by parts or Dirichlet's test. For divergence of the absolute integral, bound $|\sin x|$ from below on intervals $[k\pi + \pi/4, k\pi + 3\pi/4]$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.*

**Convergence of $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx$:** We use integration by parts. Write:

$$\int_1^R \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx = \left[-\frac{\cos x}{x}\right]_1^R - \int_1^R \frac{\cos x}{x^2}\,dx = \frac{\cos 1}{1} - \frac{\cos R}{R} - \int_1^R \frac{\cos x}{x^2}\,dx.$$

As $R \to \infty$: $\cos R / R \to 0$, and $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{\cos x}{x^2}\,dx$ converges absolutely since $|\cos x / x^2| \leq 1/x^2$ and $\int_1^{\infty} 1/x^2\,dx < \infty$. So $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx$ converges.

**Divergence of $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{|\sin x|}{x}\,dx$:** For each integer $k \geq 1$ and $x \in [k\pi + \pi/4,\, k\pi + 3\pi/4]$, we have $|\sin x| \geq \sin(\pi/4) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$. Also, on this interval $x \leq (k+1)\pi$, so $1/x \geq 1/((k+1)\pi)$. The interval has length $\pi/2$. Therefore:

$$\int_{k\pi + \pi/4}^{k\pi + 3\pi/4} \frac{|\sin x|}{x}\,dx \geq \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{(k+1)\pi} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4(k+1)}.$$

Summing over $k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$:

$$\int_1^{\infty} \frac{|\sin x|}{x}\,dx \geq \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4(k+1)} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k} = \infty$$

since the harmonic series diverges.

**Conclusion:** $\int_1^{\infty} \frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx$ converges but not absolutely. This is the improper integral analogue of a conditionally convergent series. The convergence relies on cancellation between positive and negative parts of $\sin x$, which is destroyed by taking absolute values. $\square$
</Solution>

## Numerical Exploration

<PythonPlayground
  title={"Numerically Exploring Improper Integrals"}
  defaultCode={`import math

def trapezoidal(f, a, b, n=10000):
    h = (b - a) / n
    total = 0.5 * (f(a) + f(b))
    for i in range(1, n):
        total += f(a + i * h)
    return total * h

# --- p-integral: integrate 1/x^p from 1 to R for increasing R ---
print("=== p-integral convergence ===")
for p in [0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0]:
    print(f"\np = {p}:")
    f = lambda x, p=p: 1.0 / x**p
    for R in [10, 100, 1000, 10000]:
        val = trapezoidal(f, 1, R, n=50000)
        exact = "diverges" if p <= 1 else f"{1/(p-1):.6f}"
        print(f"  R = {R:>5}: integral = {val:.6f}  (exact limit: {exact})")

print()

# --- Exploring 1/(1+x^3) via limit comparison ---
print("=== Limit comparison: 1/(1+x^3) vs 1/x^3 ===")
g = lambda x: 1.0 / (1 + x**3)
for R in [10, 100, 1000]:
    val = trapezoidal(g, 1, R, n=50000)
    print(f"  R = {R:>5}: integral = {val:.6f}")

print()
print("The integral of 1/x^3 from 1 to infinity = 1/2 = 0.5")
print("By limit comparison (ratio -> 1), the integral of 1/(1+x^3)")
print("also converges, to a value close to what we see above.")
`}
/>

<Callout type="tip">
Observe how quickly the partial integrals stabilize for $p > 1$ versus how slowly they grow for $p \leq 1$. The case $p = 1$ (harmonic) is particularly deceptive: $\ln(10000) \approx 9.2$, which seems small, but the integral diverges to infinity -- just very slowly.
</Callout>
