# Continuous and Monotone Functions Are Integrable

We now prove two of the most important integrability theorems: every continuous function on a closed
bounded interval is integrable, and every monotone function on a closed bounded interval is integrable.
The proofs showcase a beautiful chain of ideas: compactness leads to uniform continuity, which leads to
integrability. For monotone functions, the argument is more elementary but equally elegant.

## Uniform Continuity (Review)

<Callout type="definition" title="Uniform Continuity">
A function $f \colon S \to \mathbb{R}$ is **uniformly continuous** on $S$ if for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $x, y \in S$,

$$|x - y| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| < \epsilon.$$

The key distinction from pointwise continuity: $\delta$ depends only on $\epsilon$, not on the location $x$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Heine-Cantor Theorem">
If $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous on the closed bounded interval $[a, b]$, then $f$ is uniformly continuous on $[a, b]$.
</Callout>

We take this theorem from Calculus I as established. The proof uses the compactness of $[a, b]$ (every open cover has a finite subcover, or equivalently, the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem). The logical chain is:

$$[a, b] \text{ compact} \implies \text{continuous } f \text{ is uniformly continuous on } [a, b].$$

## Continuous Functions Are Integrable

<Callout type="theorem" title="Integrability of Continuous Functions">
If $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous, then $f$ is Riemann integrable on $[a, b]$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Since $f$ is continuous on the compact set $[a, b]$, it is bounded (by the Extreme Value Theorem), so the Darboux sums are well-defined.

We use the $\epsilon$-partition criterion. Let $\epsilon > 0$. Since $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, by the Heine-Cantor theorem $f$ is uniformly continuous. Choose $\delta > 0$ such that

$$|x - y| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| < \frac{\epsilon}{b - a}.$$

Now take any partition $P = \{x_0, \ldots, x_n\}$ with mesh $\|P\| < \delta$. On each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ of width less than $\delta$, for any $x, y \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$ we have $|x - y| \leq \Delta x_i < \delta$, so $|f(x) - f(y)| < \epsilon/(b-a)$. Therefore

$$\omega_i = M_i - m_i = \sup_{[x_{i-1}, x_i]} f - \inf_{[x_{i-1}, x_i]} f \leq \frac{\epsilon}{b - a}.$$

Summing over all subintervals:

$$U(f, P) - L(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^n \omega_i \, \Delta x_i \leq \frac{\epsilon}{b - a} \sum_{i=1}^n \Delta x_i = \frac{\epsilon}{b - a} \cdot (b - a) = \epsilon.$$

By the $\epsilon$-criterion, $f$ is integrable. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="info" title="The Proof Strategy">
The proof follows a clean three-step pattern: (1) invoke compactness to upgrade continuity to uniform continuity, (2) use the uniform $\delta$ to control oscillation on each subinterval, (3) sum the oscillation bounds to get $U - L < \epsilon$. This pattern appears frequently in analysis.
</Callout>

## Monotone Functions Are Integrable

<Callout type="theorem" title="Integrability of Monotone Functions">
If $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is monotone (either increasing or decreasing), then $f$ is Riemann integrable on $[a, b]$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** We give the proof for $f$ increasing; the decreasing case is analogous (or apply the result to $-f$).

Since $f$ is increasing, $f(a) \leq f(x) \leq f(b)$ for all $x \in [a, b]$, so $f$ is bounded.

Let $\epsilon > 0$. Choose $n$ large enough that

$$\frac{(b - a)(f(b) - f(a))}{n} < \epsilon.$$

Let $P$ be the uniform partition with $n$ equal subintervals: $x_i = a + i(b-a)/n$ for $i = 0, 1, \ldots, n$, so $\Delta x_i = (b-a)/n$ for all $i$.

Since $f$ is increasing on $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, we have $m_i = f(x_{i-1})$ and $M_i = f(x_i)$. Therefore

$$U(f, P) - L(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^n (f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})) \cdot \frac{b-a}{n} = \frac{b-a}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})).$$

The sum is a **telescoping sum**:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n (f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})) = f(x_n) - f(x_0) = f(b) - f(a).$$

Therefore

$$U(f, P) - L(f, P) = \frac{(b-a)(f(b) - f(a))}{n} < \epsilon.$$

By the $\epsilon$-criterion, $f$ is integrable. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Telescoping Is the Key">
For monotone functions, the sum of oscillations telescopes. This is a purely algebraic miracle: individual oscillations $M_i - m_i = f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})$ may be nonzero, but they sum to $f(b) - f(a)$ regardless of the partition. Dividing by $n$ makes the total as small as we like.
</Callout>

## What Discontinuities Does Riemann Integration Tolerate?

We have shown that continuous and monotone functions are integrable. But continuity is not necessary --
monotone functions can have (countably many) jump discontinuities and remain integrable.

<Callout type="info" title="Lebesgue's Criterion (Preview)">
A bounded function $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is Riemann integrable if and only if the set of discontinuities of $f$ has **Lebesgue measure zero**. This means: countably many discontinuities are fine, and even certain uncountable sets of discontinuities are permissible (like the Cantor set). This is a deep result we will not prove here, but it unifies all our examples.
</Callout>

Reproduce the proof that every continuous $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is Riemann integrable. Your proof should:
1. State what result you use to go from continuous to uniformly continuous.
2. Show how uniform continuity gives an oscillation bound on each subinterval.
3. Sum the bounds to verify the $\epsilon$-criterion.

<Solution>
**Proof.** Let $f \colon [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous and let $\epsilon > 0$.

**Step 1 (Uniform continuity).** Since $[a, b]$ is compact and $f$ is continuous, by the Heine-Cantor theorem $f$ is uniformly continuous on $[a, b]$. Choose $\delta > 0$ such that $|x - y| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| < \frac{\epsilon}{b - a}$.

**Step 2 (Oscillation bound).** Let $P$ be any partition with $\|P\| < \delta$. For any subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ and any $s, t \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$, we have $|s - t| \leq \Delta x_i < \delta$, so $|f(s) - f(t)| < \frac{\epsilon}{b - a}$.

Taking supremum over $s$ and infimum over $t$: $\omega_i = M_i - m_i \leq \frac{\epsilon}{b - a}$.

**Step 3 (Summing).** Therefore

$$U(f, P) - L(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^n \omega_i \, \Delta x_i \leq \frac{\epsilon}{b - a} \sum_{i=1}^n \Delta x_i = \frac{\epsilon}{b-a} \cdot (b - a) = \epsilon.$$

By the $\epsilon$-partition criterion, $f$ is Riemann integrable. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 3.3 (Counterexample).** Define $f \colon [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R}$ by enumerating the rationals in $[0,1]$ as $\{r_1, r_2, r_3, \ldots\}$ and setting

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 1/k & \text{if } x = r_k, \\ 0 & \text{if } x \notin \mathbb{Q}. \end{cases}$$

This function has **countably many** discontinuities (at every rational). Show that $f$ is Riemann integrable on $[0, 1]$ with $\int_0^1 f = 0$. Then explain why the Dirichlet function, which is also discontinuous only on a set of measure zero (the rationals), is nonetheless NOT integrable -- and identify what structural property distinguishes the two.

*Hint:* For any $\varepsilon > 0$, only finitely many rationals $r_k$ have $f(r_k) \geq \varepsilon$. This is the same idea as Thomae's function.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof of integrability.* Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Choose $K$ so that $1/K < \varepsilon/2$. The set $B = \{r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_K\}$ of "bad" rationals (where $f(r_k) \geq \varepsilon/2$) is finite, $|B| = K$.

Enclose each $r_k \in B$ in a subinterval of width $\delta = \varepsilon / (4K)$. The total width of these subintervals is at most $K \cdot \delta = \varepsilon/4$. On these subintervals, $\omega_i \leq 1$ (since $0 \leq f \leq 1$), contributing at most $1 \cdot \varepsilon/4 = \varepsilon/4$ to $U - L$.

On all remaining subintervals, $f(x) < 1/K < \varepsilon/2$ for every $x$ in the subinterval (since all rationals $r_k$ with $k \leq K$ are already enclosed). Since $f \geq 0$, the oscillation on these subintervals is at most $\varepsilon/2$. Their total width is at most $1$, contributing at most $\varepsilon/2$ to $U - L$.

Therefore $U(f, P) - L(f, P) \leq \varepsilon/4 + \varepsilon/2 < \varepsilon$, so $f$ is integrable. Since $L(f, P) = 0$ for every partition (irrationals are dense, giving $m_i = 0$), we have $\int_0^1 f = 0$.

**Why the Dirichlet function fails:** The Dirichlet function $D$ has $D(x) = 1$ for ALL rationals. There is no finite "bad set" -- every rational contributes oscillation 1, and the rationals are dense in every subinterval. The key distinction is: in $f$ above, for any threshold $\varepsilon > 0$, only finitely many points exceed $\varepsilon$. For $D$, uncountably... in fact, densely many points attain the value 1 in every subinterval.

More precisely, by Lebesgue's criterion: $f$ is continuous at every irrational (same argument as Thomae's function), so its discontinuity set $\mathbb{Q} \cap [0,1]$ has measure zero, and $f$ is integrable. The Dirichlet function is discontinuous at every point of $[0,1]$ (a set of measure 1), so it fails Lebesgue's criterion. $\square$
</Solution>

# Test integrability for several continuous functions

    print(f"f(x) = {name} on [{a:.1f}, {b:.4f}]")

    for n in [10, 100, 1000]:
        x = np.linspace(a, b, n + 1)

            samples = np.linspace(x[i], x[i+1], 200)
            vals = f(samples)
            U += np.max(vals) * (x[i+1] - x[i])
