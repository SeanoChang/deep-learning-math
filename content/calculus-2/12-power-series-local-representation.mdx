# Power Series and Local Representation

Power series are the bridge between algebra and analysis. A power series $\sum c_n(x - a)^n$ is simultaneously an infinite polynomial and a function, and understanding where and how it converges is one of the central problems of analysis.

## Power Series: Basic Setup

<Callout type="definition">
**Definition 12.1 (Power Series).** A **power series centered at $a$** is a series of the form

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n (x - a)^n = c_0 + c_1(x - a) + c_2(x - a)^2 + \cdots$$

where $(c_n)$ is a sequence of real coefficients and $x$ is a real variable.
</Callout>

The fundamental question: for which values of $x$ does this series converge?

## The Radius of Convergence

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 12.2 (Existence of Radius of Convergence).** For any power series $\sum c_n (x - a)^n$, there exists $R \in [0, \infty]$ such that:

1. The series converges absolutely for all $x$ with $|x - a| < R$.
2. The series diverges for all $x$ with $|x - a| > R$.
3. At $|x - a| = R$, either behavior is possible.

The number $R$ is called the **radius of convergence**.
</Callout>

*Proof.* Let $S = \{r \geq 0 : \sum |c_n| r^n \text{ converges}\}$. Note $0 \in S$ (trivially). Let $R = \sup S$ (possibly $+\infty$).

**Claim 1:** If $|x - a| < R$, the series converges absolutely. Choose $r$ with $|x - a| < r < R$. Since $r < R = \sup S$, there exists $s \in S$ with $r < s$. So $\sum |c_n| s^n$ converges, which means $|c_n| s^n$ is bounded: $|c_n| s^n \leq M$ for some $M$. Then

$$|c_n (x-a)^n| = |c_n| |x-a|^n \leq M \left(\frac{|x-a|}{s}\right)^n.$$

Since $|x - a|/s < r/s < 1$, this is a convergent geometric series, so $\sum |c_n(x-a)^n|$ converges by comparison.

**Claim 2:** If $|x - a| > R$, the series diverges. If it converged, the same argument would show convergence for some $r > |x-a| > R$, contradicting $R = \sup S$. More precisely, convergence at $x$ implies $|c_n(x-a)^n| \to 0$, so $|c_n||x-a|^n$ is bounded, and the geometric argument above would give convergence for all $|y - a| < |x - a|$, making $|x - a| \in S$, contradicting $|x - a| > R$. $\square$

## The Cauchy-Hadamard Formula

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 12.3 (Cauchy-Hadamard).** The radius of convergence of $\sum c_n(x - a)^n$ is given by

$$R = \frac{1}{\limsup_{n \to \infty} |c_n|^{1/n}}$$

with the conventions $1/0 = \infty$ and $1/\infty = 0$.
</Callout>

*Proof.* Let $\alpha = \limsup |c_n|^{1/n}$. The root test applied to $\sum c_n(x-a)^n$ gives

$$\limsup |c_n(x-a)^n|^{1/n} = \limsup |c_n|^{1/n} \cdot |x - a| = \alpha |x - a|.$$

By the root test: if $\alpha|x-a| < 1$ (i.e., $|x-a| < 1/\alpha$), the series converges absolutely; if $\alpha|x-a| > 1$ (i.e., $|x-a| > 1/\alpha$), the series diverges. So $R = 1/\alpha$. $\square$

<Callout type="tip">
**Computing $R$ in practice.** While the Cauchy-Hadamard formula is the definitive result, in practice we often use the ratio test: if $\lim |c_{n+1}/c_n|$ exists, then $R = \lim |c_n/c_{n+1}|$. This is typically easier to compute for explicit series.
</Callout>

## The Interval of Convergence

<Callout type="info">
**Remark.** The radius $R$ tells us about the open interval $(a - R, a + R)$, but the endpoints $x = a \pm R$ require separate analysis. Classic examples:

- $\sum x^n$: $R = 1$. Diverges at both $x = \pm 1$.
- $\sum x^n / n$: $R = 1$. Converges at $x = -1$ (alternating harmonic), diverges at $x = 1$ (harmonic).
- $\sum x^n / n^2$: $R = 1$. Converges at both $x = \pm 1$ (by comparison with $\sum 1/n^2$).
</Callout>

## Uniform Convergence on Compact Subintervals

This is the key structural result about power series: they converge not just pointwise but uniformly on any closed subinterval strictly inside the radius.

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 12.4 (Uniform Convergence of Power Series).** If $\sum c_n(x - a)^n$ has radius of convergence $R > 0$, then the series converges uniformly on $[a - r, a + r]$ for every $0 < r < R$.
</Callout>

*Proof.* Fix $0 < r < R$. For $|x - a| \leq r$, we have

$$|c_n(x - a)^n| \leq |c_n| r^n =: M_n.$$

Since $r < R$, the series $\sum M_n = \sum |c_n| r^n$ converges (by the definition of radius of convergence). By the Weierstrass $M$-test, $\sum c_n(x - a)^n$ converges uniformly on $\{x : |x - a| \leq r\} = [a - r, a + r]$. $\square$

<Callout type="warning">
**Not uniform on the full open interval!** The convergence is generally NOT uniform on the entire open interval $(a - R, a + R)$. For example, $\sum x^n = 1/(1-x)$ on $(-1, 1)$: the partial sums converge pointwise, but the supremum of the error over all $x \in (-1,1)$ never goes to zero (the function blows up as $x \to 1^-$).
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem>
**Exercise 12.1.** Prove that the power series $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n(x - a)^n$ converges uniformly on $[a - r, a + r]$ for any $0 < r < R$, by explicitly constructing the $M_n$ for the Weierstrass $M$-test and verifying all hypotheses.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* Let $0 < r < R$. For each $n \geq 0$, define $M_n = |c_n| r^n$. We verify the hypotheses of the Weierstrass $M$-test:

**Step 1 (Domination).** For any $x$ with $|x - a| \leq r$:

$$|c_n(x - a)^n| = |c_n| \cdot |x - a|^n \leq |c_n| \cdot r^n = M_n.$$

**Step 2 ($\sum M_n$ converges).** Since $r < R$, the point $x = a + r$ satisfies $|x - a| = r < R$. By Theorem 12.2, the series converges absolutely at $x = a + r$:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |c_n (a + r - a)^n| = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |c_n| r^n = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} M_n < \infty.$$

**Conclusion.** By the Weierstrass $M$-test, $\sum c_n(x-a)^n$ converges uniformly on $\{x : |x - a| \leq r\} = [a - r, a + r]$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 12.2.** Find the radius of convergence and determine convergence at the endpoints for the series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^n}{n \cdot 3^n}$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
**Solution.** Here $c_n = (-1)^n / (n \cdot 3^n)$ and the series is centered at $a = 0$.

**Radius.** By the ratio test: $|c_{n+1}/c_n| = \frac{n}{n+1} \cdot \frac{1}{3} \to \frac{1}{3}$, so $R = 3$.

**Endpoint $x = 3$:** The series becomes $\sum \frac{(-1)^n 3^n}{n \cdot 3^n} = \sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n}$, which converges by the AST.

**Endpoint $x = -3$:** The series becomes $\sum \frac{(-1)^n (-3)^n}{n \cdot 3^n} = \sum \frac{(-1)^n (-1)^n}{n} = \sum \frac{1}{n}$, which diverges (harmonic series).

**Interval of convergence:** $(-3, 3]$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 12.3 (Counterexample).** Exhibit a power series with radius of convergence $R = 1$ that converges at $x = -1$ but diverges at $x = 1$. Prove both claims.

*Hint:* Consider $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n}$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Solution.* Consider the power series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n}$, centered at $a = 0$ with $c_n = 1/n$.

**Radius of convergence:** By the ratio test, $|c_{n+1}/c_n| = n/(n+1) \to 1$, so $R = 1$.

**At $x = 1$:** The series becomes $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n}$, the harmonic series, which diverges ($p$-series with $p = 1$).

**At $x = -1$:** The series becomes $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n} = -\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}$, the negative of the alternating harmonic series. By the Leibniz AST, this converges (since $1/n$ is decreasing and $1/n \to 0$). Its sum is $-\ln 2$.

**Conclusion:** The interval of convergence is $[-1, 1)$. This demonstrates that endpoint behavior must be analyzed separately -- the radius of convergence tells us nothing about what happens at $|x - a| = R$. The series $\sum x^n/n$ converges at the left endpoint ($x = -1$, by alternation and cancellation) but diverges at the right endpoint ($x = 1$, harmonic series). In contrast, $\sum x^n/n^2$ converges at both endpoints, and $\sum x^n$ diverges at both. $\square$
</Solution>
