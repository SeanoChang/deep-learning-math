# Dual Spaces and Linear Functionals

Up to this point we have studied vector spaces and the operators between them. We now turn
to a different kind of object: *linear maps from a vector space to its base field*. These deceptively
simple maps -- called linear functionals -- form a vector space of their own (the dual space), and
their theory reveals deep structural features of linear algebra. The dual space is the gateway to
bilinear forms, tensor products, and the mathematical framework of quantum mechanics and optimization.

## Linear Functionals

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Linear functional).** Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$. A **linear functional**
  on $V$ is a linear map $\varphi \colon V \to \mathbb{F}$. That is:
  1. $\varphi(u + v) = \varphi(u) + \varphi(v)$ for all $u, v \in V$.
  2. $\varphi(cv) = c\,\varphi(v)$ for all $c \in \mathbb{F}$, $v \in V$.
</Callout>

**Examples.**

1. **Evaluation at a coordinate.** Let $V = \mathbb{R}^n$ and define $\varphi_i(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = x_i$.
   This "picks off" the $i$-th coordinate. It is clearly linear.

2. **Trace.** Let $V = M_{n \times n}(\mathbb{R})$ (the space of $n \times n$ matrices). The trace
   $\operatorname{tr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n a_{ii}$ is a linear functional on $V$.

3. **Integration.** Let $V = C([0,1])$, the space of continuous functions on $[0,1]$. Then
   $\varphi(f) = \int_0^1 f(x)\,dx$ is a linear functional.

4. **Zero functional.** $\varphi(v) = 0$ for all $v$ is always a linear functional.

## The Dual Space

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Dual space).** The **dual space** of $V$, denoted $V^*$, is the set of all linear functionals on $V$:
  $$V^* = \mathcal{L}(V, \mathbb{F}) = \{\varphi \colon V \to \mathbb{F} : \varphi \text{ is linear}\}.$$
  Since $\mathcal{L}(V, \mathbb{F})$ is itself a vector space (with pointwise addition and scalar multiplication),
  $V^*$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$.
</Callout>

The fundamental question is: how does $V^*$ relate to $V$? In particular, what is $\dim V^*$?

## The Dual Basis

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Dual basis).** Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space with basis $\mathcal{B} = \{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$.
  For each $i = 1, \ldots, n$, define $\varphi_i \colon V \to \mathbb{F}$ by
  $$\varphi_i(e_j) = \delta_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } i = j \\ 0 & \text{if } i \neq j \end{cases}$$
  and extend by linearity. Then:
  1. Each $\varphi_i$ is a well-defined linear functional (i.e., $\varphi_i \in V^*$).
  2. $\{\varphi_1, \ldots, \varphi_n\}$ is a basis for $V^*$, called the **dual basis** of $\mathcal{B}$.
  3. In particular, $\dim V^* = \dim V = n$.
  *Proof.*
  **(1)** A linear map is uniquely determined by its values on a basis. Since we specified $\varphi_i(e_j)$
  for all basis vectors, $\varphi_i$ extends uniquely to a linear functional by
  $$\varphi_i\left(\sum_{j=1}^n c_j e_j\right) = \sum_{j=1}^n c_j \varphi_i(e_j) = c_i.$$
  **(2)** We show $\{\varphi_1, \ldots, \varphi_n\}$ is linearly independent and spans $V^*$.
  **Linear independence:** Suppose $a_1 \varphi_1 + \cdots + a_n \varphi_n = 0$ (the zero functional).
  Evaluate at $e_j$:
  $$0 = (a_1 \varphi_1 + \cdots + a_n \varphi_n)(e_j) = a_1 \delta_{1j} + \cdots + a_n \delta_{nj} = a_j.$$
  So $a_j = 0$ for all $j$. The functionals are linearly independent.
  **Spanning:** Let $\psi \in V^*$ be any linear functional. Define $a_i = \psi(e_i)$. We claim
  $\psi = a_1 \varphi_1 + \cdots + a_n \varphi_n$. For any $v = c_1 e_1 + \cdots + c_n e_n$:
  $$(a_1 \varphi_1 + \cdots + a_n \varphi_n)(v) = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i c_i = \sum_{i=1}^n \psi(e_i) c_i = \psi\left(\sum_{i=1}^n c_i e_i\right) = \psi(v).$$
  Since this holds for all $v$, $\psi = \sum a_i \varphi_i$.
  **(3)** Follows immediately: a basis of $n$ elements gives $\dim V^* = n$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Notation.** The dual basis is often written $\{e^1, \ldots, e^n\}$ or $\{e_1^*, \ldots, e_n^*\}$,
  with superscripts to distinguish from the original basis. The defining property is $e^i(e_j) = \delta^i_j$.
  In the language of differential geometry and physics, this index placement (upper vs lower) is the
  beginning of the distinction between **contravariant** and **covariant** objects.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
  **The dual basis depends on the choice of basis for $V$.** Different bases for $V$ yield different
  dual bases for $V^*$. While $V$ and $V^*$ have the same dimension, there is no *canonical* (basis-independent)
  isomorphism between them in general. This changes once we add extra structure (an inner product), as we
  will see in later lessons.
</Callout>

## Dual Basis in Coordinates

If $\mathcal{B} = \{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ is the standard basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$, then the dual basis
elements are $\varphi_i(x) = x_i$ -- they extract coordinates. A general functional $\psi \in (\mathbb{R}^n)^*$
with $\psi(e_i) = a_i$ acts as

$$\psi(x) = a_1 x_1 + a_2 x_2 + \cdots + a_n x_n = a^T x.$$

Every linear functional on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a dot product with some fixed vector. This is a special
case of the Riesz representation theorem (for inner product spaces).

## The Transpose (Dual Map)

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Transpose / Dual map).** Let $T \colon V \to W$ be a linear map. The **transpose** (or **dual map**)
  is the map $T^* \colon W^* \to V^*$ defined by
  $$T^*(\psi) = \psi \circ T$$
  for $\psi \in W^*$. That is, $(T^*\psi)(v) = \psi(Tv)$ for all $v \in V$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Proposition.** The transpose $T^*$ is a linear map from $W^*$ to $V^*$.
  *Proof.* Let $\psi_1, \psi_2 \in W^*$ and $c \in \mathbb{F}$.
  $$T^*(\psi_1 + \psi_2)(v) = (\psi_1 + \psi_2)(Tv) = \psi_1(Tv) + \psi_2(Tv) = T^*(\psi_1)(v) + T^*(\psi_2)(v).$$
  $$T^*(c\psi)(v) = (c\psi)(Tv) = c \cdot \psi(Tv) = c \cdot T^*(\psi)(v).$$
  These hold for all $v$, so $T^*(\psi_1 + \psi_2) = T^*\psi_1 + T^*\psi_2$ and $T^*(c\psi) = cT^*\psi$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Proposition (Matrix of the transpose).** If $T \colon V \to W$ has matrix $A$ with respect to chosen bases
  $\mathcal{B}_V$ and $\mathcal{B}_W$, then $T^* \colon W^* \to V^*$ has matrix $A^T$ with respect to the
  corresponding dual bases.
  *Proof sketch.* Let $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ be the basis for $V$ with dual basis $\{\varphi_1, \ldots, \varphi_n\}$,
  and $\{f_1, \ldots, f_m\}$ for $W$ with dual $\{\psi_1, \ldots, \psi_m\}$. Then
  $(T^*\psi_j)(e_i) = \psi_j(Te_i) = \psi_j\left(\sum_k a_{ki} f_k\right) = a_{ji}$. This means the
  $(i,j)$-entry of the matrix of $T^*$ is $a_{ji}$, which is the $(j,i)$-entry of $A$. Hence the matrix
  is $A^T$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

This justifies the name "transpose" -- the dual map is the abstract, coordinate-free version of
matrix transposition.

## The Double Dual and the Natural Isomorphism

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Double dual).** The **double dual** of $V$ is $V^{**} = (V^*)^*$, the dual space of $V^*$.
  Its elements are linear functionals on $V^*$, i.e., maps $\Phi \colon V^* \to \mathbb{F}$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Canonical isomorphism $V \cong V^{**}$).** For a finite-dimensional vector space $V$, there
  is a natural (canonical, basis-independent) isomorphism $\iota \colon V \to V^{**}$ defined by
  $$\iota(v)(\varphi) = \varphi(v)$$
  for $v \in V$ and $\varphi \in V^*$.
  *Proof.* We verify:
  **$\iota$ is well-defined:** For fixed $v$, the map $\hat{v} = \iota(v)$ sends $\varphi \mapsto \varphi(v)$.
  This is linear in $\varphi$: $\hat{v}(\varphi + \psi) = (\varphi + \psi)(v) = \varphi(v) + \psi(v) = \hat{v}(\varphi) + \hat{v}(\psi)$,
  and $\hat{v}(c\varphi) = c\varphi(v) = c\hat{v}(\varphi)$. So $\hat{v} \in V^{**}$.
  **$\iota$ is linear:** $\iota(u + v)(\varphi) = \varphi(u + v) = \varphi(u) + \varphi(v) = \iota(u)(\varphi) + \iota(v)(\varphi)$,
  and $\iota(cv)(\varphi) = \varphi(cv) = c\varphi(v) = c\iota(v)(\varphi)$.
  **$\iota$ is injective:** Suppose $\iota(v) = 0$, i.e., $\varphi(v) = 0$ for all $\varphi \in V^*$.
  We must show $v = 0$. If $v \neq 0$, extend $\{v\}$ to a basis and let $\varphi$ be the dual basis
  element corresponding to $v$. Then $\varphi(v) = 1 \neq 0$, a contradiction. Hence $v = 0$, and
  $\ker \iota = \{0\}$.
  **$\iota$ is surjective:** Since $\dim V = \dim V^* = \dim V^{**} = n$, an injective linear map
  between spaces of equal dimension is surjective. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Why "natural" matters.** The isomorphism $V \cong V^*$ *requires choosing a basis* -- different bases give
  different isomorphisms. The isomorphism $V \cong V^{**}$ is defined *without any choice*: the formula
  $\iota(v)(\varphi) = \varphi(v)$ makes sense for any $v$ and $\varphi$. This is a key example of
  the distinction between a "natural" (canonical) isomorphism and a mere dimensional coincidence.
  In category theory, this becomes the concept of a **natural transformation**.
</Callout>

## Annihilators

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Annihilator).** Let $U \subseteq V$ be a subspace. The **annihilator** of $U$ is
  $$U^0 = \{\varphi \in V^* : \varphi(u) = 0 \text{ for all } u \in U\}.$$
  This is a subspace of $V^*$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Dimension of annihilator).** If $V$ is finite-dimensional and $U \subseteq V$ is a subspace, then
  $$\dim U^0 = \dim V - \dim U.$$
  *Proof.* Let $\dim U = k$ and choose a basis $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$ of $U$. Extend to a basis
  $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k, e_{k+1}, \ldots, e_n\}$ of $V$. Let $\{\varphi_1, \ldots, \varphi_n\}$ be the dual basis.
  We claim $U^0 = \operatorname{span}\{\varphi_{k+1}, \ldots, \varphi_n\}$.
  ($\supseteq$): For $i > k$ and $j \leq k$: $\varphi_i(e_j) = \delta_{ij} = 0$. By linearity,
  $\varphi_i(u) = 0$ for all $u \in U$. So $\varphi_i \in U^0$.
  ($\subseteq$): Let $\psi = \sum a_i \varphi_i \in U^0$. For $j \leq k$: $0 = \psi(e_j) = a_j$.
  So $\psi = \sum_{i=k+1}^n a_i \varphi_i \in \operatorname{span}\{\varphi_{k+1}, \ldots, \varphi_n\}$.
  Hence $\dim U^0 = n - k$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem>
  **Exercise 1 (Dual basis construction).** Let $V = \mathbb{R}^2$ with basis
  $\mathcal{B} = \{(1, 1)^T, (1, -1)^T\}$.
  (a) Find the dual basis $\{\varphi_1, \varphi_2\}$ explicitly. That is, express each $\varphi_i$ as
  $\varphi_i(x_1, x_2) = a_i x_1 + b_i x_2$.
  (b) Verify the defining property $\varphi_i(e_j) = \delta_{ij}$.
  (c) Express the functional $\psi(x_1, x_2) = 3x_1 + x_2$ as a linear combination of $\varphi_1$ and $\varphi_2$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** We need $\varphi_1(1,1) = 1$, $\varphi_1(1,-1) = 0$, $\varphi_2(1,1) = 0$, $\varphi_2(1,-1) = 1$.
  For $\varphi_1(x_1, x_2) = a x_1 + b x_2$:
  - $a + b = 1$
  - $a - b = 0$
  Solving: $a = b = 1/2$. So $\varphi_1(x_1, x_2) = \frac{1}{2}x_1 + \frac{1}{2}x_2$.
  For $\varphi_2(x_1, x_2) = c x_1 + d x_2$:
  - $c + d = 0$
  - $c - d = 1$
  Solving: $c = 1/2$, $d = -1/2$. So $\varphi_2(x_1, x_2) = \frac{1}{2}x_1 - \frac{1}{2}x_2$.
  **(b)** Verification:
  - $\varphi_1(1,1) = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1$ and $\varphi_1(1,-1) = 1/2 - 1/2 = 0$.
  - $\varphi_2(1,1) = 1/2 - 1/2 = 0$ and $\varphi_2(1,-1) = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1$.
  **(c)** We need $\psi = \alpha \varphi_1 + \beta \varphi_2$. Evaluate at the basis vectors:
  - $\psi(1,1) = 3 + 1 = 4 = \alpha \cdot 1 + \beta \cdot 0 = \alpha$.
  - $\psi(1,-1) = 3 - 1 = 2 = \alpha \cdot 0 + \beta \cdot 1 = \beta$.
  So $\psi = 4\varphi_1 + 2\varphi_2$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
  **Exercise 2 (Natural isomorphism proof).** Prove that the canonical map $\iota \colon V \to V^{**}$
  defined by $\iota(v)(\varphi) = \varphi(v)$ is injective, without using the dimension argument.
  *Hint:* If $\iota(v) = 0$, then $\varphi(v) = 0$ for every $\varphi \in V^*$. Construct a specific
  $\varphi$ that contradicts $v \neq 0$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  *Proof.* Suppose $\iota(v) = 0$, i.e., $\varphi(v) = 0$ for all $\varphi \in V^*$. We claim $v = 0$.
  Assume for contradiction that $v \neq 0$. Extend $\{v\}$ to a basis $\{v, e_2, \ldots, e_n\}$ of $V$.
  Let $\{\varphi_1, \varphi_2, \ldots, \varphi_n\}$ be the corresponding dual basis. By definition of the
  dual basis, $\varphi_1(v) = 1$.
  But $\iota(v) = 0$ means $\varphi(v) = 0$ for *all* $\varphi \in V^*$. In particular,
  $\varphi_1(v) = 0$. This contradicts $\varphi_1(v) = 1$.
  Therefore $v = 0$, hence $\ker \iota = \{0\}$, and $\iota$ is injective. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
  **Exercise 3 (Transpose computation).** Let $T \colon \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ be defined by
  $T(x_1, x_2) = (x_1 + x_2, \, 2x_1, \, x_1 - x_2)$ with respect to standard bases.
  (a) Write the matrix $A$ of $T$.
  (b) Compute $A^T$ and interpret it as the matrix of $T^*$.
  (c) If $\psi \in (\mathbb{R}^3)^*$ is given by $\psi(y_1, y_2, y_3) = y_1 + y_2 + y_3$, compute
  $T^*(\psi) \in (\mathbb{R}^2)^*$ directly from the definition $T^*(\psi) = \psi \circ T$, and verify
  it matches $A^T$ applied to the coordinate vector of $\psi$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** $T(e_1) = (1, 2, 1)^T$ and $T(e_2) = (1, 0, -1)^T$, so
  $$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}.$$
  **(b)** $A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$.
  This is a $2 \times 3$ matrix representing $T^* \colon (\mathbb{R}^3)^* \to (\mathbb{R}^2)^*$.
  **(c)** $(T^*\psi)(x_1, x_2) = \psi(T(x_1, x_2)) = \psi(x_1 + x_2, 2x_1, x_1 - x_2)$
  $= (x_1 + x_2) + 2x_1 + (x_1 - x_2) = 4x_1$.
  The coordinate vector of $\psi$ is $(1, 1, 1)^T$. Applying $A^T$:
  $$A^T \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 + 2 + 1 \\ 1 + 0 - 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}.$$
  So $T^*\psi$ has coordinate vector $(4, 0)^T$, representing the functional $(x_1, x_2) \mapsto 4x_1 + 0x_2 = 4x_1$. This matches the direct computation. $\square$
</Solution>
