# Inner Products and Orthogonality

Until now, our theory of vector spaces has been purely algebraic: we can add vectors, scale them,
and study linear maps, but we cannot measure *lengths* or *angles*. An inner product introduces
geometry into a vector space, allowing us to define distance, perpendicularity, and projection.
These geometric ideas are not merely visual aids -- they underpin least squares, Fourier analysis,
quantum mechanics, and the optimization algorithms at the heart of deep learning.

## Inner Products

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Inner product, real case).** Let $V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb{R}$. An **inner product**
  on $V$ is a function $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle \colon V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying for all
  $u, v, w \in V$ and $c \in \mathbb{R}$:
  1. **Symmetry:** $\langle u, v \rangle = \langle v, u \rangle$.
  2. **Linearity in the first argument:** $\langle cu + w, v \rangle = c\langle u, v \rangle + \langle w, v \rangle$.
  3. **Positive definiteness:** $\langle v, v \rangle \geq 0$, with equality if and only if $v = 0$.
  A vector space equipped with an inner product is called an **inner product space**.
</Callout>

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Inner product, complex case).** Let $V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb{C}$. An **inner product**
  on $V$ is a function $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle \colon V \times V \to \mathbb{C}$ satisfying:
  1. **Conjugate symmetry:** $\langle u, v \rangle = \overline{\langle v, u \rangle}$.
  2. **Linearity in the first argument:** $\langle cu + w, v \rangle = c\langle u, v \rangle + \langle w, v \rangle$.
  3. **Positive definiteness:** $\langle v, v \rangle \geq 0$ (this value is real by conjugate symmetry), with equality iff $v = 0$.
  Note that conjugate symmetry and linearity in the first argument imply **conjugate-linearity in the second argument:**
  $\langle u, cv \rangle = \bar{c}\langle u, v \rangle$.
</Callout>

**Examples.**

1. **Standard inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$.** $\langle x, y \rangle = x^T y = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i$.

2. **Standard inner product on $\mathbb{C}^n$.** $\langle x, y \rangle = x^* y = \sum_{i=1}^n \bar{x}_i y_i$,
   where $x^*$ denotes the conjugate transpose.

3. **$L^2$ inner product on $C([a,b])$.** $\langle f, g \rangle = \int_a^b f(x)g(x)\,dx$ (real-valued)
   or $\int_a^b \overline{f(x)}g(x)\,dx$ (complex-valued).

4. **Weighted inner product.** $\langle x, y \rangle_W = x^T W y$ where $W$ is a symmetric positive definite matrix.

## Norms

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Induced norm).** Given an inner product space $(V, \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle)$, the
  **norm** (or **length**) of $v \in V$ is
  $$\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v, v \rangle}.$$
  The **distance** between $u$ and $v$ is $\|u - v\|$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Proposition (Basic norm properties).** For all $u, v \in V$ and $c \in \mathbb{F}$:
  1. $\|v\| \geq 0$, with equality iff $v = 0$.
  2. $\|cv\| = |c| \cdot \|v\|$ (absolute homogeneity).
  3. $\|u + v\| \leq \|u\| + \|v\|$ (triangle inequality -- proved below).
  *Proof of (2).* $\|cv\|^2 = \langle cv, cv \rangle = c\bar{c}\langle v, v \rangle = |c|^2 \|v\|^2$.
  Taking square roots: $\|cv\| = |c| \cdot \|v\|$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## The Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

This is arguably the most important inequality in all of mathematics.

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Cauchy-Schwarz inequality).** For all $u, v$ in an inner product space,
  $$|\langle u, v \rangle| \leq \|u\| \cdot \|v\|.$$
  Equality holds if and only if $u$ and $v$ are linearly dependent (i.e., one is a scalar multiple of the other).
  *Proof.* If $v = 0$, both sides are zero. Assume $v \neq 0$.
  For any $t \in \mathbb{F}$, consider the vector $u - tv$. By positive definiteness:
  $$0 \leq \|u - tv\|^2 = \langle u - tv, u - tv \rangle = \langle u, u \rangle - t\langle v, u \rangle - \bar{t}\langle u, v \rangle + |t|^2 \langle v, v \rangle.$$
  Choose $t = \frac{\langle u, v \rangle}{\langle v, v \rangle}$ (the optimal value that minimizes the expression). Then $\bar{t} = \frac{\overline{\langle u, v \rangle}}{\langle v, v \rangle} = \frac{\langle v, u \rangle}{\|v\|^2}$.
  Substituting:
  $$0 \leq \|u\|^2 - \frac{\langle u, v \rangle \langle v, u \rangle}{\|v\|^2} - \frac{\langle v, u \rangle \langle u, v \rangle}{\|v\|^2} + \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^4}\|v\|^2$$
  $$= \|u\|^2 - \frac{2|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2} + \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2} = \|u\|^2 - \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2}.$$
  Rearranging: $|\langle u, v \rangle|^2 \leq \|u\|^2 \|v\|^2$. Taking square roots gives the result.
  **Equality case:** Equality holds iff $\|u - tv\|^2 = 0$ iff $u = tv$, i.e., $u$ and $v$ are linearly dependent. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Geometric interpretation.** In $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the standard inner product, $\langle u, v \rangle = \|u\|\|v\|\cos\theta$
  where $\theta$ is the angle between $u$ and $v$. Cauchy-Schwarz says $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$ -- a fact
  obvious geometrically but whose proof requires the algebraic argument above. The inner product lets us
  *define* angles in abstract vector spaces.
</Callout>

## The Triangle Inequality

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Triangle inequality).** For all $u, v$ in an inner product space,
  $$\|u + v\| \leq \|u\| + \|v\|.$$
  *Proof.* Expand $\|u + v\|^2$:
  $$\|u + v\|^2 = \langle u + v, u + v \rangle = \|u\|^2 + \langle u, v \rangle + \overline{\langle u, v \rangle} + \|v\|^2 = \|u\|^2 + 2\operatorname{Re}\langle u, v \rangle + \|v\|^2.$$
  Since $\operatorname{Re}\langle u, v \rangle \leq |\langle u, v \rangle| \leq \|u\|\|v\|$ (by Cauchy-Schwarz):
  $$\|u + v\|^2 \leq \|u\|^2 + 2\|u\|\|v\| + \|v\|^2 = (\|u\| + \|v\|)^2.$$
  Taking square roots (both sides are nonneg): $\|u + v\| \leq \|u\| + \|v\|$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## Orthogonality

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Orthogonal vectors).** Two vectors $u, v$ in an inner product space are **orthogonal**
  (written $u \perp v$) if $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$.
  A set of vectors $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ is **orthogonal** if $\langle v_i, v_j \rangle = 0$ for all $i \neq j$.
  It is **orthonormal** if additionally $\|v_i\| = 1$ for all $i$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Orthogonal vectors are independent).** An orthogonal set of nonzero vectors is linearly independent.
  *Proof.* Suppose $c_1 v_1 + \cdots + c_k v_k = 0$ where $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ is orthogonal with each
  $v_i \neq 0$. Take the inner product with $v_j$:
  $$0 = \left\langle \sum_{i=1}^k c_i v_i, v_j \right\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^k c_i \langle v_i, v_j \rangle = c_j \langle v_j, v_j \rangle = c_j \|v_j\|^2.$$
  Since $v_j \neq 0$, $\|v_j\|^2 > 0$, so $c_j = 0$. This holds for all $j$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## Orthogonal Complements

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Orthogonal complement).** Let $U$ be a subset (typically a subspace) of an inner product space $V$.
  The **orthogonal complement** of $U$ is
  $$U^\perp = \{v \in V : \langle v, u \rangle = 0 \text{ for all } u \in U\}.$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Properties of orthogonal complements).** Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional inner product space
  and $U \subseteq V$ a subspace. Then:
  1. $U^\perp$ is a subspace of $V$.
  2. $V = U \oplus U^\perp$ (direct sum decomposition).
  3. $\dim U^\perp = \dim V - \dim U$.
  4. $(U^\perp)^\perp = U$.
  *Proof of (1).* Let $v_1, v_2 \in U^\perp$ and $c \in \mathbb{F}$. For any $u \in U$:
  $\langle cv_1 + v_2, u \rangle = c\langle v_1, u \rangle + \langle v_2, u \rangle = 0 + 0 = 0$.
  So $cv_1 + v_2 \in U^\perp$.
  *Proof of (2).* First, $U \cap U^\perp = \{0\}$: if $v \in U \cap U^\perp$, then $\langle v, v \rangle = 0$,
  so $v = 0$.
  To show $V = U + U^\perp$: let $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$ be an orthonormal basis for $U$ (obtained by
  Gram-Schmidt, which we prove in the next lesson). For any $v \in V$, define
  $$u = \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i \in U, \qquad w = v - u.$$
  We claim $w \in U^\perp$. For any $e_j$ ($j \leq k$):
  $$\langle w, e_j \rangle = \langle v, e_j \rangle - \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle \langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \langle v, e_j \rangle - \langle v, e_j \rangle = 0.$$
  Since $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$ spans $U$, $w \perp u'$ for all $u' \in U$. Hence $v = u + w$ with $u \in U$, $w \in U^\perp$.
  *Proof of (3).* Follows from $V = U \oplus U^\perp$: $\dim V = \dim U + \dim U^\perp$.
  *Proof of (4).* $U \subseteq (U^\perp)^\perp$ is clear (if $u \in U$, then $\langle u, w \rangle = 0$ for all
  $w \in U^\perp$, so $u \in (U^\perp)^\perp$). By (3), $\dim(U^\perp)^\perp = \dim V - \dim U^\perp = \dim V - (\dim V - \dim U) = \dim U$.
  Since $U \subseteq (U^\perp)^\perp$ and they have the same dimension, $U = (U^\perp)^\perp$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## The Pythagorean Theorem

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Pythagorean theorem).** If $u \perp v$, then
  $$\|u + v\|^2 = \|u\|^2 + \|v\|^2.$$
  *Proof.* $\|u + v\|^2 = \|u\|^2 + 2\operatorname{Re}\langle u, v \rangle + \|v\|^2 = \|u\|^2 + 0 + \|v\|^2$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

More generally, if $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ is an orthogonal set, then
$\|v_1 + \cdots + v_k\|^2 = \|v_1\|^2 + \cdots + \|v_k\|^2$.

<Problem>
  **Exercise 1 (Cauchy-Schwarz proof).** Reproduce the proof of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.
  Your proof should:
  (a) State the inequality precisely.
  (b) Handle the case $v = 0$ separately.
  (c) For $v \neq 0$, expand $\|u - tv\|^2 \geq 0$ with the optimal choice of $t$.
  (d) Characterize when equality holds.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **Theorem.** For all $u, v$ in an inner product space, $|\langle u, v \rangle| \leq \|u\|\|v\|$.
  Equality holds iff $u$ and $v$ are linearly dependent.
  *Proof.*
  **(b)** If $v = 0$, then $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$ and $\|v\| = 0$, so both sides are $0$.
  **(c)** Assume $v \neq 0$. For any $t \in \mathbb{F}$, positive definiteness gives:
  $$0 \leq \|u - tv\|^2 = \|u\|^2 - t\langle v, u \rangle - \bar{t}\langle u, v \rangle + |t|^2\|v\|^2.$$
  Set $t = \frac{\langle u, v \rangle}{\|v\|^2}$. Then $|t|^2 = \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^4}$,
  $t\langle v, u \rangle = \frac{\langle u, v \rangle \overline{\langle u, v \rangle}}{\|v\|^2} = \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2}$,
  and $\bar{t}\langle u, v \rangle = \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2}$.
  Substituting:
  $$0 \leq \|u\|^2 - \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2} - \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2} + \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2} = \|u\|^2 - \frac{|\langle u, v \rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2}.$$
  Therefore $|\langle u, v \rangle|^2 \leq \|u\|^2\|v\|^2$, giving $|\langle u, v \rangle| \leq \|u\|\|v\|$.
  **(d)** Equality holds iff $\|u - tv\| = 0$ iff $u = tv$, i.e., $\{u, v\}$ is linearly dependent. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
  **Exercise 2 (Orthogonal complement computation).** Let $V = \mathbb{R}^3$ with the standard inner product,
  and let $U = \operatorname{span}\{(1, 1, 1)^T\}$.
  (a) Find $U^\perp$ by solving $\langle v, (1,1,1)^T \rangle = 0$.
  (b) Verify that $\dim U + \dim U^\perp = 3$.
  (c) Decompose $w = (3, 1, 2)^T$ as $w = u + u^\perp$ where $u \in U$ and $u^\perp \in U^\perp$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** $U^\perp = \{(x_1, x_2, x_3)^T : x_1 + x_2 + x_3 = 0\}$. This is a plane through the origin
  with normal vector $(1,1,1)^T$. A basis is $\{(1,-1,0)^T, (1,0,-1)^T\}$.
  **(b)** $\dim U = 1$ and $\dim U^\perp = 2$. Sum: $1 + 2 = 3 = \dim V$.
  **(c)** The projection of $w$ onto $U$:
  $$u = \frac{\langle w, (1,1,1)^T \rangle}{\|(1,1,1)^T\|^2}(1,1,1)^T = \frac{3+1+2}{3}(1,1,1)^T = 2(1,1,1)^T = (2,2,2)^T.$$
  Then $u^\perp = w - u = (3,1,2)^T - (2,2,2)^T = (1,-1,0)^T$.
  Verification: $\langle u^\perp, (1,1,1)^T \rangle = 1 - 1 + 0 = 0$, confirming $u^\perp \in U^\perp$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
  **Exercise 3 (Parallelogram law).** Prove the **parallelogram law**: for all $u, v$ in an inner product space,
  $$\|u + v\|^2 + \|u - v\|^2 = 2\|u\|^2 + 2\|v\|^2.$$
  Then show that the converse holds: if a norm satisfies the parallelogram law, it arises from an inner
  product (state the polarization identity but you need not prove the full converse).
</Problem>

<Solution>
  *Proof of the parallelogram law.* Expand both sides:
  $$\|u + v\|^2 = \langle u+v, u+v \rangle = \|u\|^2 + 2\operatorname{Re}\langle u, v \rangle + \|v\|^2$$
  $$\|u - v\|^2 = \langle u-v, u-v \rangle = \|u\|^2 - 2\operatorname{Re}\langle u, v \rangle + \|v\|^2$$
  Adding: $\|u+v\|^2 + \|u-v\|^2 = 2\|u\|^2 + 2\|v\|^2$. The cross terms cancel. $\blacksquare$
  **Polarization identity (real case).** If $\|\cdot\|$ satisfies the parallelogram law, the inner product
  can be recovered as:
  $$\langle u, v \rangle = \frac{1}{4}\left(\|u+v\|^2 - \|u-v\|^2\right).$$
  This follows from the expansion above: $\|u+v\|^2 - \|u-v\|^2 = 4\operatorname{Re}\langle u,v\rangle = 4\langle u,v\rangle$
  in the real case.
  **Geometric interpretation:** The parallelogram law says that the sum of the squares of the diagonals of
  a parallelogram equals the sum of the squares of its four sides. This is a necessary (and, with suitable
  verification of the inner product axioms, sufficient) condition for a norm to come from an inner product.
  Not all norms arise from inner products -- the $\ell^1$ and $\ell^\infty$ norms violate the parallelogram law. $\square$
</Solution>
