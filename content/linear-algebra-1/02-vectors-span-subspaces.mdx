## Vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$

<Callout type="definition" title="Vectors in R^n">
  A **vector** in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is an ordered $n$-tuple of real numbers, written as a column:
  $$\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^n.$$
  **Vector addition** and **scalar multiplication** are defined component-wise:
  $$\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} u_1 + v_1 \\ \vdots \\ u_n + v_n \end{pmatrix}, \qquad c\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} cv_1 \\ \vdots \\ cv_n \end{pmatrix}.$$
  The **zero vector** is $\mathbf{0} = (0, 0, \ldots, 0)^T$.
</Callout>

These operations satisfy eight axioms (commutativity, associativity, distributivity, etc.) that make $\mathbb{R}^n$ a **vector space**. We will formalize this in Lesson 5. For now, we work concretely in $\mathbb{R}^n$ and build intuition for the abstract definition.

## Linear Combinations

<Callout type="definition" title="Linear Combination">
  A **linear combination** of vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is any vector of the form
  $$c_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_p \mathbf{v}_p,$$
  where $c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_p \in \mathbb{R}$ are scalars called the **weights** (or coefficients).
</Callout>

The question "Is $\mathbf{b}$ a linear combination of $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p$?" is equivalent to asking whether the system

$$x_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + x_2 \mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + x_p \mathbf{v}_p = \mathbf{b}$$

has a solution -- that is, whether the linear system with augmented matrix $[\mathbf{v}_1 \; \mathbf{v}_2 \; \cdots \; \mathbf{v}_p \mid \mathbf{b}]$ is consistent. This connection between geometric/algebraic questions and linear systems is the bridge between the two perspectives of linear algebra.

<Problem title="Definition Drill: Linear Combinations">
  Determine whether $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}$ is a linear combination of $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -5 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}$.
<Solution>
  We need to solve $x_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + x_2 \mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{b}$, which gives the augmented matrix:
  $$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 7 \\ -2 & 5 & 4 \\ -5 & 6 & -3 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 + 2R_1} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 7 \\ 0 & 9 & 18 \\ -5 & 6 & -3 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_3 + 5R_1} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 7 \\ 0 & 9 & 18 \\ 0 & 16 & 32 \end{pmatrix}$$
  $$\xrightarrow{R_2/9,\; R_3/16} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 7 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_3 - R_2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 7 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$
  The system is consistent. Back-substituting: $x_2 = 2$, $x_1 = 7 - 2(2) = 3$.
  Therefore $\mathbf{b} = 3\mathbf{v}_1 + 2\mathbf{v}_2$, and $\mathbf{b}$ is indeed a linear combination of $\mathbf{v}_1$ and $\mathbf{v}_2$.
</Solution>
</Problem>

## Span

<Callout type="definition" title="Span">
  The **span** of vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the set of all linear combinations:
  $$\text{Span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p\} = \{c_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + c_p \mathbf{v}_p : c_1, \ldots, c_p \in \mathbb{R}\}.$$
  We say that $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p$ **span** (or **generate**) the set $\text{Span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p\}$.
</Callout>

Geometrically:
- $\text{Span}\{\mathbf{0}\} = \{\mathbf{0}\}$: just the origin.
- $\text{Span}\{\mathbf{v}\}$ for $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$: a line through the origin.
- $\text{Span}\{\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\}$ for non-parallel nonzero vectors: a plane through the origin.

<Callout type="tip" title="Span and Linear Systems">
  $\mathbf{b} \in \text{Span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p\}$ if and only if the linear system $[\mathbf{v}_1 \; \cdots \; \mathbf{v}_p \mid \mathbf{b}]$ is consistent. Thus $\text{Span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p\} = \mathbb{R}^n$ if and only if the matrix $[\mathbf{v}_1 \; \cdots \; \mathbf{v}_p]$ has a pivot in every row.
</Callout>

## Subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^n$

<Callout type="definition" title="Subspace">
  A **subspace** of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a subset $H \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ satisfying:
  1. $\mathbf{0} \in H$ (contains the zero vector).
  2. If $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in H$, then $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in H$ (closed under addition).
  3. If $\mathbf{u} \in H$ and $c \in \mathbb{R}$, then $c\mathbf{u} \in H$ (closed under scalar multiplication).
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="Compact Subspace Test">
  Conditions (2) and (3) can be combined: $H$ is a subspace if and only if $H$ is nonempty and $c_1\mathbf{u} + c_2\mathbf{v} \in H$ for all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in H$ and all $c_1, c_2 \in \mathbb{R}$. Condition (1) then follows by taking $c_1 = c_2 = 0$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Subspace Iff Closed Under + and Scalar Mult">
  A nonempty subset $H \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is a subspace if and only if for all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in H$ and all $c \in \mathbb{R}$:
  1. $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in H$, and
  2. $c\mathbf{u} \in H$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** ($\Rightarrow$) This is immediate from the definition.

($\Leftarrow$) We must show $\mathbf{0} \in H$. Since $H$ is nonempty, there exists some $\mathbf{u} \in H$. By closure under scalar multiplication, $0 \cdot \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0} \in H$. $\blacksquare$

### Examples and Non-Examples

**Examples of subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^3$:**
- $\{\mathbf{0}\}$ (the trivial subspace).
- Any line through the origin: $\{t\mathbf{v} : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$ for a fixed nonzero $\mathbf{v}$.
- Any plane through the origin: $\{s\mathbf{u} + t\mathbf{v} : s, t \in \mathbb{R}\}$ for non-parallel $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}$.
- $\mathbb{R}^3$ itself.

**Non-examples:**
- A line not through the origin: e.g., $\{(t, t+1, 0)^T : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$ does not contain $\mathbf{0}$ (when $t = 0$, the vector is $(0,1,0)^T \neq \mathbf{0}$).
- The first quadrant $\{(x,y) : x \geq 0, y \geq 0\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$: not closed under scalar multiplication (multiply by $-1$).

<Problem title="Counterexample: A Set That Fails One Closure Property">
  Show that $S = \{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : xy \geq 0\}$ is not a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^2$, even though it contains $\mathbf{0}$ and is closed under scalar multiplication.
<Solution>
  First, note $\mathbf{0} = (0,0) \in S$ since $0 \cdot 0 = 0 \geq 0$.
  **Closure under scalar multiplication:** If $(x,y) \in S$, then $xy \geq 0$. For any $c \in \mathbb{R}$, we have $(cx)(cy) = c^2(xy) \geq 0$ (since $c^2 \geq 0$ and $xy \geq 0$). So $c(x,y) = (cx, cy) \in S$.
  **Failure of closure under addition:** Take $\mathbf{u} = (1, -1)$ and $\mathbf{v} = (-1, 1)$. Then $\mathbf{u} \in S$ since $1 \cdot (-1) = -1$... wait, $-1 \not\geq 0$, so $\mathbf{u} \notin S$.
  Let me reconsider. $S$ consists of points in the first and third quadrants (plus axes). Take $\mathbf{u} = (1, 0) \in S$ (since $1 \cdot 0 = 0 \geq 0$) and $\mathbf{v} = (0, -1) \in S$ (since $0 \cdot (-1) = 0 \geq 0$). Wait, these are on the axes and their sum $(1, -1)$ has $1 \cdot (-1) = -1 < 0$, so $(1, -1) \notin S$.
  Therefore $S$ is not closed under addition, so $S$ is not a subspace. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

## Spans Are Subspaces

<Callout type="theorem" title="Span Is a Subspace">
  For any vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the set $\text{Span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p\}$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $H = \text{Span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p\}$.

**Zero vector:** $\mathbf{0} = 0\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + 0\mathbf{v}_p \in H$.

**Closed under addition:** Let $\mathbf{u} = a_1\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + a_p\mathbf{v}_p$ and $\mathbf{w} = b_1\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + b_p\mathbf{v}_p$ be in $H$. Then

$$\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w} = (a_1 + b_1)\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + (a_p + b_p)\mathbf{v}_p \in H.$$

**Closed under scalar multiplication:** For $c \in \mathbb{R}$,

$$c\mathbf{u} = (ca_1)\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + (ca_p)\mathbf{v}_p \in H.$$

Hence $H$ is a subspace. $\blacksquare$

In fact, $\text{Span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p\}$ is the **smallest** subspace containing $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_p$: it is contained in every subspace that contains those vectors.

## Column Space and Null Space

Two subspaces arise naturally from any matrix $A$.

<Callout type="definition" title="Column Space">
  The **column space** of an $m \times n$ matrix $A$ is
  $$\text{Col}(A) = \text{Span}\{\mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_n\} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m,$$
  where $\mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_n$ are the columns of $A$. Equivalently,
  $$\text{Col}(A) = \{A\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n\} = \{\text{all } \mathbf{b} \text{ such that } A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \text{ is consistent}\}.$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="definition" title="Null Space">
  The **null space** of an $m \times n$ matrix $A$ is
  $$\text{Nul}(A) = \{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n : A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}\}.$$
  That is, the null space is the solution set of the homogeneous system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Null Space Is a Subspace">
  For any $m \times n$ matrix $A$, $\text{Nul}(A)$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** We verify the three subspace properties.

**Zero vector:** $A\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$, so $\mathbf{0} \in \text{Nul}(A)$.

**Closed under addition:** If $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \text{Nul}(A)$, then $A(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = A\mathbf{u} + A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$, so $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in \text{Nul}(A)$.

**Closed under scalar multiplication:** If $\mathbf{u} \in \text{Nul}(A)$ and $c \in \mathbb{R}$, then $A(c\mathbf{u}) = c(A\mathbf{u}) = c\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$, so $c\mathbf{u} \in \text{Nul}(A)$.

Hence $\text{Nul}(A)$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Col(A) Lives in R^m, Nul(A) Lives in R^n">
  For an $m \times n$ matrix $A$: $\text{Col}(A) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$ (it is a set of outputs) and $\text{Nul}(A) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ (it is a set of inputs). Don't confuse the ambient spaces.
</Callout>
