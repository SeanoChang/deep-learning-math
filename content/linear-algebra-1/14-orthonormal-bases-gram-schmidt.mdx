# Orthonormal Bases, Gram-Schmidt, and Projections

In the previous lesson we introduced inner products and orthogonality. Now we build the computational
machinery: Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization produces orthonormal bases from arbitrary ones, orthogonal
projections decompose vectors optimally, and the normal equations solve the ubiquitous least squares
problem. These tools appear throughout applied mathematics, statistics, signal processing, and machine learning.

## Orthonormal Sets and Bases

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Orthonormal set).** A set $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$ in an inner product space $V$ is
  **orthonormal** if
  $$\langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \delta_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } i = j \\ 0 & \text{if } i \neq j. \end{cases}$$
  An orthonormal set that is also a basis for $V$ is an **orthonormal basis** (ONB).
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Proposition (Coordinates in an ONB).** If $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ is an orthonormal basis for $V$,
  then every $v \in V$ can be written as
  $$v = \sum_{i=1}^n \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i.$$
  The coefficients are simply $c_i = \langle v, e_i \rangle$.
  *Proof.* Since $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ is a basis, write $v = \sum c_i e_i$. Take the inner product with $e_j$:
  $$\langle v, e_j \rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \delta_{ij} = c_j. \quad \blacksquare$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Why orthonormal bases are superior.** In a general basis, finding coordinates requires solving a linear
  system. In an orthonormal basis, the coordinates are given by inner products -- no matrix inversion needed.
  This is why ONBs are preferred in applications: Fourier series, wavelet decompositions, PCA, and QR
  factorization all rely on orthonormality.
</Callout>

## Parseval's Identity and Bessel's Inequality

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Parseval's identity).** If $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ is an orthonormal basis for $V$, then for all $v \in V$:
  $$\|v\|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n |\langle v, e_i \rangle|^2.$$
  *Proof.* $\|v\|^2 = \langle v, v \rangle = \left\langle \sum_i \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i, \sum_j \langle v, e_j \rangle e_j \right\rangle = \sum_{i,j} \langle v, e_i \rangle \overline{\langle v, e_j \rangle} \langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \sum_i |\langle v, e_i \rangle|^2$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Bessel's inequality).** If $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$ is an orthonormal set (not necessarily a basis)
  in $V$, then for all $v \in V$:
  $$\sum_{i=1}^k |\langle v, e_i \rangle|^2 \leq \|v\|^2.$$
  Equality holds if and only if $v \in \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$.
  *Proof.* Define $w = v - \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i$. Then $w \perp e_j$ for each $j$ (as
  verified in the orthogonal complement proof). By the Pythagorean theorem:
  $$\|v\|^2 = \left\|\sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i\right\|^2 + \|w\|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^k |\langle v, e_i \rangle|^2 + \|w\|^2 \geq \sum_{i=1}^k |\langle v, e_i \rangle|^2.$$
  Equality holds iff $w = 0$ iff $v = \sum \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i \in \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## The Gram-Schmidt Process

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization).** Let $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ be a linearly independent set
  in an inner product space $V$. There exists an orthonormal set $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$ such that
  $$\operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_j\} = \operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_j\} \quad \text{for each } j = 1, \ldots, k.$$
  The process is constructive:
  **Step 1.** $\tilde{e}_1 = v_1$, then $e_1 = \frac{\tilde{e}_1}{\|\tilde{e}_1\|}$.
  **Step $j$ (for $j = 2, \ldots, k$).** Subtract the projection of $v_j$ onto the previously constructed
  orthonormal vectors:
  $$\tilde{e}_j = v_j - \sum_{i=1}^{j-1} \langle v_j, e_i \rangle e_i, \qquad e_j = \frac{\tilde{e}_j}{\|\tilde{e}_j\|}.$$
  *Proof.* By induction on $j$. The key facts at each step:
  1. $\tilde{e}_j \neq 0$ because $v_j \notin \operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_{j-1}\}$ (linear independence),
   and $\operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_{j-1}\} = \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_{j-1}\}$ by induction.
  2. $\tilde{e}_j \perp e_i$ for $i < j$: $\langle \tilde{e}_j, e_i \rangle = \langle v_j, e_i \rangle - \langle v_j, e_i \rangle = 0$.
  3. $\operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_j\} = \operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_j\}$: since $e_j$ is a
   linear combination of $v_j$ and $e_1, \ldots, e_{j-1}$ (which by induction are in $\operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_{j-1}\}$),
   we have $e_j \in \operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_j\}$. Conversely, $v_j = \tilde{e}_j + \sum \langle v_j, e_i \rangle e_i \in \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_j\}$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Corollary (Existence of ONB).** Every finite-dimensional inner product space has an orthonormal basis.
  *Proof.* Start with any basis (which exists since $V$ is finite-dimensional). Apply Gram-Schmidt. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## Orthogonal Projection

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Orthogonal projection).** Let $U$ be a subspace of an inner product space $V$ with
  $V = U \oplus U^\perp$. The **orthogonal projection** of $v$ onto $U$ is the unique $\hat{v} \in U$
  such that $v - \hat{v} \in U^\perp$. We write $\hat{v} = \operatorname{proj}_U(v)$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Projection formula).** If $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$ is an orthonormal basis for $U$, then
  $$\operatorname{proj}_U(v) = \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i.$$
  *Proof.* Let $\hat{v} = \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i \in U$. Then for any $e_j$ ($j \leq k$):
  $$\langle v - \hat{v}, e_j \rangle = \langle v, e_j \rangle - \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle \langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \langle v, e_j \rangle - \langle v, e_j \rangle = 0.$$
  So $v - \hat{v} \perp e_j$ for all $j$, hence $v - \hat{v} \perp U$. Since the decomposition $V = U \oplus U^\perp$
  is unique, $\hat{v} = \operatorname{proj}_U(v)$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Best approximation / closest point).** The orthogonal projection $\hat{v} = \operatorname{proj}_U(v)$
  is the unique closest point to $v$ in $U$:
  $$\|v - \hat{v}\| < \|v - u\| \quad \text{for all } u \in U, \; u \neq \hat{v}.$$
  *Proof.* For any $u \in U$, write $v - u = (v - \hat{v}) + (\hat{v} - u)$. Since $v - \hat{v} \in U^\perp$
  and $\hat{v} - u \in U$, these are orthogonal. By the Pythagorean theorem:
  $$\|v - u\|^2 = \|v - \hat{v}\|^2 + \|\hat{v} - u\|^2 \geq \|v - \hat{v}\|^2$$
  with equality iff $\hat{v} = u$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## Least Squares and the Normal Equations

The least squares problem is the most important application of orthogonal projection. Given an
$m \times n$ matrix $A$ (with $m > n$, typically) and $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$, the system $Ax = b$ is
generally inconsistent. The least squares solution minimizes $\|Ax - b\|^2$.

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Normal equations).** The vector $\hat{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ minimizes $\|Ax - b\|^2$ if and
  only if
  $$A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b.$$
  If $A$ has linearly independent columns (i.e., $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$), then $A^T A$ is invertible and
  the unique solution is $\hat{x} = (A^T A)^{-1} A^T b$.
  *Proof.* The vector $Ax$ lies in $\operatorname{Col}(A)$ for any $x$. We want the $Ax$ closest to $b$,
  which is $\hat{b} = \operatorname{proj}_{\operatorname{Col}(A)}(b)$. By the projection theorem, $b - A\hat{x} \perp \operatorname{Col}(A)$, meaning
  $$\langle A^T_j, b - A\hat{x} \rangle = 0 \quad \text{for each column } A_j \text{ of } A.$$
  In matrix form: $A^T(b - A\hat{x}) = 0$, i.e., $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$.
  If $A$ has linearly independent columns, then $A^T A$ is a positive definite $n \times n$ matrix (for $x \neq 0$:
  $x^T A^T A x = \|Ax\|^2 > 0$ since $Ax \neq 0$ by injectivity). Hence $A^T A$ is invertible. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Connection to the QR factorization.** If we apply Gram-Schmidt to the columns of $A$, we get
  $A = QR$ where $Q$ has orthonormal columns and $R$ is upper triangular. Then $A^T A = R^T Q^T Q R = R^T R$,
  and the normal equations become $R^T R \hat{x} = R^T Q^T b$, simplifying to $R\hat{x} = Q^T b$.
  This is more numerically stable than forming $A^T A$ directly.
</Callout>

## The QR Factorization

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (QR factorization).** Let $A$ be an $m \times n$ matrix with linearly independent columns.
  Then $A = QR$ where $Q$ is $m \times n$ with orthonormal columns and $R$ is $n \times n$ upper triangular
  with positive diagonal entries. This factorization is unique.
  *Proof.* Apply Gram-Schmidt to the columns $a_1, \ldots, a_n$ of $A$ to obtain orthonormal vectors
  $q_1, \ldots, q_n$. Since $\operatorname{span}\{q_1, \ldots, q_j\} = \operatorname{span}\{a_1, \ldots, a_j\}$,
  each $a_j$ is a linear combination of $q_1, \ldots, q_j$:
  $$a_j = \langle a_j, q_1 \rangle q_1 + \langle a_j, q_2 \rangle q_2 + \cdots + \langle a_j, q_j \rangle q_j.$$
  In matrix form: $A = QR$ where $Q = [q_1 \cdots q_n]$ and $R_{ij} = \langle a_j, q_i \rangle$ for $i \leq j$,
  $R_{ij} = 0$ for $i > j$. The diagonal entry $R_{jj} = \langle a_j, q_j \rangle = \|\tilde{e}_j\| > 0$
  (since $\tilde{e}_j \neq 0$ by linear independence). $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

# Gram-Schmidt from scratch
def gram_schmidt(V):
    """Apply Gram-Schmidt to columns of V. Returns Q (orthonormal columns)."""
    n = V.shape[1]
    Q = np.zeros_like(V, dtype=float)
    for j in range(n):
        q = V[:, j].astype(float).copy()
        for i in range(j):
            q -= np.dot(V[:, j], Q[:, i]) * Q[:, i]
        Q[:, j] = q / np.linalg.norm(q)
    return Q

<Problem>
  **Exercise 1 (Gram-Schmidt by hand).** Apply the Gram-Schmidt process to the vectors
  $v_1 = (1, 1, 0)^T$, $v_2 = (1, 0, 1)^T$, $v_3 = (0, 1, 1)^T$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with the standard
  inner product. Show each step explicitly.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **Step 1.** $\tilde{e}_1 = v_1 = (1, 1, 0)^T$. $\|\tilde{e}_1\| = \sqrt{1+1+0} = \sqrt{2}$.
  $$e_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1, 0)^T.$$
  **Step 2.** Subtract the projection of $v_2$ onto $e_1$:
  $$\langle v_2, e_1 \rangle = (1)(1/\sqrt{2}) + (0)(1/\sqrt{2}) + (1)(0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}.$$
  $$\tilde{e}_2 = v_2 - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} e_1 = (1, 0, 1)^T - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1, 0)^T = (1, 0, 1)^T - \frac{1}{2}(1, 1, 0)^T = \left(\frac{1}{2}, -\frac{1}{2}, 1\right)^T.$$
  $\|\tilde{e}_2\| = \sqrt{1/4 + 1/4 + 1} = \sqrt{3/2}$.
  $$e_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3/2}}\left(\frac{1}{2}, -\frac{1}{2}, 1\right)^T = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(1, -1, 2)^T.$$
  **Step 3.** Subtract projections of $v_3$ onto $e_1$ and $e_2$:
  $$\langle v_3, e_1 \rangle = (0)(1/\sqrt{2}) + (1)(1/\sqrt{2}) + (1)(0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}.$$
  $$\langle v_3, e_2 \rangle = (0)(1/\sqrt{6}) + (1)(-1/\sqrt{6}) + (1)(2/\sqrt{6}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}.$$
  $$\tilde{e}_3 = v_3 - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} e_1 - \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} e_2 = (0, 1, 1)^T - \frac{1}{2}(1, 1, 0)^T - \frac{1}{6}(1, -1, 2)^T.$$
  $$= \left(-\frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{6}, \; 1 - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{6}, \; 1 - \frac{1}{3}\right)^T = \left(-\frac{2}{3}, \frac{2}{3}, \frac{2}{3}\right)^T.$$
  $\|\tilde{e}_3\| = \frac{2}{3}\sqrt{3}$.
  $$e_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(-1, 1, 1)^T.$$
  The orthonormal basis is $\left\{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1,0)^T, \; \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(1,-1,2)^T, \; \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(-1,1,1)^T\right\}$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
  **Exercise 2 (Projection theorem proof).** Prove that if $U$ is a subspace of a finite-dimensional
  inner product space $V$ and $v \in V$, then the orthogonal projection $\hat{v} = \operatorname{proj}_U(v)$
  minimizes $\|v - u\|$ over all $u \in U$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  *Proof.* Let $\hat{v} = \operatorname{proj}_U(v)$, so $\hat{v} \in U$ and $v - \hat{v} \in U^\perp$.
  For any $u \in U$, write
  $$v - u = (v - \hat{v}) + (\hat{v} - u).$$
  Now $v - \hat{v} \in U^\perp$ and $\hat{v} - u \in U$, so these two vectors are orthogonal. By the
  Pythagorean theorem:
  $$\|v - u\|^2 = \|v - \hat{v}\|^2 + \|\hat{v} - u\|^2.$$
  Since $\|\hat{v} - u\|^2 \geq 0$:
  $$\|v - u\|^2 \geq \|v - \hat{v}\|^2$$
  with equality iff $\hat{v} - u = 0$, i.e., $u = \hat{v}$.
  Therefore $\hat{v}$ is the unique minimizer of $\|v - u\|$ over $u \in U$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
  **Exercise 3 (Least squares application).** A set of data points is collected:
  $(0, 2)$, $(1, 3)$, $(2, 7)$, $(3, 8)$.
  (a) Set up the least squares problem to fit a line $y = c_0 + c_1 x$.
  (b) Write the normal equations $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$ explicitly.
  (c) Solve for $c_0$ and $c_1$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** The design matrix and right-hand side are:
  $$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 7 \\ 8 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \hat{x} = \begin{pmatrix} c_0 \\ c_1 \end{pmatrix}.$$
  **(b)** Compute:
  $$A^T A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad A^T b = \begin{pmatrix} 20 \\ 41 \end{pmatrix}.$$
  The normal equations are:
  $$\begin{pmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c_0 \\ c_1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 20 \\ 41 \end{pmatrix}.$$
  **(c)** From the first equation: $4c_0 + 6c_1 = 20$. From the second: $6c_0 + 14c_1 = 41$.
  Multiply the first by $3/2$: $6c_0 + 9c_1 = 30$. Subtract: $5c_1 = 11$, so $c_1 = 11/5 = 2.2$.
  Then $c_0 = (20 - 6(2.2))/4 = (20 - 13.2)/4 = 6.8/4 = 1.7$.
  The best-fit line is $y = 1.7 + 2.2x$. $\square$
</Solution>
