# Diagonalization and When It Is Possible

Diagonalization is the central structural question of elementary spectral theory: given a linear operator
$T \colon V \to V$ on a finite-dimensional vector space, can we choose a basis for $V$ in which the matrix
of $T$ is diagonal? When such a basis exists, the operator decomposes into independent scalings along
each basis direction, reducing virtually every computation -- powers, exponentials, systems of ODEs --
to arithmetic on eigenvalues. In this lesson we develop the precise criterion for diagonalizability,
prove that distinct eigenvalues suffice, and analyze the failure modes.

## Recall: Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

We assume from the previous lesson the definitions of eigenvalue, eigenvector, eigenspace, and
the characteristic polynomial. Let us fix notation.

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Eigenspace).** Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$
  over a field $\mathbb{F}$. For $\lambda \in \mathbb{F}$, the **eigenspace** of $T$ corresponding to $\lambda$ is
  $$E_\lambda = \ker(T - \lambda I) = \{v \in V : Tv = \lambda v\}.$$
  This is a subspace of $V$. The eigenvalue $\lambda$ exists if and only if $E_\lambda \neq \{0\}$.
</Callout>

## What Does Diagonalizable Mean?

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Diagonalizable operator).** A linear operator $T \colon V \to V$ is **diagonalizable** if there
  exists a basis $\mathcal{B} = \{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ of $V$ consisting entirely of eigenvectors of $T$.
  Such a basis is called an **eigenbasis** for $T$.
  Equivalently, an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is **diagonalizable** if there exists an invertible matrix $P$ such that
  $$P^{-1}AP = D$$
  where $D = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$ is diagonal. The columns of $P$ are the eigenvectors
  forming the eigenbasis.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Why diagonalization matters.** If $A = PDP^{-1}$, then $A^k = PD^kP^{-1}$, and $D^k = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1^k, \ldots, \lambda_n^k)$.
  Computing the $k$-th power of a diagonal matrix is trivial. This makes diagonalization indispensable for
  solving linear recurrences, systems of differential equations, and understanding long-term behavior of
  dynamical systems.
</Callout>

## Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity

The interplay between two notions of multiplicity governs diagonalizability.

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Algebraic multiplicity).** Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ matrix with characteristic polynomial
  $p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$. If $\lambda_0$ is a root of $p$, its **algebraic multiplicity**
  $\operatorname{am}(\lambda_0)$ is its multiplicity as a root of $p(\lambda)$.
  That is, if $p(\lambda) = (\lambda_0 - \lambda)^m q(\lambda)$ where $q(\lambda_0) \neq 0$, then
  $\operatorname{am}(\lambda_0) = m$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="definition">
  **Definition (Geometric multiplicity).** The **geometric multiplicity** of $\lambda_0$ is
  $$\operatorname{gm}(\lambda_0) = \dim E_{\lambda_0} = \dim \ker(A - \lambda_0 I).$$
  This counts the maximum number of linearly independent eigenvectors for $\lambda_0$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Lemma (Geometric $\leq$ Algebraic).** For every eigenvalue $\lambda_0$ of $A$,
  $$1 \leq \operatorname{gm}(\lambda_0) \leq \operatorname{am}(\lambda_0).$$
  *Proof.* The lower bound $\operatorname{gm}(\lambda_0) \geq 1$ holds because $\lambda_0$ is an eigenvalue, so
  $E_{\lambda_0} \neq \{0\}$.
  For the upper bound, let $g = \operatorname{gm}(\lambda_0)$ and choose a basis $\{v_1, \ldots, v_g\}$
  of $E_{\lambda_0}$. Extend to a basis $\{v_1, \ldots, v_g, v_{g+1}, \ldots, v_n\}$ of $V$. In this
  basis, the matrix of $T$ has the block form
  $$[T]_\mathcal{B} = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_0 I_g & B \\ 0 & C \end{pmatrix}$$
  where $I_g$ is the $g \times g$ identity. The characteristic polynomial is
  $$\det([T]_\mathcal{B} - \lambda I) = (\lambda_0 - \lambda)^g \det(C - \lambda I).$$
  Hence $(\lambda_0 - \lambda)^g$ divides the characteristic polynomial, so $\operatorname{am}(\lambda_0) \geq g$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## The Diagonalization Criterion

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Eigenbasis criterion).** Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear operator on an $n$-dimensional
  vector space. The following are equivalent:
  1. $T$ is diagonalizable.
  2. $V$ has a basis of eigenvectors of $T$.
  3. $V = E_{\lambda_1} \oplus E_{\lambda_2} \oplus \cdots \oplus E_{\lambda_k}$, where $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$
   are the distinct eigenvalues of $T$.
  4. $\sum_{i=1}^{k} \dim E_{\lambda_i} = n$, i.e., $\sum_{i=1}^{k} \operatorname{gm}(\lambda_i) = n$.
  5. The characteristic polynomial splits completely over $\mathbb{F}$, and $\operatorname{gm}(\lambda_i) = \operatorname{am}(\lambda_i)$
   for every eigenvalue $\lambda_i$.
  *Proof.* $(1) \Leftrightarrow (2)$ is the definition.
  $(2) \Rightarrow (3)$: If $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ is an eigenbasis, group the basis vectors by eigenvalue.
  Each group spans a subspace of the corresponding eigenspace. Since the full set is a basis for $V$,
  and eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent (proved below), we get the direct sum.
  $(3) \Rightarrow (4)$: The dimension of a direct sum equals the sum of dimensions.
  $(4) \Rightarrow (2)$: Choose a basis for each $E_{\lambda_i}$. By linear independence of eigenvectors
  for distinct eigenvalues, their union is linearly independent. Condition (4) says this union has
  $n$ vectors, so it is a basis for $V$.
  $(4) \Leftrightarrow (5)$: Since $\sum \operatorname{am}(\lambda_i) = n$ (when the characteristic polynomial splits)
  and $\operatorname{gm}(\lambda_i) \leq \operatorname{am}(\lambda_i)$, the sum $\sum \operatorname{gm}(\lambda_i) = n$
  if and only if equality holds for each $i$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## Distinct Eigenvalues Suffice

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Theorem (Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are independent).** Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear
  operator, and let $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ be eigenvectors for *distinct* eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$.
  Then $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ is linearly independent.
  *Proof.* By strong induction on $k$. The base case $k = 1$ is immediate: a single eigenvector is nonzero
  by definition, hence linearly independent.
  Suppose the result holds for any set of fewer than $k$ eigenvectors with distinct eigenvalues.
  Assume for contradiction that
  $$c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 + \cdots + c_k v_k = 0 \tag{$*$}$$
  is a nontrivial linear combination. Apply $T$ to both sides:
  $$c_1 \lambda_1 v_1 + c_2 \lambda_2 v_2 + \cdots + c_k \lambda_k v_k = 0. \tag{$**$}$$
  Multiply $(*)$ by $\lambda_k$ and subtract from $(**)$:
  $$c_1(\lambda_1 - \lambda_k) v_1 + c_2(\lambda_2 - \lambda_k) v_2 + \cdots + c_{k-1}(\lambda_{k-1} - \lambda_k) v_{k-1} = 0.$$
  By the inductive hypothesis (applied to $v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1}$ with distinct eigenvalues), we conclude
  $c_i(\lambda_i - \lambda_k) = 0$ for $i = 1, \ldots, k-1$. Since $\lambda_i \neq \lambda_k$ for all $i < k$,
  we get $c_i = 0$ for $i = 1, \ldots, k-1$. Substituting back into $(*)$: $c_k v_k = 0$, and $v_k \neq 0$
  gives $c_k = 0$. Contradiction. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
  **Corollary (Distinct eigenvalues implies diagonalizable).** If $T \colon V \to V$ has $n = \dim V$
  distinct eigenvalues, then $T$ is diagonalizable.
  *Proof.* Choose one eigenvector for each eigenvalue. By the theorem above, these $n$ eigenvectors are
  linearly independent, hence form a basis of $V$. This is an eigenbasis. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
  **The converse is false.** The identity operator $I$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is diagonalizable (it is already
  diagonal), but it has only one eigenvalue ($\lambda = 1$) with algebraic and geometric multiplicity $n$.
  Distinct eigenvalues is sufficient but not necessary.
</Callout>

## Failure of Diagonalizability

When does diagonalization fail? The eigenbasis criterion tells us: it fails when some eigenvalue has
geometric multiplicity strictly less than its algebraic multiplicity. Let us see the prototypical example.

**Example.** Consider the matrix

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The characteristic polynomial is $p(\lambda) = (2 - \lambda)^2$, so $\lambda = 2$ is the only eigenvalue
with $\operatorname{am}(2) = 2$.

The eigenspace: $A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, so $\ker(A - 2I) = \operatorname{span}\{e_1\}$,
giving $\operatorname{gm}(2) = 1 < 2 = \operatorname{am}(2)$.

Since we can only find one linearly independent eigenvector in a 2-dimensional space, $A$ is not diagonalizable.
This matrix is in **Jordan normal form** -- the off-diagonal 1 (the Jordan block) is the obstruction.

**Why does this happen geometrically?** The operator $A$ acts on $\mathbb{R}^2$ by scaling by 2 and also
"shearing" in the $e_1$ direction. The shear component cannot be eliminated by a change of basis; it
is an intrinsic feature of the operator. Diagonalizable operators correspond to pure scalings along
independent directions.

## The Diagonalization Algorithm

Given an $n \times n$ matrix $A$:

1. Compute the characteristic polynomial $p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$.
2. Find all roots $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$ and their algebraic multiplicities.
3. For each $\lambda_i$, compute $E_{\lambda_i} = \ker(A - \lambda_i I)$ and its dimension (geometric multiplicity).
4. Check: if $\sum \operatorname{gm}(\lambda_i) = n$, then $A$ is diagonalizable. The eigenbasis is the union of bases of the eigenspaces. Otherwise, $A$ is not diagonalizable.
5. Form $P$ with eigenvectors as columns and $D = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$.

  **Exercise 1 (Diagonalization criterion proof).** Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$.
  (a) Compute the characteristic polynomial and find all eigenvalues with their algebraic multiplicities.
  (b) Compute the eigenspace for each eigenvalue and its geometric multiplicity.
  (c) Determine whether $A$ is diagonalizable. Justify using the eigenbasis criterion.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** The characteristic polynomial is
  $$p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I) = \det\begin{pmatrix} 3 - \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & 3 - \lambda \end{pmatrix} = (3 - \lambda)^2.$$
  The only eigenvalue is $\lambda = 3$ with $\operatorname{am}(3) = 2$.
  **(b)** The eigenspace:
  $$A - 3I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.$$
  Solving $(A - 3I)x = 0$: the system gives $x_2 = 0$, $x_1$ free. So $E_3 = \operatorname{span}\{(1, 0)^T\}$
  and $\operatorname{gm}(3) = 1$.
  **(c)** Since $\operatorname{gm}(3) = 1 < 2 = \operatorname{am}(3)$, we have $\sum \operatorname{gm}(\lambda_i) = 1 < 2 = n$.
  By the eigenbasis criterion, $A$ is **not diagonalizable**. We cannot find a basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$
  consisting of eigenvectors of $A$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
  **Exercise 2 (Proof reconstruction).** Prove that if $A$ is an $n \times n$ matrix with $n$ distinct
  eigenvalues, then $A$ is diagonalizable. You must prove the key lemma: eigenvectors for distinct
  eigenvalues are linearly independent.
  *Hint:* Use induction on the number of eigenvectors. Apply $A$ to a dependence relation, then subtract
  a scaled copy to eliminate one vector.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **Lemma.** Let $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ be eigenvectors of $A$ for distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$.
  Then $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ is linearly independent.
  *Proof.* By induction on $k$.
  *Base case ($k = 1$):* A single eigenvector is nonzero, hence linearly independent.
  *Inductive step:* Assume the result for $k - 1$. Suppose $c_1 v_1 + \cdots + c_k v_k = 0$. Apply $A$:
  $$c_1 \lambda_1 v_1 + \cdots + c_k \lambda_k v_k = 0.$$
  Subtract $\lambda_k$ times the original relation:
  $$c_1(\lambda_1 - \lambda_k)v_1 + \cdots + c_{k-1}(\lambda_{k-1} - \lambda_k)v_{k-1} = 0.$$
  By the inductive hypothesis, $c_i(\lambda_i - \lambda_k) = 0$ for $i = 1, \ldots, k-1$. Since all
  eigenvalues are distinct, $\lambda_i - \lambda_k \neq 0$, so $c_i = 0$ for all $i < k$. Then the
  original relation gives $c_k v_k = 0$, hence $c_k = 0$.
  **Corollary.** If $A$ has $n$ distinct eigenvalues, pick one eigenvector for each. The $n$ eigenvectors
  are linearly independent by the lemma, hence form a basis. This basis is an eigenbasis, so $A$ is
  diagonalizable. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
  **Exercise 3 (Counterexample analysis).** Consider the family of matrices
  $$A_t = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & t \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
  parametrized by $t \in \mathbb{R}$.
  (a) For which values of $t$ is $A_t$ diagonalizable?
  (b) Interpret geometrically: what does $A_t$ do to vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$?
  (c) Compute $A_t^k$ for the non-diagonalizable case and explain why diagonalization would have
  simplified this computation.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** The characteristic polynomial is $(\lambda - 1)^2$ for all $t$. The eigenspace is $\ker(A_t - I) = \ker\begin{pmatrix} 0 & t \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.
  - If $t = 0$: $A_0 = I$, and $E_1 = \mathbb{R}^2$, so $\operatorname{gm}(1) = 2 = \operatorname{am}(1)$. Diagonalizable.
  - If $t \neq 0$: $E_1 = \operatorname{span}\{e_1\}$, so $\operatorname{gm}(1) = 1 < 2$. Not diagonalizable.
  **(b)** $A_t$ acts as a **horizontal shear** by factor $t$: it maps $(x, y)^T \mapsto (x + ty, y)^T$.
  The $y$-coordinate is preserved while the $x$-coordinate is shifted proportionally to $y$. This is a
  shearing transformation that cannot be decomposed into independent scalings (unless $t = 0$).
  **(c)** For $t \neq 0$, write $A_t = I + N$ where $N = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & t \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ is nilpotent
  ($N^2 = 0$). By the binomial theorem:
  $$A_t^k = (I + N)^k = \sum_{j=0}^{k} \binom{k}{j} N^j = I + kN = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & kt \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
  since $N^j = 0$ for $j \geq 2$. If $A_t$ were diagonalizable as $PDP^{-1}$, then $A_t^k = PD^kP^{-1}$
  and computing $D^k$ would be trivial. The non-diagonalizable case required the nilpotent decomposition
  instead. $\square$
</Solution>
