## Rank and Nullity: Formal Definitions

Having developed the concepts of basis and dimension, we can now give precise measures of the "size" of the column space and null space.

<Callout type="definition" title="Rank and Nullity">
  Let $A$ be an $m \times n$ matrix.
  - The **rank** of $A$ is $\text{rank}(A) = \dim(\text{Col}(A))$, the number of pivot columns of $A$.
  - The **nullity** of $A$ is $\text{nullity}(A) = \dim(\text{Nul}(A))$, the number of free variables in $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="Rank from RREF">
  To compute $\text{rank}(A)$: row reduce $A$ and count pivot columns. To compute $\text{nullity}(A)$: count non-pivot columns (free variables). Note that $\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n$ (the number of columns). This is not a coincidence -- it is the Rank-Nullity Theorem.
</Callout>

## The Column Space Revisited

<Callout type="theorem" title="Pivot Columns of A Form a Basis for Col(A)">
  The pivot columns of $A$ (the original columns of $A$ corresponding to pivot positions in the RREF) form a basis for $\text{Col}(A)$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $A = [\mathbf{a}_1 \; \cdots \; \mathbf{a}_n]$ and let $R$ be the RREF of $A$. Suppose the pivot columns are at positions $j_1 < j_2 < \cdots < j_r$.

**Spanning:** Any column $\mathbf{a}_k$ of $A$ can be written as a linear combination of the pivot columns. This follows from the RREF: in $R$, column $k$ is a specific linear combination of the pivot columns (columns $j_1, \ldots, j_r$ of $R$ are $\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_r$). Since row operations preserve linear dependency relations among columns, the same combination expresses $\mathbf{a}_k$ in terms of $\mathbf{a}_{j_1}, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_{j_r}$. Hence $\text{Col}(A) = \text{Span}\{\mathbf{a}_{j_1}, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_{j_r}\}$.

**Independence:** Suppose $c_1\mathbf{a}_{j_1} + \cdots + c_r\mathbf{a}_{j_r} = \mathbf{0}$. The same relation holds in $R$: $c_1\mathbf{e}_1 + \cdots + c_r\mathbf{e}_r = \mathbf{0}$, which immediately gives $c_1 = \cdots = c_r = 0$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Warning: Use Original Columns, Not RREF Columns">
  The pivot columns **of the original matrix $A$** form a basis for $\text{Col}(A)$. The columns of the RREF are generally different vectors and span a different subspace (unless $A$ happens to already be in RREF). The RREF tells you *which* columns to pick, but you pick them from $A$.
</Callout>

## The Null Space Revisited

<Callout type="theorem" title="Null Space Is a Subspace">
  For any $m \times n$ matrix $A$, $\text{Nul}(A)$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$.
</Callout>

We proved this in Lesson 2. The new insight from Lesson 3 is that we can compute a basis for $\text{Nul}(A)$ explicitly.

**Algorithm:** Row reduce $[A \mid \mathbf{0}]$ to RREF. Express each basic variable in terms of the free variables. Set one free variable to $1$ and the rest to $0$, cycling through each free variable. The resulting vectors form a basis for $\text{Nul}(A)$.

### Example

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 & 5 & 7 \\ 2 & 6 & 10 & 14 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$.

Row reducing:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 & 5 & 7 \\ 2 & 6 & 10 & 14 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 & 5 & 7 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & -1 & -2 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Pivots in columns 1, 2. Free variables: $x_3, x_4$.
- $x_1 = x_3 + 2x_4$
- $x_2 = -2x_3 - 3x_4$

Setting $(x_3, x_4) = (1, 0)$: $\mathbf{v}_1 = (1, -2, 1, 0)^T$.

Setting $(x_3, x_4) = (0, 1)$: $\mathbf{v}_2 = (2, -3, 0, 1)^T$.

Basis for $\text{Nul}(A)$: $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2\}$. Nullity $= 2$.

## The Rank-Nullity Theorem

<Callout type="theorem" title="Rank-Nullity Theorem (Dimension Theorem)">
  If $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix, then
  $$\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n.$$
  Equivalently, $\dim(\text{Col}(A)) + \dim(\text{Nul}(A)) = n$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $r = \text{rank}(A)$, which is the number of pivot columns. The remaining $n - r$ columns are non-pivot columns, each corresponding to a free variable. By the null space algorithm, the standard basis for $\text{Nul}(A)$ has exactly $n - r$ vectors (one for each free variable). Therefore $\text{nullity}(A) = n - r$, and

$$\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = r + (n - r) = n. \quad \blacksquare$$

This theorem is one of the most important results in linear algebra. It says that the $n$ columns of $A$ are partitioned into two roles: some contribute to the column space (the rank counts them), and the rest contribute to the null space (the nullity counts them). The total is always $n$.

<Problem title="Theorem Application: Using Rank-Nullity">
  Let $A$ be a $5 \times 8$ matrix with $\text{nullity}(A) = 3$. Determine $\text{rank}(A)$, the number of pivot positions, and whether $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is consistent for every $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^5$.
<Solution>
  By the Rank-Nullity Theorem: $\text{rank}(A) + 3 = 8$, so $\text{rank}(A) = 5$.
  The number of pivot positions equals $\text{rank}(A) = 5$. Since $A$ has $5$ rows and $5$ pivots, every row contains a pivot.
  When every row has a pivot, $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is consistent for every $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^5$, because the augmented matrix $[A \mid \mathbf{b}]$ can never produce a row of the form $(0 \; 0 \; \cdots \; 0 \mid c)$ with $c \neq 0$.
  So yes, $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is consistent for every $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^5$.
</Solution>
</Problem>

## Applications to Linear Systems

The Rank-Nullity Theorem provides a powerful framework for understanding when linear systems have solutions and when those solutions are unique.

<Callout type="theorem" title="Consistency and Uniqueness via Rank">
  Let $A$ be an $m \times n$ matrix with $\text{rank}(A) = r$.
  1. **Consistency for all $\mathbf{b}$:** $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is consistent for every $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ if and only if $r = m$ (every row has a pivot, i.e., $\text{Col}(A) = \mathbb{R}^m$).
  2. **Unique solutions:** When $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is consistent, the solution is unique if and only if $r = n$ (every column is a pivot column, i.e., $\text{Nul}(A) = \{\mathbf{0}\}$).
  3. **Invertible case:** If $m = n$, both conditions hold simultaneously ($r = m = n$) if and only if $A$ is invertible.
</Callout>

**Proof.** (1) $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is consistent for all $\mathbf{b}$ iff $\text{Col}(A) = \mathbb{R}^m$ iff $\dim(\text{Col}(A)) = m$ iff $r = m$.

(2) The general solution is $\mathbf{x}_p + \text{Nul}(A)$. This is a single point iff $\text{Nul}(A) = \{\mathbf{0}\}$ iff $\text{nullity}(A) = 0$ iff $r = n$.

(3) If $m = n$, then $r = m = n$ combines both conditions. Such a matrix has $n$ pivots in $n$ rows and $n$ columns, so the RREF is $I_n$. The matrix is invertible with $A^{-1}$ obtainable via row reducing $[A \mid I_n]$. $\blacksquare$

## Row Space and the Full Picture

<Callout type="definition" title="Row Space">
  The **row space** of $A$ is the span of the rows of $A$, viewed as vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$:
  $$\text{Row}(A) = \text{Col}(A^T) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n.$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Rank Equals Row Rank">
  $\dim(\text{Row}(A)) = \dim(\text{Col}(A)) = \text{rank}(A)$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Row operations do not change the row space (each new row is a linear combination of the old rows, and the operation is reversible). In RREF, the nonzero rows are precisely the $r$ rows containing pivots. These rows are linearly independent (each has a leading $1$ in a position where all other rows have $0$). Hence $\dim(\text{Row}(A)) = r = \text{rank}(A)$.

Since $\text{Row}(A) = \text{Col}(A^T)$, we also have $\text{rank}(A^T) = \text{rank}(A)$. Row rank equals column rank. $\blacksquare$

This means there are four fundamental subspaces associated to an $m \times n$ matrix $A$ of rank $r$:

| Subspace | Ambient Space | Dimension |
|----------|--------------|-----------|
| $\text{Col}(A)$ | $\mathbb{R}^m$ | $r$ |
| $\text{Nul}(A)$ | $\mathbb{R}^n$ | $n - r$ |
| $\text{Row}(A)$ | $\mathbb{R}^n$ | $r$ |
| $\text{Nul}(A^T)$ (left null space) | $\mathbb{R}^m$ | $m - r$ |

<Problem title="Counterexample: Rank Does Not Determine the Matrix">
  Give two different $2 \times 3$ matrices $A$ and $B$ with $\text{rank}(A) = \text{rank}(B) = 1$ but $\text{Col}(A) \neq \text{Col}(B)$ and $\text{Nul}(A) \neq \text{Nul}(B)$.
<Solution>
  Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.
  Both have rank 1.
  $\text{Col}(A) = \text{Span}\{(1, 0)^T\}$ (the $x_1$-axis in $\mathbb{R}^2$).
  $\text{Col}(B) = \text{Span}\{(1, 0)^T\}$... wait, that is the same. Let me adjust.
  Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.
  $\text{Col}(A) = \text{Span}\{(1, 0)^T\}$ and $\text{Col}(B) = \text{Span}\{(0, 1)^T\}$. These are different subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^2$.
  $\text{Nul}(A) = \{(0, s, t)^T : s, t \in \mathbb{R}\}$ (free variables $x_2, x_3$).
  $\text{Nul}(B) = \{(0, s, t)^T : s, t \in \mathbb{R}\}$ (same). Hmm, same null space.
  Let me use $B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ instead.
  $\text{Col}(B) = \text{Span}\{(1, 0)^T\}$. Still the same column space as $A$ (just generated by a different column).
  OK, let me try $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.
  $\text{Col}(A) = \text{Span}\{(1, 0)^T\}$ and $\text{Col}(B) = \text{Span}\{(0, 1)^T\}$, so $\text{Col}(A) \neq \text{Col}(B)$.
  $\text{Nul}(A) = \{(0, s, t)^T\}$ and $\text{Nul}(B) = \{(s, 0, t)^T\}$, so $\text{Nul}(A) \neq \text{Nul}(B)$.
  Both have rank 1 and nullity 2, but the actual subspaces are different. This shows that rank/nullity are *dimensions* -- they capture size but not geometric position.
</Solution>
</Problem>
