# Continuity

Continuity is one of the most fundamental concepts in analysis. Intuitively, a function is continuous if small changes in the input produce small changes in the output. We now make this precise.

## Continuity at a Point

<Callout type="definition">
**Definition 7.1 (Continuity at a Point).** Let $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ and $f : A \to \mathbb{R}$. We say $f$ is **continuous at $c \in A$** if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that

$$|f(x) - f(c)| < \varepsilon \quad \text{whenever} \quad |x - c| < \delta \text{ and } x \in A.$$

If $f$ is continuous at every point of $A$, we say $f$ is **continuous on $A$**.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**Remark.** Compare with the limit definition: here we use $|x - c| < \delta$ (not $0 < |x - c| < \delta$), and we require $f(c)$ itself to be defined and equal to $L$. If $c$ is a limit point of $A$, then $f$ is continuous at $c$ if and only if $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)$. If $c$ is an isolated point of $A$, then $f$ is automatically continuous at $c$.
</Callout>

## Sequential Characterization

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 7.2 (Sequential Characterization of Continuity).** Let $f : A \to \mathbb{R}$ and $c \in A$. Then $f$ is continuous at $c$ if and only if for every sequence $(x_n)$ in $A$ with $x_n \to c$, we have $f(x_n) \to f(c)$.
</Callout>

*Proof.* If $c$ is an isolated point of $A$, then eventually $x_n = c$, so $f(x_n) = f(c) \to f(c)$, and both conditions hold trivially.

If $c$ is a limit point of $A$, then continuity at $c$ means $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)$. By the sequential criterion for function limits (Theorem 6.5), this is equivalent to: for every sequence $(x_n)$ in $A \setminus \{c\}$ with $x_n \to c$, $f(x_n) \to f(c)$. Since $f(c) \to f(c)$ trivially, we may include the constant sequence at $c$ as well, giving the stated equivalence. $\square$

## Algebra of Continuous Functions

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 7.3 (Algebra of Continuous Functions).** Let $f, g : A \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous at $c \in A$. Then:

1. $f + g$ is continuous at $c$.
2. $f \cdot g$ is continuous at $c$.
3. If $g(c) \neq 0$, then $f/g$ is continuous at $c$ (on a suitable domain).
4. $\alpha f$ is continuous at $c$ for every $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$.
</Callout>

*Proof of (2).* Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Since $f$ is continuous at $c$, it is bounded near $c$: there exists $\delta_1 > 0$ and $M > 0$ such that $|f(x)| \leq M$ for $|x - c| < \delta_1$, $x \in A$. Choose $\delta_2 > 0$ such that $|f(x) - f(c)| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2(|g(c)| + 1)}$ for $|x - c| < \delta_2$, and $\delta_3 > 0$ such that $|g(x) - g(c)| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2M}$ for $|x - c| < \delta_3$.

Set $\delta = \min(\delta_1, \delta_2, \delta_3)$. For $|x - c| < \delta$:

$$|f(x)g(x) - f(c)g(c)| \leq |f(x)||g(x) - g(c)| + |g(c)||f(x) - f(c)| < M \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2M} + |g(c)| \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2(|g(c)|+1)} < \varepsilon. \quad \square$$

## Composition of Continuous Functions

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 7.4 (Composition Theorem).** Let $f : A \to \mathbb{R}$ and $g : B \to \mathbb{R}$ with $f(A) \subseteq B$. If $f$ is continuous at $c$ and $g$ is continuous at $f(c)$, then $g \circ f$ is continuous at $c$.
</Callout>

*Proof.* Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Since $g$ is continuous at $f(c)$, there exists $\eta > 0$ such that

$$|y - f(c)| < \eta \implies |g(y) - g(f(c))| < \varepsilon.$$

Since $f$ is continuous at $c$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that

$$|x - c| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(c)| < \eta.$$

Combining: $|x - c| < \delta$ implies $|f(x) - f(c)| < \eta$, which implies $|g(f(x)) - g(f(c))| < \varepsilon$. $\square$

## Continuity of Polynomials and Rational Functions

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 7.5.** Every polynomial $p(x) = a_n x^n + \cdots + a_1 x + a_0$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$. Every rational function $p(x)/q(x)$ is continuous on its domain $\{x : q(x) \neq 0\}$.
</Callout>

*Proof.* The constant function $f(x) = a_0$ and the identity function $g(x) = x$ are continuous on $\mathbb{R}$ (direct ε-δ verification). By Theorem 7.3, products $x^k$ and scalar multiples $a_k x^k$ are continuous, and sums of continuous functions are continuous. Thus $p(x)$ is continuous.

For rational functions, $p/q$ is the quotient of two continuous functions, continuous wherever $q(x) \neq 0$ by Theorem 7.3(3). $\square$

## Equivalent Formulations

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem 7.6 (Topological Characterization).** Let $f : A \to \mathbb{R}$. The following are equivalent:

1. $f$ is continuous on $A$.
2. For every open set $V \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, the preimage $f^{-1}(V) = \{x \in A : f(x) \in V\}$ is open in $A$.
</Callout>

*Proof.*

$(1) \Rightarrow (2)$: Let $V$ be open in $\mathbb{R}$ and let $c \in f^{-1}(V)$. Then $f(c) \in V$, and since $V$ is open, there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ with $(f(c) - \varepsilon, f(c) + \varepsilon) \subseteq V$. By continuity, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|x - c| < \delta$, $x \in A$ implies $|f(x) - f(c)| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $f(x) \in V$. So $(c - \delta, c + \delta) \cap A \subseteq f^{-1}(V)$, showing $f^{-1}(V)$ is open in $A$.

$(2) \Rightarrow (1)$: Let $c \in A$ and $\varepsilon > 0$. The set $V = (f(c) - \varepsilon, f(c) + \varepsilon)$ is open. By (2), $f^{-1}(V)$ is open in $A$, so there exists $\delta > 0$ with $(c - \delta, c + \delta) \cap A \subseteq f^{-1}(V)$, meaning $|x - c| < \delta$, $x \in A$ implies $|f(x) - f(c)| < \varepsilon$. $\square$

## Exercises

<Problem>
**Exercise 7.1.** Prove that $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ is continuous on $[0, \infty)$.

*Hint:* For the case $c > 0$, use the identity $|\sqrt{x} - \sqrt{c}| = \frac{|x - c|}{\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{c}} \leq \frac{|x - c|}{\sqrt{c}}$. Treat $c = 0$ separately.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* **Case $c > 0$:** Let $\varepsilon > 0$. For $x \geq 0$:

$$|\sqrt{x} - \sqrt{c}| = \frac{|x - c|}{\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{c}} \leq \frac{|x - c|}{\sqrt{c}}.$$

Choose $\delta = \varepsilon \sqrt{c}$. Then $|x - c| < \delta$ implies $|\sqrt{x} - \sqrt{c}| \leq |x - c|/\sqrt{c} < \varepsilon$.

**Case $c = 0$:** We need $\sqrt{x} < \varepsilon$ for $x \in [0, \infty)$ with $|x| < \delta$. Choose $\delta = \varepsilon^2$. Then $0 \leq x < \delta = \varepsilon^2$ implies $\sqrt{x} < \varepsilon$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 7.2.** Prove that the composition of two continuous functions is continuous using the sequential characterization (Theorem 7.2), providing an alternative proof of Theorem 7.4.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* Let $f : A \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous at $c$ and $g : B \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous at $f(c)$, with $f(A) \subseteq B$. Let $(x_n)$ be a sequence in $A$ with $x_n \to c$.

Since $f$ is continuous at $c$ and $x_n \to c$, by the sequential characterization, $f(x_n) \to f(c)$.

Now $(f(x_n))$ is a sequence in $B$ (since $f(A) \subseteq B$) converging to $f(c)$. Since $g$ is continuous at $f(c)$, by the sequential characterization, $g(f(x_n)) \to g(f(c))$.

Thus $(g \circ f)(x_n) \to (g \circ f)(c)$ for every sequence $x_n \to c$, so $g \circ f$ is continuous at $c$ by the sequential characterization. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 7.3 (Counterexample).** Give an example of a function that is continuous on the open interval $(0, 1)$ but not uniformly continuous on $(0, 1)$. Prove both claims.

*Hint:* Consider $f(x) = \sin(1/x)$ or $f(x) = 1/x$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Answer.* Let $f(x) = \sin(1/x)$ on $(0,1)$.

**Continuous on $(0,1)$:** For each $c \in (0,1)$, $f$ is the composition of $g(x) = 1/x$ (continuous at $c$ since $c \neq 0$) and $h(t) = \sin(t)$ (continuous everywhere). By Theorem 7.4, $f = h \circ g$ is continuous at $c$.

**Not uniformly continuous on $(0,1)$:** Let $\varepsilon_0 = 1$. For any $\delta > 0$, choose $n$ large enough that $\frac{1}{2\pi n} < \delta$ and $n \geq 1$. Set

$$x = \frac{1}{2\pi n + \pi/2}, \qquad y = \frac{1}{2\pi n}.$$

Both $x, y \in (0,1)$ for large $n$. We have $|x - y| \leq \frac{1}{2\pi n} < \delta$. But

$$|f(x) - f(y)| = |\sin(2\pi n + \pi/2) - \sin(2\pi n)| = |1 - 0| = 1 \geq \varepsilon_0.$$

Since for every $\delta > 0$ we can find $x, y \in (0,1)$ with $|x - y| < \delta$ but $|f(x) - f(y)| \geq 1$, the function $f$ is not uniformly continuous on $(0,1)$.

**Remark.** This does not contradict the Heine-Cantor theorem because $(0,1)$ is not compact (it is bounded but not closed). $\square$
</Solution>
