## Monotone Sequences

<Callout type="definition" title="Monotone Sequences">
A sequence $(a_n)$ is:
- **Monotone increasing** (or nondecreasing) if $a_n \leq a_{n+1}$ for all $n$.
- **Strictly increasing** if $a_n < a_{n+1}$ for all $n$.
- **Monotone decreasing** (or nonincreasing) if $a_n \geq a_{n+1}$ for all $n$.
- **Strictly decreasing** if $a_n > a_{n+1}$ for all $n$.
- **Monotone** if it is either monotone increasing or monotone decreasing.
</Callout>

Monotone sequences have a powerful convergence criterion that relies directly on completeness.

## The Monotone Convergence Theorem

<Callout type="theorem" title="Monotone Convergence Theorem (MCT)">
Every bounded monotone sequence converges.

More precisely:
- If $(a_n)$ is monotone increasing and bounded above, then $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \sup\{a_n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$.
- If $(a_n)$ is monotone decreasing and bounded below, then $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \inf\{a_n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$.
</Callout>

**Proof (increasing case).** Let $(a_n)$ be monotone increasing and bounded above. The set $S = \{a_n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is nonempty and bounded above, so by completeness, $L = \sup S$ exists in $\mathbb{R}$.

We claim $a_n \to L$. Let $\epsilon > 0$. By the $\epsilon$-characterization of supremum, there exists $a_N \in S$ with $a_N > L - \epsilon$. Since $(a_n)$ is monotone increasing, for all $n \geq N$:

$$L - \epsilon < a_N \leq a_n \leq L < L + \epsilon.$$

Therefore $|a_n - L| < \epsilon$ for all $n \geq N$, so $a_n \to L$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Why MCT is Powerful">
MCT gives convergence without knowing the limit. You only need to verify two things: (1) the sequence is monotone, and (2) it is bounded. The limit is then guaranteed to exist, and equals the supremum (or infimum). This is a quintessential use of completeness.
</Callout>

<Problem title="Apply the Monotone Convergence Theorem">
Define $a_1 = 1$ and $a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}(a_n + 3/a_n)$ for $n \geq 1$. Show that $(a_n)$ converges, and find its limit.

<Solution>
**Step 1: Show $a_n \geq \sqrt{3}$ for all $n \geq 2$.**

By AM-GM: $a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}(a_n + 3/a_n) \geq \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2\sqrt{a_n \cdot 3/a_n} = \sqrt{3}$ for all $n \geq 1$. So $a_n \geq \sqrt{3}$ for $n \geq 2$.

**Step 2: Show $(a_n)_{n \geq 2}$ is monotone decreasing.**

For $n \geq 2$: $a_{n+1} - a_n = \frac{1}{2}(a_n + 3/a_n) - a_n = \frac{1}{2}(3/a_n - a_n) = \frac{3 - a_n^2}{2a_n}$.

Since $a_n \geq \sqrt{3}$ for $n \geq 2$, we have $a_n^2 \geq 3$, so $a_{n+1} - a_n \leq 0$.

**Step 3: Converge and find the limit.**

$(a_n)_{n \geq 2}$ is monotone decreasing and bounded below by $\sqrt{3}$. By MCT, $L = \lim a_n$ exists.

Taking limits in the recurrence: $L = \frac{1}{2}(L + 3/L)$, so $2L = L + 3/L$, hence $L = 3/L$, giving $L^2 = 3$. Since $L \geq \sqrt{3} > 0$, we conclude $L = \sqrt{3}$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

## Subsequences

<Callout type="definition" title="Subsequence">
Let $(a_n)$ be a sequence and let $n_1 < n_2 < n_3 < \cdots$ be a strictly increasing sequence of natural numbers. The sequence $(a_{n_k})_{k=1}^{\infty}$ is called a **subsequence** of $(a_n)$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Subsequences of Convergent Sequences">
If $a_n \to L$, then every subsequence $(a_{n_k})$ also converges to $L$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $\epsilon > 0$. Since $a_n \to L$, there exists $N$ with $|a_n - L| < \epsilon$ for all $n \geq N$. For $k \geq N$: $n_k \geq k \geq N$ (since the indices are strictly increasing), so $|a_{n_k} - L| < \epsilon$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="info" title="Contrapositive for Divergence">
The contrapositive gives a useful divergence test: if a sequence has two subsequences converging to different limits, then the sequence diverges.

Example: $a_n = (-1)^n$. The subsequence $a_{2k} = 1 \to 1$ and $a_{2k-1} = -1 \to -1$. Since $1 \neq -1$, the sequence diverges.
</Callout>

## The Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem

<Callout type="theorem" title="Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem">
Every bounded sequence in $\mathbb{R}$ has a convergent subsequence.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $(a_n)$ be bounded: $a_n \in [A, B]$ for all $n$.

We construct a convergent subsequence using **interval bisection**.

**Step 1:** Set $[a^{(0)}, b^{(0)}] = [A, B]$. This interval contains infinitely many terms of $(a_n)$ (in fact, all of them).

**Step 2:** Bisect: consider $[A, (A+B)/2]$ and $[(A+B)/2, B]$. At least one of these half-intervals contains infinitely many terms. Choose one that does; call it $[a^{(1)}, b^{(1)}]$ and pick one term $a_{n_1}$ from it.

**Step 3 (Induction):** Having chosen $[a^{(k)}, b^{(k)}]$ with length $(B-A)/2^k$ containing infinitely many terms, and having picked $a_{n_k}$ from it, bisect $[a^{(k)}, b^{(k)}]$ and choose a half containing infinitely many terms. Call it $[a^{(k+1)}, b^{(k+1)}]$ and pick $a_{n_{k+1}}$ with $n_{k+1} > n_k$ (possible since infinitely many terms remain).

**Step 4:** The nested intervals $[a^{(k)}, b^{(k)}]$ have lengths $(B-A)/2^k \to 0$. By the nested interval property, there is a unique $L \in \bigcap_k [a^{(k)}, b^{(k)}]$.

Since $a_{n_k} \in [a^{(k)}, b^{(k)}]$ and $b^{(k)} - a^{(k)} = (B-A)/2^k \to 0$, we have $|a_{n_k} - L| \leq (B-A)/2^k \to 0$, so $a_{n_k} \to L$. $\blacksquare$

## Divergence versus Oscillation

Not all non-convergent sequences behave the same way.

<Callout type="definition" title="Types of Divergence">
A sequence $(a_n)$ **diverges to $+\infty$** if for every $M > 0$, there exists $N$ such that $a_n > M$ for all $n \geq N$. We write $a_n \to +\infty$.

Similarly for $a_n \to -\infty$.

A sequence that diverges but does not diverge to $\pm\infty$ is said to **oscillate**. Example: $(-1)^n$.
</Callout>

<Problem title="Prove: A Convergent Sequence Has a Unique Subsequential Limit">
Prove that if $a_n \to L$, then every subsequence of $(a_n)$ converges to $L$. Then use the contrapositive to show that $a_n = (-1)^n$ diverges.

<Solution>
**Part 1.** Let $(a_{n_k})$ be a subsequence of $(a_n)$, and let $\epsilon > 0$. Since $a_n \to L$, there exists $N$ with $|a_n - L| < \epsilon$ for all $n \geq N$.

We claim $n_k \geq k$ for all $k$ (proved by induction: $n_1 \geq 1$, and if $n_k \geq k$, then $n_{k+1} > n_k \geq k$, so $n_{k+1} \geq k + 1$).

For $k \geq N$: $n_k \geq k \geq N$, so $|a_{n_k} - L| < \epsilon$. Hence $a_{n_k} \to L$.

**Part 2.** Consider $a_n = (-1)^n$. The subsequence $a_{2k} = 1 \to 1$. The subsequence $a_{2k-1} = -1 \to -1$. If $(a_n)$ converged to some $L$, then by Part 1, both subsequences would converge to $L$, giving $L = 1$ and $L = -1$, a contradiction. Therefore $(a_n)$ diverges. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

<Problem>
**Exercise 4.3 (Definition Drill).** Prove directly from the definition that the sequence $a_n = \frac{n}{n+1}$ is monotone increasing. Then use the Monotone Convergence Theorem to conclude it converges, and find the limit.

*Hint:* Show $a_{n+1} - a_n > 0$ for all $n \geq 1$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* **Monotone increasing:** We compute

$$a_{n+1} - a_n = \frac{n+1}{n+2} - \frac{n}{n+1} = \frac{(n+1)^2 - n(n+2)}{(n+2)(n+1)} = \frac{n^2 + 2n + 1 - n^2 - 2n}{(n+2)(n+1)} = \frac{1}{(n+2)(n+1)} > 0$$

for all $n \geq 1$. So $(a_n)$ is strictly increasing.

**Bounded above:** For all $n \geq 1$, $a_n = \frac{n}{n+1} = 1 - \frac{1}{n+1} < 1$.

**Convergence by MCT:** Since $(a_n)$ is monotone increasing and bounded above by $1$, the Monotone Convergence Theorem guarantees $L = \lim a_n$ exists and equals $\sup\{a_n\}$.

**Finding the limit:** By the algebra of limits, $L = \lim \frac{n}{n+1} = \lim \frac{1}{1 + 1/n} = \frac{1}{1+0} = 1$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 4.4 (Counterexample).** Give an example of a sequence that has a convergent subsequence but is itself divergent. Prove both claims.

*Hint:* Consider a sequence that oscillates between two values while also visiting increasingly large numbers.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Answer.* Define $a_n$ by:

$$a_n = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is even} \\ n & \text{if } n \text{ is odd} \end{cases}$$

**Convergent subsequence:** The subsequence $(a_{2k})_{k=1}^\infty = (0, 0, 0, \ldots)$ converges to $0$.

**Divergent:** The sequence is unbounded since $a_{2k-1} = 2k - 1 \to \infty$. A convergent sequence must be bounded (Theorem: convergent implies bounded), so $(a_n)$ diverges.

This shows that the converse of "if $a_n \to L$ then every subsequence converges to $L$" fails: a sequence can have a convergent subsequence without being convergent itself. The Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem guarantees a convergent subsequence for any *bounded* sequence, but does not require the full sequence to converge. $\square$
</Solution>
