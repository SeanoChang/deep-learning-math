# Algebra of Derivatives

Having defined the derivative and established its first properties, we now develop the algebraic rules that
allow us to differentiate combinations of functions. Each rule is stated precisely and proved from the
limit definition. The chain rule, in particular, requires a careful argument that avoids a subtle pitfall
involving division by zero.

## Linearity of the Derivative

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem (Linearity).** If $f$ and $g$ are differentiable at $a$, and $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$, then
$\alpha f + \beta g$ is differentiable at $a$ and

$$(\alpha f + \beta g)'(a) = \alpha f'(a) + \beta g'(a).$$

*Proof.* By the sum and scalar multiple rules for limits:

$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{[\alpha f + \beta g](a+h) - [\alpha f + \beta g](a)}{h} = \alpha \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} + \beta \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{g(a+h)-g(a)}{h} = \alpha f'(a) + \beta g'(a). \quad \blacksquare$$
</Callout>

## The Product Rule

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem (Product rule).** If $f$ and $g$ are differentiable at $a$, then $fg$ is differentiable at $a$ and

$$(fg)'(a) = f'(a)g(a) + f(a)g'(a).$$

*Proof.* The key technique is **adding and subtracting a bridge term**. Write:

$$\frac{(fg)(a+h) - (fg)(a)}{h} = \frac{f(a+h)g(a+h) - f(a)g(a)}{h}.$$

Add and subtract $f(a+h)g(a)$ in the numerator:

$$= \frac{f(a+h)g(a+h) - f(a+h)g(a) + f(a+h)g(a) - f(a)g(a)}{h}$$

$$= f(a+h) \cdot \frac{g(a+h) - g(a)}{h} + g(a) \cdot \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}.$$

Now take the limit as $h \to 0$. Since $f$ is differentiable at $a$, it is continuous there (by the
theorem from Lesson 11), so $\lim_{h \to 0} f(a+h) = f(a)$. Hence:

$$\lim_{h \to 0}\left[f(a+h) \cdot \frac{g(a+h)-g(a)}{h}\right] = f(a) \cdot g'(a)$$

and

$$\lim_{h \to 0}\left[g(a) \cdot \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\right] = g(a) \cdot f'(a).$$

By the sum rule for limits:

$$(fg)'(a) = f(a)g'(a) + g(a)f'(a). \quad \blacksquare$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**Remark.** The bridge term $f(a+h)g(a)$ is not the only choice. We could equally use $f(a)g(a+h)$, which
leads to the same result by a symmetric argument. The essential point is that we need to isolate one factor
at a time.
</Callout>

## The Quotient Rule

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem (Quotient rule).** If $f$ and $g$ are differentiable at $a$ and $g(a) \neq 0$, then $f/g$ is
differentiable at $a$ and

$$\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(a) = \frac{f'(a)g(a) - f(a)g'(a)}{[g(a)]^2}.$$

*Proof.* It suffices to prove that $1/g$ is differentiable (the general case then follows from the product rule).

$$\frac{\frac{1}{g(a+h)} - \frac{1}{g(a)}}{h} = \frac{g(a) - g(a+h)}{h \cdot g(a+h) \cdot g(a)} = \frac{-1}{g(a+h) \cdot g(a)} \cdot \frac{g(a+h) - g(a)}{h}.$$

Since $g$ is differentiable at $a$, it is continuous there, so $g(a+h) \to g(a) \neq 0$. Therefore:

$$\left(\frac{1}{g}\right)'(a) = \frac{-g'(a)}{[g(a)]^2}.$$

Applying the product rule to $f \cdot (1/g)$:

$$\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(a) = f'(a) \cdot \frac{1}{g(a)} + f(a) \cdot \frac{-g'(a)}{[g(a)]^2} = \frac{f'(a)g(a) - f(a)g'(a)}{[g(a)]^2}. \quad \blacksquare$$
</Callout>

## The Chain Rule

The chain rule is the most important differentiation rule and the one whose proof requires the most care.

<Callout type="definition">
**Setup.** Suppose $g$ is differentiable at $a$ and $f$ is differentiable at $g(a)$. We want to differentiate
the composition $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$ at $x = a$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**A naive attempt fails.** One might try:

$$\frac{f(g(a+h)) - f(g(a))}{h} = \frac{f(g(a+h)) - f(g(a))}{g(a+h) - g(a)} \cdot \frac{g(a+h) - g(a)}{h}$$

and take limits. But $g(a+h) - g(a)$ could be $0$ for some $h \neq 0$, making the first fraction undefined.
We need a different approach.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem (Chain rule).** If $g$ is differentiable at $a$ and $f$ is differentiable at $b = g(a)$, then
$f \circ g$ is differentiable at $a$ and

$$(f \circ g)'(a) = f'(g(a)) \cdot g'(a).$$

*Proof.* Since $f$ is differentiable at $b = g(a)$, by the linear approximation characterization (Lesson 11)
there exists a function $\epsilon$ with $\lim_{k \to 0}\epsilon(k) = 0$ and $\epsilon(0) = 0$ such that

$$f(b + k) - f(b) = f'(b) \cdot k + \epsilon(k) \cdot k$$

for all $k$ (including $k = 0$, where both sides are $0$). Set $k = g(a+h) - g(a)$:

$$f(g(a+h)) - f(g(a)) = f'(g(a)) \cdot [g(a+h) - g(a)] + \epsilon(g(a+h) - g(a)) \cdot [g(a+h) - g(a)].$$

Divide by $h \neq 0$:

$$\frac{f(g(a+h)) - f(g(a))}{h} = [f'(g(a)) + \epsilon(g(a+h) - g(a))] \cdot \frac{g(a+h) - g(a)}{h}.$$

As $h \to 0$: $g(a+h) - g(a) \to 0$ (since $g$ is continuous at $a$), so $\epsilon(g(a+h)-g(a)) \to 0$.
Also $\frac{g(a+h)-g(a)}{h} \to g'(a)$. Therefore:

$$(f \circ g)'(a) = [f'(g(a)) + 0] \cdot g'(a) = f'(g(a)) \cdot g'(a). \quad \blacksquare$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
The key insight in this proof is using the linear approximation form $f(b+k) = f(b) + f'(b)k + \epsilon(k)k$,
which is valid even when $k = 0$. This avoids the division-by-zero issue entirely.
</Callout>

## Derivatives of Inverse Functions

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem (Inverse function derivative).** Let $f$ be continuous and strictly monotone on an open interval $I$,
and suppose $f$ is differentiable at $a \in I$ with $f'(a) \neq 0$. Then $f^{-1}$ is differentiable at
$b = f(a)$ and

$$(f^{-1})'(b) = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(b))} = \frac{1}{f'(a)}.$$

*Proof.* Let $y = f(a)$ and consider the difference quotient for $f^{-1}$ at $y$:

$$\frac{f^{-1}(y + k) - f^{-1}(y)}{k}.$$

Set $a + h = f^{-1}(y + k)$, so $k = f(a+h) - f(a)$. Since $f$ is strictly monotone, $k \neq 0$ when $h \neq 0$,
and $k \to 0$ iff $h \to 0$ (by continuity of $f$ and $f^{-1}$). Then:

$$\frac{f^{-1}(y+k) - f^{-1}(y)}{k} = \frac{h}{f(a+h) - f(a)} = \frac{1}{\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}}.$$

As $k \to 0$ (equivalently $h \to 0$), the right side $\to \frac{1}{f'(a)}$, provided $f'(a) \neq 0$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**Application.** From $\frac{d}{dx}x^n = nx^{n-1}$ and the inverse function theorem, we can derive

$$\frac{d}{dx}x^{1/n} = \frac{1}{n}x^{1/n - 1}$$

for $x > 0$, extending the power rule to rational exponents.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem>
**Exercise 1.** Give a complete proof of the product rule using the definition of the derivative. Identify
precisely where you use: (a) differentiability of $f$, (b) differentiability of $g$, (c) the theorem that
differentiability implies continuity.
</Problem>

<Solution>
We compute:

$$\frac{f(a+h)g(a+h) - f(a)g(a)}{h}$$

Adding and subtracting $f(a+h)g(a)$:

$$= f(a+h)\frac{g(a+h)-g(a)}{h} + g(a)\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}.$$

Taking limits as $h \to 0$:

- **(b)** The second fraction converges to $g'(a)$ by differentiability of $g$.
- **(a)** The fourth fraction converges to $f'(a)$ by differentiability of $f$.
- **(c)** $\lim_{h\to 0}f(a+h) = f(a)$ because differentiability of $f$ implies continuity of $f$.

By the product and sum rules for limits:

$$(fg)'(a) = f(a)g'(a) + g(a)f'(a). \quad \blacksquare$$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 2.** Prove that if $f$ is differentiable at $a$ and $f(a) \neq 0$, then $1/f$ is differentiable
at $a$ and $(1/f)'(a) = -f'(a)/[f(a)]^2$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
Compute the difference quotient:

$$\frac{\frac{1}{f(a+h)} - \frac{1}{f(a)}}{h} = \frac{f(a) - f(a+h)}{h \cdot f(a+h) \cdot f(a)}.$$

Rewrite as:

$$= \frac{-1}{f(a+h) \cdot f(a)} \cdot \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}.$$

As $h \to 0$:
- $\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \to f'(a)$ (differentiability of $f$).
- $f(a+h) \to f(a)$ (differentiability implies continuity), and $f(a) \neq 0$ by hypothesis.

By the product rule for limits:

$$\left(\frac{1}{f}\right)'(a) = \frac{-1}{f(a) \cdot f(a)} \cdot f'(a) = \frac{-f'(a)}{[f(a)]^2}. \quad \blacksquare$$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 3 (Definition Drill).** Prove from the limit definition of the derivative (not the chain rule) that if $f(x) = (x^2 + 1)^2$, then $f'(x) = 4x(x^2 + 1)$.

*Hint:* Expand $f(x) = x^4 + 2x^2 + 1$ and compute the difference quotient directly.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* Expanding: $f(x) = x^4 + 2x^2 + 1$. The difference quotient at $a$ is:

$$\frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h} = \frac{(a+h)^4 + 2(a+h)^2 + 1 - a^4 - 2a^2 - 1}{h}.$$

Expanding $(a+h)^4 = a^4 + 4a^3h + 6a^2h^2 + 4ah^3 + h^4$ and $(a+h)^2 = a^2 + 2ah + h^2$:

$$= \frac{4a^3h + 6a^2h^2 + 4ah^3 + h^4 + 4ah + 2h^2}{h} = 4a^3 + 6a^2h + 4ah^2 + h^3 + 4a + 2h.$$

Taking $\lim_{h \to 0}$:

$$f'(a) = 4a^3 + 4a = 4a(a^2 + 1).$$

This agrees with the chain rule computation: if $g(x) = x^2 + 1$ and $u(t) = t^2$, then $f = u \circ g$ and $f'(a) = u'(g(a)) \cdot g'(a) = 2(a^2+1) \cdot 2a = 4a(a^2+1)$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 4 (Counterexample).** The chain rule requires $f$ to be differentiable at $g(a)$, not merely continuous. Give an example of continuous functions $g$ and $f$ where $g$ is differentiable at $a$, $f$ is continuous but not differentiable at $g(a)$, and $(f \circ g)'(a)$ does not exist.

*Hint:* Let $g(x) = x$ and $f(x) = |x|$, and examine $a = 0$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Answer.* Let $g(x) = x$ (differentiable everywhere with $g'(x) = 1$) and $f(x) = |x|$ (continuous everywhere). Consider $a = 0$: $g(0) = 0$ and $f$ is not differentiable at $0$ (as shown in Lesson 11).

The composition $(f \circ g)(x) = |g(x)| = |x|$, and:

$$\frac{(f \circ g)(0 + h) - (f \circ g)(0)}{h} = \frac{|h|}{h}$$

which has left-hand limit $-1$ and right-hand limit $+1$. So $(f \circ g)'(0)$ does not exist.

If we naively tried to apply the chain rule, we would write $(f \circ g)'(0) = f'(g(0)) \cdot g'(0) = f'(0) \cdot 1$, but $f'(0)$ does not exist. The chain rule's hypothesis that $f$ be differentiable at $g(a)$ is essential.

**Remark.** It is possible for $f \circ g$ to be differentiable even when $f$ is not differentiable at $g(a)$, if $g$ "smooths out" the problem. For example, $g(x) = x^2$ and $f(x) = |x|$ give $(f \circ g)(x) = x^2$, which is differentiable at $0$. The chain rule is a sufficient condition, not necessary. $\square$
</Solution>

## Verifying Derivative Rules Numerically

<PythonPlayground
  title="Numerical verification of the product, quotient, and chain rules"
  defaultCode={`import numpy as np

h = 1e-7
a = 2.0

def f(x): return x**3
def g(x): return np.sin(x)

def df(x): return 3*x**2
def dg(x): return np.cos(x)

# Product rule: (fg)' = f'g + fg'
prod_num = (f(a+h)*g(a+h) - f(a)*g(a)) / h
prod_exact = df(a)*g(a) + f(a)*dg(a)
print("=== Product Rule ===")
print(f"Numerical:  {prod_num:.8f}")
print(f"Analytical: {prod_exact:.8f}")
print(f"Error:      {abs(prod_num - prod_exact):.2e}")

# Quotient rule: (f/g)' = (f'g - fg') / g^2
quot_num = (f(a+h)/g(a+h) - f(a)/g(a)) / h
quot_exact = (df(a)*g(a) - f(a)*dg(a)) / g(a)**2
print("\\n=== Quotient Rule ===")
print(f"Numerical:  {quot_num:.8f}")
print(f"Analytical: {quot_exact:.8f}")
print(f"Error:      {abs(quot_num - quot_exact):.2e}")

# Chain rule: (f(g(x)))' = f'(g(x)) * g'(x)
chain_num = (f(g(a+h)) - f(g(a))) / h
chain_exact = df(g(a)) * dg(a)
print("\\n=== Chain Rule ===")
print(f"Numerical:  {chain_num:.8f}")
print(f"Analytical: {chain_exact:.8f}")
print(f"Error:      {abs(chain_num - chain_exact):.2e}")
`}
