## The Completeness Axiom

The ordered field axioms alone do not distinguish $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$. The key additional axiom is **completeness**.

<Callout type="definition" title="Least Upper Bound Property (Completeness Axiom)">
Every nonempty subset of $\mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above has a least upper bound (supremum) in $\mathbb{R}$.

Symbolically: if $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, $S \neq \emptyset$, and $S$ is bounded above, then $\sup S$ exists in $\mathbb{R}$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="Why Q Fails Completeness">
The set $\{q \in \mathbb{Q} : q^2 < 2\}$ is nonempty and bounded above in $\mathbb{Q}$, but has no least upper bound in $\mathbb{Q}$. Its "would-be" supremum is $\sqrt{2}$, which is irrational. Completeness is precisely the axiom that fills these gaps.
</Callout>

This single axiom has profound consequences. We now derive three of the most important.

## The Archimedean Property

<Callout type="theorem" title="Archimedean Property">
For every $x \in \mathbb{R}$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $n > x$.

Equivalently: $\mathbb{N}$ is not bounded above in $\mathbb{R}$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Suppose for contradiction that $\mathbb{N}$ is bounded above in $\mathbb{R}$. Since $\mathbb{N}$ is nonempty and (by assumption) bounded above, the completeness axiom gives $\alpha = \sup \mathbb{N} \in \mathbb{R}$.

Since $\alpha$ is the least upper bound, $\alpha - 1$ is not an upper bound of $\mathbb{N}$. Therefore there exists $m \in \mathbb{N}$ with $m > \alpha - 1$, i.e., $m + 1 > \alpha$.

But $m + 1 \in \mathbb{N}$, and $\alpha$ is an upper bound of $\mathbb{N}$, so $m + 1 \leq \alpha$. This contradicts $m + 1 > \alpha$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Useful Corollary">
The Archimedean property has an equivalent formulation that appears constantly: for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $1/n < \epsilon$. (Apply the Archimedean property to $x = 1/\epsilon$.)
</Callout>

## Density of $\mathbb{Q}$ in $\mathbb{R}$

<Callout type="theorem" title="Density of Rationals">
For every $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$ with $a < b$, there exists $q \in \mathbb{Q}$ with $a < q < b$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** We need to find integers $m, n$ with $n > 0$ such that $a < m/n < b$, i.e., $na < m < nb$.

**Step 1.** By the Archimedean property, choose $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n > 0$ such that $n(b - a) > 1$, i.e., $nb - na > 1$. (This is possible since $b - a > 0$, so apply the Archimedean property to $1/(b-a)$.)

**Step 2.** By the Archimedean property again, the set $\{k \in \mathbb{Z} : k > na\}$ is nonempty (since $\mathbb{N}$ is unbounded, and similarly $-\mathbb{N}$ is unbounded below). Let $m$ be the smallest integer greater than $na$. Then $m - 1 \leq na < m$, so $m \leq na + 1$.

**Step 3.** We have $m > na$, so $m/n > a$. Also, $m \leq na + 1 < na + n(b - a) = nb$, so $m/n < b$.

Therefore $a < m/n < b$ with $m/n \in \mathbb{Q}$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="theorem" title="Density of Irrationals">
For every $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$ with $a < b$, there exists an irrational number $t$ with $a < t < b$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Since $a < b$, we have $a - \sqrt{2} < b - \sqrt{2}$. By density of $\mathbb{Q}$, choose $q \in \mathbb{Q}$ with $a - \sqrt{2} < q < b - \sqrt{2}$. Then $t = q + \sqrt{2}$ satisfies $a < t < b$.

It remains to show $t$ is irrational. If $t = q + \sqrt{2}$ were rational, then $\sqrt{2} = t - q$ would be rational (since the rationals are closed under subtraction), contradicting the irrationality of $\sqrt{2}$. $\blacksquare$

<Problem title="Prove: Density of Irrationals">
Give a complete proof that between any two distinct real numbers there lies an irrational number. You may use the density of $\mathbb{Q}$ in $\mathbb{R}$ and the fact that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

<Solution>
**Proof.** Let $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$ with $a < b$. Then $a/\sqrt{2} < b/\sqrt{2}$. By the density of $\mathbb{Q}$ in $\mathbb{R}$, there exists a rational $q$ with $a/\sqrt{2} < q < b/\sqrt{2}$. We may assume $q \neq 0$ (if $q = 0$, then $a < 0 < b$, and we can find a nonzero rational between $a/\sqrt{2}$ and $b/\sqrt{2}$ since the interval is nonempty and not $\{0\}$).

Let $t = q\sqrt{2}$. Then $a < t < b$. We claim $t$ is irrational: if $t$ were rational, then $\sqrt{2} = t/q$ would be rational (since $q \neq 0$ and the rationals are closed under division), contradicting the irrationality of $\sqrt{2}$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

## Existence of Square Roots

<Callout type="theorem" title="Existence of Square Roots">
For every $a > 0$, there exists a unique $b > 0$ such that $b^2 = a$. We write $b = \sqrt{a}$.
</Callout>

**Proof (Existence via Supremum).** Define $S = \{t \in \mathbb{R} : t \geq 0 \text{ and } t^2 < a\}$.

**$S$ is nonempty:** $0 \in S$ since $0^2 = 0 < a$.

**$S$ is bounded above:** Let $M = \max(a, 1)$. If $t > M$, then $t > 1$ and $t \geq a$, so $t^2 \geq t \cdot 1 = t \geq a$, meaning $t \notin S$. Hence $M$ is an upper bound.

By completeness, $b = \sup S$ exists. We show $b^2 = a$ by ruling out $b^2 < a$ and $b^2 > a$.

**Case 1: $b^2 < a$.** Let $\epsilon = \frac{a - b^2}{2b + 1}$. Then $\epsilon > 0$, and for $0 < h < \min(\epsilon, 1)$:

$$(b + h)^2 = b^2 + 2bh + h^2 \leq b^2 + 2bh + h = b^2 + h(2b + 1) < b^2 + \epsilon(2b + 1) = a.$$

So $b + h \in S$, contradicting $b = \sup S$.

**Case 2: $b^2 > a$.** Let $\epsilon = \frac{b^2 - a}{2b}$. Then $\epsilon > 0$, and for $0 < h < \min(\epsilon, b)$:

$$(b - h)^2 = b^2 - 2bh + h^2 > b^2 - 2bh \geq b^2 - 2b\epsilon = a.$$

So $b - h$ is still an upper bound of $S$ (since $t \in S \Rightarrow t^2 < a \leq (b-h)^2 \Rightarrow t < b-h$), contradicting $b = \sup S$ being the *least* upper bound.

Therefore $b^2 = a$.

**Uniqueness:** If $b_1, b_2 > 0$ with $b_1^2 = b_2^2 = a$, then $b_1^2 - b_2^2 = (b_1 - b_2)(b_1 + b_2) = 0$. Since $b_1 + b_2 > 0$, we get $b_1 = b_2$. $\blacksquare$

## Nested Interval Reasoning

<Callout type="theorem" title="Nested Interval Property">
Let $I_n = [a_n, b_n]$ be a sequence of nonempty closed bounded intervals with $I_1 \supseteq I_2 \supseteq I_3 \supseteq \cdots$. Then

$$\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} I_n \neq \emptyset.$$

If additionally $b_n - a_n \to 0$, then $\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} I_n$ contains exactly one point.
</Callout>

**Proof.** The sequence $(a_n)$ is nondecreasing and bounded above (by $b_1$), so by completeness, $\alpha = \sup\{a_n\}$ exists. Similarly, $\beta = \inf\{b_n\}$ exists.

For every $n$: $a_n \leq \alpha \leq \beta \leq b_n$ (since each $b_k$ is an upper bound for all $a_n$, so $\alpha \leq b_k$ for all $k$, hence $\alpha \leq \beta$). Therefore $\alpha \in [a_n, b_n]$ for all $n$, and $\bigcap I_n \neq \emptyset$.

If $b_n - a_n \to 0$, then for any $x, y \in \bigcap I_n$: $|x - y| \leq b_n - a_n \to 0$, so $x = y$. $\blacksquare$

## Numerical Approximation of $\sqrt{2}$ via Supremum

The following playground constructs the set $S = \{t \geq 0 : t^2 < 2\}$ and approximates $\sup S$ using a bisection method -- essentially the same supremum-based argument used in the existence proof.

<PythonPlayground
  title="Approximating sqrt(2) via Supremum Construction"
  defaultCode={`# Bisection to approximate sup{t >= 0 : t^2 < 2}
# This mirrors the proof: we narrow down the supremum

a, b = 0.0, 2.0  # Initial interval: a^2 < 2, b^2 >= 2

print("Bisection approximation of sqrt(2):")
print(f"{'Step':>4} {'a':>14} {'b':>14} {'midpoint':>14} {'mid^2':>14}")
print("-" * 65)

for i in range(1, 51):
    mid = (a + b) / 2
    if mid * mid < 2:
        a = mid  # mid is in S, so sup >= mid
    else:
        b = mid  # mid is an upper bound of S
    if i <= 20 or i % 10 == 0:
        print(f"{i:4d} {a:14.10f} {b:14.10f} {(a+b)/2:14.10f} {((a+b)/2)**2:14.10f}")

print(f"\\nFinal approximation: {(a+b)/2:.15f}")
print(f"Actual sqrt(2):      {2**0.5:.15f}")
print(f"Error:               {abs((a+b)/2 - 2**0.5):.2e}")`}
/>

<Problem>
**Exercise 2.2 (Definition Drill).** Prove directly from the definition of supremum that $\sup\{1 - 1/n : n \in \mathbb{N},\, n \geq 1\} = 1$.

*Hint:* Verify the two conditions: (1) $1$ is an upper bound, and (2) for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists an element of the set greater than $1 - \epsilon$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* Let $S = \{1 - 1/n : n \geq 1\}$.

**(1) $1$ is an upper bound:** For every $n \geq 1$, $1/n > 0$, so $1 - 1/n < 1$. Thus $s < 1$ for all $s \in S$, and in particular $s \leq 1$.

**(2) $1$ is the least upper bound:** Let $\epsilon > 0$. By the Archimedean property, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ with $N > 1/\epsilon$, i.e., $1/N < \epsilon$. Then $1 - 1/N \in S$ and $1 - 1/N > 1 - \epsilon$. Since $\epsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, no number less than $1$ is an upper bound of $S$.

By (1) and (2), $\sup S = 1$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 2.3 (Counterexample).** Show that $\mathbb{Q}$ does not satisfy the completeness axiom by exhibiting a nonempty subset of $\mathbb{Q}$ that is bounded above in $\mathbb{Q}$ but has no least upper bound in $\mathbb{Q}$.

*Hint:* Consider $\{q \in \mathbb{Q} : q^2 < 2\}$ and use the irrationality of $\sqrt{2}$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Answer.* Let $S = \{q \in \mathbb{Q} : q \geq 0 \text{ and } q^2 < 2\}$.

**$S$ is nonempty:** $1 \in S$ since $1^2 = 1 < 2$.

**$S$ is bounded above in $\mathbb{Q}$:** $2 \in \mathbb{Q}$ is an upper bound since $q \in S$ implies $q^2 < 2 < 4$, so $q < 2$.

**$S$ has no least upper bound in $\mathbb{Q}$:** Suppose for contradiction that $\alpha \in \mathbb{Q}$ satisfies $\alpha = \sup S$ (in $\mathbb{Q}$). Then $\alpha^2 = 2$ is impossible since $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$. If $\alpha^2 < 2$, one can find a rational $q > \alpha$ with $q^2 < 2$ (by choosing $q = \alpha + \epsilon$ for sufficiently small rational $\epsilon > 0$), contradicting $\alpha$ being an upper bound. If $\alpha^2 > 2$, one can find a rational $\beta < \alpha$ with $\beta^2 > 2$, so $\beta$ is still an upper bound of $S$, contradicting $\alpha$ being the *least* upper bound.

Therefore no rational number is the supremum of $S$ in $\mathbb{Q}$, and $\mathbb{Q}$ fails the completeness axiom. $\square$
</Solution>
