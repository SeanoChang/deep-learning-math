# Taylor's Theorem and Semester Synthesis

Taylor's theorem is the crowning achievement of the differential calculus developed over this semester.
It extends the idea of local linear approximation (Lesson 11) to polynomial approximations of arbitrary
degree, with precise quantitative error control. We prove the theorem with the Lagrange form of the
remainder, explore its applications, and then step back to survey the logical arc of the entire course.

## Higher-Order Derivatives

<Callout type="definition">
**Definition (Higher derivatives).** If $f'$ is itself differentiable at $a$, we define the **second derivative**:

$$f''(a) = (f')'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f'(a+h) - f'(a)}{h}.$$

Inductively, the **$n$-th derivative** $f^{(n)}(a)$ is defined as $(f^{(n-1)})'(a)$, provided all
intermediate derivatives exist in a neighborhood of $a$. We write $f^{(0)} = f$ by convention.

We say $f$ is **$n$ times differentiable** at $a$ if $f^{(n)}(a)$ exists. If $f^{(n)}$ exists and is
continuous on an interval $I$, we write $f \in C^n(I)$.
</Callout>

## Taylor Polynomials

<Callout type="definition">
**Definition (Taylor polynomial).** If $f$ is $n$ times differentiable at $a$, the **Taylor polynomial of
degree $n$** centered at $a$ is

$$P_n(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k = f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2!}(x-a)^2 + \cdots + \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n.$$

When $a = 0$, this is also called the **Maclaurin polynomial**.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**Key property.** The Taylor polynomial $P_n$ is the unique polynomial of degree $\leq n$ that matches $f$
and its first $n$ derivatives at $a$:

$$P_n^{(k)}(a) = f^{(k)}(a) \quad \text{for } k = 0, 1, \ldots, n.$$

This follows directly from the construction.
</Callout>

## Taylor's Theorem with Lagrange Remainder

<Callout type="theorem">
**Theorem (Taylor's theorem, Lagrange form).** Let $f$ be $(n+1)$ times differentiable on an open interval
$I$ containing $a$. For every $x \in I$, there exists a point $c$ strictly between $a$ and $x$ such that

$$f(x) = P_n(x) + R_n(x)$$

where the **Lagrange remainder** is

$$R_n(x) = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1}.$$

*Proof.* Fix $x \neq a$ and define the constant $M$ by requiring

$$f(x) = P_n(x) + M(x-a)^{n+1}.$$

That is, $M = \frac{f(x) - P_n(x)}{(x-a)^{n+1}}$. We must show $M = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}$ for some $c$
between $a$ and $x$.

Define $g \colon I \to \mathbb{R}$ by

$$g(t) = f(t) - P_n(t) - M(t-a)^{n+1}.$$

Then $g$ has the following properties:

- $g(a) = f(a) - P_n(a) - 0 = 0$ (since $P_n(a) = f(a)$).
- $g(x) = f(x) - P_n(x) - M(x-a)^{n+1} = 0$ (by definition of $M$).
- $g^{(k)}(a) = f^{(k)}(a) - P_n^{(k)}(a) = 0$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n$ (since $P_n$ matches $f$ through order $n$ at $a$).

Apply Rolle's theorem repeatedly:

1. $g(a) = g(x) = 0 \implies$ there exists $c_1$ between $a$ and $x$ with $g'(c_1) = 0$.
2. $g'(a) = 0$ and $g'(c_1) = 0 \implies$ there exists $c_2$ between $a$ and $c_1$ with $g''(c_2) = 0$.
3. Continuing, $g^{(k)}(a) = 0$ and $g^{(k)}(c_k) = 0 \implies$ there exists $c_{k+1}$ between $a$ and $c_k$ with $g^{(k+1)}(c_{k+1}) = 0$.

After $n+1$ applications, we obtain $c = c_{n+1}$ between $a$ and $x$ with $g^{(n+1)}(c) = 0$.

Now compute $g^{(n+1)}$. Since $P_n$ has degree $\leq n$, $P_n^{(n+1)} = 0$. The $(n+1)$-th derivative
of $M(t-a)^{n+1}$ is $M \cdot (n+1)!$. Therefore:

$$0 = g^{(n+1)}(c) = f^{(n+1)}(c) - 0 - M(n+1)!$$

$$M = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}. \quad \blacksquare$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
The proof is elegant: define $M$ so that $g$ vanishes at both $a$ and $x$, use the matching derivatives at
$a$ to get additional zeros of successive derivatives, and then apply Rolle's theorem $n+1$ times to drive
down to the $(n+1)$-th derivative.
</Callout>

## Error Bounds

<Callout type="theorem">
**Corollary (Taylor remainder bound).** Under the hypotheses of Taylor's theorem, if $|f^{(n+1)}(t)| \leq M_{n+1}$
for all $t$ between $a$ and $x$, then

$$|R_n(x)| = |f(x) - P_n(x)| \leq \frac{M_{n+1}}{(n+1)!}|x-a|^{n+1}.$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**Example.** For $f(x) = e^x$ centered at $a = 0$, we have $f^{(k)}(x) = e^x$ for all $k$. On $[0,1]$:

$$|R_n(x)| \leq \frac{e}{(n+1)!} \quad \text{for } x \in [0,1].$$

Since $(n+1)! \to \infty$, the Taylor polynomials converge to $e^x$ on $[0,1]$ as $n \to \infty$.
</Callout>

## Common Taylor Series

For reference, here are the Taylor series (centered at $a = 0$) for the fundamental functions, all provable
from the machinery developed in this course:

| Function | Taylor Series | Radius of Convergence |
|----------|--------------|----------------------|
| $e^x$ | $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^k}{k!}$ | $\infty$ |

## Exercises

<Problem>
**Exercise 1.** Prove Taylor's theorem with Lagrange remainder for the case $n = 1$, starting from the
Mean Value Theorem. (This special case is sometimes called the **extended MVT**.)
</Problem>

<Solution>
For $n = 1$, Taylor's theorem states: if $f$ is twice differentiable on an interval containing $a$ and $x$,
then there exists $c$ between $a$ and $x$ with

$$f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(c)}{2}(x-a)^2.$$

**Proof.** Define $g(t) = f(t) - f(a) - f'(a)(t-a) - M(t-a)^2$ where $M$ is chosen so that $g(x) = 0$,
i.e., $M = \frac{f(x) - f(a) - f'(a)(x-a)}{(x-a)^2}$.

Note $g(a) = 0$ and $g(x) = 0$, so by Rolle's theorem there exists $c_1$ between $a$ and $x$ with $g'(c_1) = 0$.

Also $g'(t) = f'(t) - f'(a) - 2M(t-a)$, so $g'(a) = 0$.

Since $g'(a) = 0$ and $g'(c_1) = 0$, by Rolle's theorem there exists $c$ between $a$ and $c_1$ with $g''(c) = 0$.

$g''(t) = f''(t) - 2M$, so $g''(c) = 0$ gives $f''(c) = 2M$, hence $M = f''(c)/2$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 2.** Use Taylor's theorem to show that for all $x > 0$:

$$x - \frac{x^2}{2} < \ln(1+x) < x.$$
</Problem>

<Solution>
Let $f(t) = \ln(1+t)$. Then $f(0) = 0$, $f'(t) = \frac{1}{1+t}$, $f''(t) = -\frac{1}{(1+t)^2}$.

**Upper bound ($\ln(1+x) < x$):** Apply Taylor's theorem with $n = 0$ (i.e., the MVT) to $f$ on $[0, x]$:

$$\ln(1+x) = f(0) + f'(c) \cdot x = \frac{x}{1+c}$$

for some $c \in (0, x)$. Since $c > 0$, $1 + c > 1$, so $\frac{x}{1+c} < x$.

**Lower bound ($x - x^2/2 < \ln(1+x)$):** Apply Taylor's theorem with $n = 1$:

$$\ln(1+x) = x + \frac{f''(c)}{2}x^2 = x - \frac{x^2}{2(1+c)^2}$$

for some $c \in (0,x)$. Since $c > 0$, $(1+c)^2 > 1$, so $\frac{x^2}{2(1+c)^2} < \frac{x^2}{2}$.
Therefore $\ln(1+x) = x - \frac{x^2}{2(1+c)^2} > x - \frac{x^2}{2}$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 3 (Definition Drill).** Compute the Taylor polynomial $P_3(x)$ of $f(x) = \cos(x)$ centered at $a = 0$ directly from the definition. Then use the Lagrange remainder to bound $|\cos(0.5) - P_3(0.5)|$.

*Hint:* Compute $f(0), f'(0), f''(0), f'''(0)$ and form the polynomial. For the error bound, use $|f^{(4)}(c)| \leq 1$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* We have $f(x) = \cos x$, so:

$$f(0) = 1, \quad f'(0) = -\sin(0) = 0, \quad f''(0) = -\cos(0) = -1, \quad f'''(0) = \sin(0) = 0.$$

The Taylor polynomial of degree 3 centered at $0$ is:

$$P_3(x) = 1 + 0 \cdot x + \frac{-1}{2!}x^2 + \frac{0}{3!}x^3 = 1 - \frac{x^2}{2}.$$

(Note: the degree-3 and degree-2 polynomials coincide since $f'''(0) = 0$.)

**Error bound:** The fourth derivative is $f^{(4)}(x) = \cos(x)$, and $|\cos(c)| \leq 1$ for all $c$. By the Lagrange remainder bound:

$$|\cos(0.5) - P_3(0.5)| = |R_3(0.5)| \leq \frac{1}{4!}|0.5|^4 = \frac{(0.5)^4}{24} = \frac{0.0625}{24} \approx 0.0026.$$

For comparison: $P_3(0.5) = 1 - 0.125 = 0.875$ and $\cos(0.5) \approx 0.8776$, so the actual error is about $0.0026$, within the bound. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 4 (Counterexample).** Give an example of a function $f$ that is infinitely differentiable ($C^\infty$) at $a = 0$, whose Taylor series converges for all $x$, but does NOT converge to $f(x)$ for $x \neq 0$.

*Hint:* Consider $f(x) = e^{-1/x^2}$ for $x \neq 0$ and $f(0) = 0$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Answer.* Define

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} e^{-1/x^2} & \text{if } x \neq 0 \\ 0 & \text{if } x = 0. \end{cases}$$

**$f$ is $C^\infty$ and $f^{(n)}(0) = 0$ for all $n$:** One can show by induction that $f^{(n)}(x)$ for $x \neq 0$ is of the form $p(1/x) e^{-1/x^2}$ for some polynomial $p$. Using the fact that $e^{-1/x^2}$ decays faster than any power of $x$ as $x \to 0$, one proves $\lim_{x \to 0} f^{(n)}(x)/x^k = 0$ for all $k$, which gives $f^{(n)}(0) = 0$ for all $n \geq 0$ (via L'Hopital or direct estimation).

**The Taylor series is identically zero:** Since $f^{(n)}(0) = 0$ for all $n$, the Taylor series of $f$ at $0$ is

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!}x^n = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 0 = 0.$$

This converges to $0$ for all $x$.

**But $f(x) \neq 0$ for $x \neq 0$:** For $x \neq 0$, $f(x) = e^{-1/x^2} > 0 \neq 0$.

Therefore the Taylor series converges everywhere but represents $f$ only at $x = 0$. This shows that the Lagrange remainder $R_n(x) \to 0$ is a *necessary condition* for the Taylor series to converge to $f$, and it does not hold automatically even for $C^\infty$ functions. The functions for which it does hold (like $e^x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$) are called **real analytic**. $\square$
</Solution>

## Semester Synthesis

As we close the semester, let us trace the logical arc of the course:

<Callout type="info">
**The logical structure of rigorous calculus.**

1. **Foundations:** The real numbers $\mathbb{R}$, completeness (least upper bound property), and the
   Archimedean property. These axioms distinguish $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$ and make analysis possible.

2. **Sequences and limits:** $\epsilon$-$N$ definitions, convergence theorems (Monotone Convergence, Bolzano-Weierstrass),
   Cauchy sequences. The language of rigorous approximation.

3. **Continuity:** $\epsilon$-$\delta$ definition, sequential characterization, preservation of compactness
   (Extreme Value Theorem), preservation of connectedness (Intermediate Value Theorem). Continuous functions
   respect the topological structure of $\mathbb{R}$.

4. **Differentiation:** The derivative as a limit of difference quotients, differentiability implies continuity
   (but not conversely), algebraic rules (product, quotient, chain), and the Mean Value Theorem -- the bridge
   between local (derivative) and global (function values) information.

5. **Taylor's theorem:** The culmination. Local information ($f(a), f'(a), f''(a), \ldots$) determines
   the function's behavior near $a$ with quantitative error control. The factorial growth in the denominator
   of $R_n$ ensures that for "nice" functions, the approximation improves as $n$ increases.
</Callout>

Each layer depends on the previous ones: Taylor's theorem requires repeated application of Rolle's theorem,
which requires the Extreme Value Theorem, which requires completeness of $\mathbb{R}$.

This hierarchy is not just logical -- it reflects a deepening understanding of how local and global, discrete
and continuous, approximate and exact relate to one another. These ideas form the foundation for everything
that follows: integration, series, multivariable calculus, and ultimately the mathematical framework of
deep learning.
