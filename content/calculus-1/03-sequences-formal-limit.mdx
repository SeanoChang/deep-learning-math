## Sequences as Functions

<Callout type="definition" title="Sequence">
A **sequence** of real numbers is a function $a : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R}$. We write $a_n$ for $a(n)$ and denote the sequence by $(a_n)_{n=1}^{\infty}$ or simply $(a_n)$.
</Callout>

Sequences are the fundamental objects through which we study limits. Before defining convergence, observe that a sequence is not a set -- the ordering and repetition matter. The sequence $(1, 1, 1, \ldots)$ and the set $\{1\}$ are different objects.

## The $\epsilon$-$N$ Definition of Convergence

<Callout type="definition" title="Convergence of a Sequence">
A sequence $(a_n)$ **converges** to $L \in \mathbb{R}$ if: for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \geq N$,

$$|a_n - L| < \epsilon.$$

We write $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L$ or $a_n \to L$.

A sequence that does not converge is called **divergent**.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="Reading the Definition">
Parse the quantifiers carefully:

$$\forall \epsilon > 0,\; \exists N \in \mathbb{N},\; \forall n \geq N,\; |a_n - L| < \epsilon.$$

The $\epsilon$ is given to you (by an adversary, if you like). Your job is to produce $N$ (which typically depends on $\epsilon$) such that the tail of the sequence past index $N$ lies within the $\epsilon$-band $(L - \epsilon, L + \epsilon)$.
</Callout>

## Proving Convergence from the Definition

<Problem title="Prove: 1/n converges to 0">
Prove from the $\epsilon$-$N$ definition that $\lim_{n \to \infty} 1/n = 0$.

<Solution>
**Proof.** Let $\epsilon > 0$ be given. By the Archimedean property, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ with $N > 1/\epsilon$ (equivalently, $1/N < \epsilon$).

For all $n \geq N$:

$$|a_n - 0| = \left|\frac{1}{n}\right| = \frac{1}{n} \leq \frac{1}{N} < \epsilon.$$

Since $\epsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, $1/n \to 0$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

## Uniqueness of Limits

<Callout type="theorem" title="Uniqueness of Limits">
If $a_n \to L$ and $a_n \to M$, then $L = M$. That is, the limit of a convergent sequence is unique.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Suppose $a_n \to L$ and $a_n \to M$. Let $\epsilon > 0$. By convergence to $L$, there exists $N_1$ such that $|a_n - L| < \epsilon/2$ for all $n \geq N_1$. By convergence to $M$, there exists $N_2$ such that $|a_n - M| < \epsilon/2$ for all $n \geq N_2$.

Let $N = \max(N_1, N_2)$. For $n \geq N$:

$$|L - M| = |L - a_n + a_n - M| \leq |a_n - L| + |a_n - M| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} + \frac{\epsilon}{2} = \epsilon.$$

Since $|L - M| < \epsilon$ for every $\epsilon > 0$, and $|L - M| \geq 0$, we conclude $|L - M| = 0$, so $L = M$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="info" title="The Key Trick">
The argument "$|L - M| < \epsilon$ for every $\epsilon > 0$ implies $L = M$" is used throughout analysis. It works because if $|L - M| > 0$, we could take $\epsilon = |L - M|/2$ to get a contradiction.
</Callout>

## Algebra of Limits

<Callout type="theorem" title="Limit Laws">
Let $a_n \to L$ and $b_n \to M$. Then:

1. **Sum:** $a_n + b_n \to L + M$
2. **Scalar multiple:** $ca_n \to cL$ for any $c \in \mathbb{R}$
3. **Product:** $a_n b_n \to LM$
4. **Quotient:** $a_n / b_n \to L/M$, provided $M \neq 0$ and $b_n \neq 0$ for all $n$
</Callout>

**Proof of the Sum Law.** Let $\epsilon > 0$. Since $a_n \to L$, there exists $N_1$ with $|a_n - L| < \epsilon/2$ for $n \geq N_1$. Since $b_n \to M$, there exists $N_2$ with $|b_n - M| < \epsilon/2$ for $n \geq N_2$.

Set $N = \max(N_1, N_2)$. For $n \geq N$:

$$|(a_n + b_n) - (L + M)| = |(a_n - L) + (b_n - M)| \leq |a_n - L| + |b_n - M| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} + \frac{\epsilon}{2} = \epsilon.$$

Hence $a_n + b_n \to L + M$. $\blacksquare$

## Boundedness of Convergent Sequences

<Callout type="theorem" title="Convergent Implies Bounded">
If $(a_n)$ converges, then $(a_n)$ is bounded: there exists $M > 0$ with $|a_n| \leq M$ for all $n$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Suppose $a_n \to L$. Taking $\epsilon = 1$, there exists $N$ with $|a_n - L| < 1$ for all $n \geq N$. By the triangle inequality, for $n \geq N$:

$$|a_n| = |a_n - L + L| \leq |a_n - L| + |L| < 1 + |L|.$$

The finitely many terms $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_{N-1}$ are each bounded by their own absolute values. Set

$$M = \max(|a_1|, |a_2|, \ldots, |a_{N-1}|, 1 + |L|).$$

Then $|a_n| \leq M$ for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="warning" title="The Converse is False">
Bounded does NOT imply convergent. The sequence $a_n = (-1)^n$ is bounded ($|a_n| \leq 1$ for all $n$) but divergent, since it oscillates between $-1$ and $1$ without settling.
</Callout>

## The Squeeze Theorem

<Callout type="theorem" title="Squeeze Theorem (for Sequences)">
Let $(a_n)$, $(b_n)$, $(c_n)$ be sequences with $a_n \leq b_n \leq c_n$ for all $n \geq n_0$. If $a_n \to L$ and $c_n \to L$, then $b_n \to L$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $\epsilon > 0$. Choose $N_1 \geq n_0$ with $|a_n - L| < \epsilon$ for $n \geq N_1$, and $N_2 \geq n_0$ with $|c_n - L| < \epsilon$ for $n \geq N_2$. Set $N = \max(N_1, N_2)$.

For $n \geq N$: $L - \epsilon < a_n \leq b_n \leq c_n < L + \epsilon$, so $|b_n - L| < \epsilon$. $\blacksquare$

<Problem title="Prove: Squeeze Theorem Application">
Use the squeeze theorem to prove that $\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\sin(n)}{n} = 0$.

<Solution>
**Proof.** For all $n \geq 1$, we have $-1 \leq \sin(n) \leq 1$. Dividing by $n > 0$:

$$\frac{-1}{n} \leq \frac{\sin(n)}{n} \leq \frac{1}{n}.$$

We have shown that $1/n \to 0$, and by the scalar multiple law, $-1/n \to 0$. By the squeeze theorem, $\frac{\sin(n)}{n} \to 0$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

<Problem>
**Exercise 3.3 (Counterexample).** Give an example of a bounded sequence that does not converge. Prove that your sequence is bounded and that it diverges.

*Hint:* Consider $a_n = (-1)^n$. To prove divergence, use the fact that subsequences must converge to the same limit.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Answer.* Let $a_n = (-1)^n$.

**Bounded:** For all $n$, $|a_n| = |(-1)^n| = 1 \leq 1$, so the sequence is bounded.

**Divergent:** Suppose for contradiction that $a_n \to L$ for some $L \in \mathbb{R}$. Then every subsequence converges to $L$. The even subsequence $a_{2k} = 1 \to 1$, so $L = 1$. The odd subsequence $a_{2k-1} = -1 \to -1$, so $L = -1$. Since $1 \neq -1$, we have a contradiction.

Therefore $((-1)^n)$ is bounded but divergent. This shows that the converse of "convergent implies bounded" is false. $\square$
</Solution>
