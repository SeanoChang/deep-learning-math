## Mathematical Statements and Quantifiers

A **mathematical statement** (or **proposition**) is a declarative sentence that is either true or false, but not both. We build complex statements from simpler ones using logical connectives: conjunction ($\land$), disjunction ($\lor$), negation ($\lnot$), implication ($\Rightarrow$), and biconditional ($\Leftrightarrow$).

The **universal quantifier** $\forall$ ("for all") and the **existential quantifier** $\exists$ ("there exists") allow us to make claims about collections of objects. Their negations are crucial:

$$\lnot(\forall x,\, P(x)) \iff \exists x,\, \lnot P(x)$$

$$\lnot(\exists x,\, P(x)) \iff \forall x,\, \lnot P(x)$$

<Callout type="tip" title="Reading Quantified Statements">
Always read quantified statements from left to right. The statement $\forall \epsilon > 0,\, \exists N \in \mathbb{N},\, \forall n \geq N,\, |a_n - L| < \epsilon$ says: "No matter how small a positive tolerance $\epsilon$ you choose, I can find a stage $N$ beyond which every term $a_n$ stays within $\epsilon$ of $L$." The order of quantifiers matters: swapping $\forall$ and $\exists$ changes the meaning entirely.
</Callout>

## Proof Techniques

### Direct Proof

To prove $P \Rightarrow Q$: assume $P$ is true and derive $Q$ using definitions, axioms, and previously proven results.

### Proof by Contrapositive

Since $P \Rightarrow Q$ is logically equivalent to $\lnot Q \Rightarrow \lnot P$, we may instead assume $\lnot Q$ and derive $\lnot P$.

### Proof by Contradiction

To prove a statement $S$: assume $\lnot S$ and derive a logical contradiction. This forces $S$ to be true.

### Mathematical Induction

To prove $P(n)$ for all $n \geq n_0$: (1) prove $P(n_0)$ (base case), and (2) prove that $P(k) \Rightarrow P(k+1)$ for all $k \geq n_0$ (inductive step).

## The Ordered Field Axioms of $\mathbb{R}$

<Callout type="definition" title="Ordered Field">
The real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ form an **ordered field**: a set equipped with operations $+$ and $\cdot$ and a total order $\leq$ satisfying:

**Field axioms:** Associativity, commutativity, and distributivity of $+$ and $\cdot$; existence of additive identity $0$ and multiplicative identity $1 \neq 0$; additive inverses; multiplicative inverses for nonzero elements.

**Order axioms:** For all $a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}$:
- (Trichotomy) Exactly one holds: $a < b$, $a = b$, or $a > b$.
- (Transitivity) $a < b$ and $b < c$ imply $a < c$.
- (Addition) $a < b$ implies $a + c < b + c$.
- (Multiplication) $a < b$ and $c > 0$ imply $ac < bc$.
</Callout>

## Absolute Value and the Triangle Inequality

<Callout type="definition" title="Absolute Value">
For $x \in \mathbb{R}$, the **absolute value** is defined by

$$|x| = \begin{cases} x & \text{if } x \geq 0 \\ -x & \text{if } x < 0 \end{cases}$$

Equivalently, $|x| = \max(x, -x)$. The key property is: $|x| \leq c \iff -c \leq x \leq c$ for $c \geq 0$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Triangle Inequality">
For all $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$,

$$|a + b| \leq |a| + |b|.$$
</Callout>

**Proof.** We have $-|a| \leq a \leq |a|$ and $-|b| \leq b \leq |b|$. Adding these inequalities:

$$-(|a| + |b|) \leq a + b \leq |a| + |b|.$$

By the characterization of absolute value, this gives $|a + b| \leq |a| + |b|$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="theorem" title="Reverse Triangle Inequality">
For all $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$,

$$\bigl||a| - |b|\bigr| \leq |a - b|.$$
</Callout>

**Proof.** By the triangle inequality applied to $a = (a - b) + b$:

$$|a| = |(a - b) + b| \leq |a - b| + |b|,$$

so $|a| - |b| \leq |a - b|$. Swapping the roles of $a$ and $b$:

$$|b| - |a| \leq |b - a| = |a - b|.$$

Combining: $-(|a - b|) \leq |a| - |b| \leq |a - b|$, which gives $\bigl||a| - |b|\bigr| \leq |a - b|$. $\blacksquare$

## Upper Bounds, Lower Bounds, Supremum, and Infimum

<Callout type="definition" title="Bounded Sets, Supremum, and Infimum">
Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ be nonempty.

- $M$ is an **upper bound** of $S$ if $s \leq M$ for all $s \in S$. If such $M$ exists, $S$ is **bounded above**.
- $m$ is a **lower bound** of $S$ if $m \leq s$ for all $s \in S$. If such $m$ exists, $S$ is **bounded below**.
- The **supremum** $\sup S$ is the least upper bound: $\sup S$ is an upper bound of $S$, and if $M$ is any upper bound of $S$, then $\sup S \leq M$.
- The **infimum** $\inf S$ is the greatest lower bound, defined analogously.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="The Epsilon Characterization of Supremum">
$\alpha = \sup S$ if and only if: (1) $\alpha$ is an upper bound of $S$, and (2) for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $s \in S$ with $s > \alpha - \epsilon$. This characterization is indispensable in proofs.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Uniqueness of Supremum">
If $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is nonempty and bounded above, then $\sup S$ is unique.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Suppose $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are both least upper bounds of $S$. Since $\alpha$ is an upper bound and $\beta$ is the *least* upper bound, we have $\beta \leq \alpha$. Since $\beta$ is an upper bound and $\alpha$ is the *least* upper bound, we have $\alpha \leq \beta$. By antisymmetry, $\alpha = \beta$. $\blacksquare$

<Problem title="Prove: Uniqueness of Supremum (Your Turn)">
Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ be nonempty and bounded above. Reproduce the proof above from memory: show that if $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are both suprema of $S$, then $\alpha = \beta$. Write your proof carefully, citing exactly which property of "least upper bound" you use at each step.

<Solution>
**Proof.** Suppose $\alpha = \sup S$ and $\beta = \sup S$. We show $\alpha = \beta$.

Since $\alpha$ is a supremum, it is in particular an upper bound of $S$. Since $\beta$ is a *least* upper bound, we have $\beta \leq M$ for every upper bound $M$ of $S$. Taking $M = \alpha$, we get $\beta \leq \alpha$.

By the symmetric argument (swapping $\alpha$ and $\beta$), $\alpha \leq \beta$.

By antisymmetry of $\leq$, we conclude $\alpha = \beta$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

<Problem>
**Exercise 1.2 (Theorem Application).** Let $S$ and $T$ be nonempty subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ that are bounded above. Define $S + T = \{s + t : s \in S,\, t \in T\}$. Prove that $\sup(S + T) = \sup S + \sup T$.

*Hint:* Show $\sup S + \sup T$ is an upper bound of $S + T$, then use the $\epsilon$-characterization of supremum to show no smaller number is an upper bound.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Proof.* Let $\alpha = \sup S$ and $\beta = \sup T$.

**Upper bound:** For any $s \in S$ and $t \in T$, we have $s \leq \alpha$ and $t \leq \beta$, so $s + t \leq \alpha + \beta$. Thus $\alpha + \beta$ is an upper bound of $S + T$.

**Least upper bound:** Let $\epsilon > 0$. By the $\epsilon$-characterization of supremum, there exist $s_0 \in S$ with $s_0 > \alpha - \epsilon/2$ and $t_0 \in T$ with $t_0 > \beta - \epsilon/2$. Then $s_0 + t_0 \in S + T$ and

$$s_0 + t_0 > (\alpha - \epsilon/2) + (\beta - \epsilon/2) = \alpha + \beta - \epsilon.$$

Since $\epsilon > 0$ was arbitrary and $\alpha + \beta$ is an upper bound of $S + T$, we conclude $\sup(S + T) = \alpha + \beta$. $\square$
</Solution>

<Problem>
**Exercise 1.3 (Counterexample).** Give an example of a nonempty subset $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above but has no maximum (i.e., $\sup S \notin S$). Prove that your example has the claimed properties.

*Hint:* Consider an open interval.
</Problem>

<Solution>
*Answer.* Let $S = (0, 1) = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : 0 < x < 1\}$.

**$S$ is nonempty and bounded above:** $1/2 \in S$, so $S \neq \emptyset$. Every element satisfies $x < 1$, so $1$ is an upper bound.

**$\sup S = 1$:** We showed $1$ is an upper bound. For any $\epsilon > 0$, choose $x = \max(1/2,\, 1 - \epsilon/2) \in (0,1)$. Then $x > 1 - \epsilon$, so no number smaller than $1$ is an upper bound. Hence $\sup S = 1$.

**$S$ has no maximum:** Suppose for contradiction that $m = \max S$ exists, so $m \in S$ and $m \leq x$ fails for no $x \in S$ larger than $m$. Since $m \in (0,1)$, we have $m < 1$, so $(m + 1)/2 \in (0,1) = S$ and $(m+1)/2 > m$. This contradicts $m$ being the maximum. $\square$
</Solution>
