# Conditional Expectation as Structure

In a first course, $E[X \mid Y = y]$ is defined by conditioning on a specific value. But this breaks down when $P(Y = y) = 0$ (the continuous case). The measure-theoretic definition of conditional expectation resolves this by defining $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ not as a number but as a *random variable* -- the best $\mathcal{G}$-measurable approximation to $X$.

## Sigma-Algebras as Partial Information

<Callout type="definition" title="Sub-sigma-algebra as Information">
  A sub-$\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ represents **partial information** about $\omega$. Knowing which events in $\mathcal{G}$ have occurred (i.e., knowing $\omega$ up to $\mathcal{G}$-equivalence) gives partial knowledge of the outcome.

  - $\mathcal{G} = \{\emptyset, \Omega\}$: no information at all.
  - $\mathcal{G} = \sigma(Y)$: the information contained in observing $Y$.
  - $\mathcal{G} = \mathcal{F}$: complete information.
</Callout>

A random variable $Z$ is **$\mathcal{G}$-measurable** if $Z^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{G}$ for every Borel set $B$. Intuitively, $Z$ is computable from the information in $\mathcal{G}$. If $\mathcal{G} = \sigma(Y)$, then $Z$ is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable if and only if $Z = g(Y)$ for some Borel function $g$ (the Doob--Dynkin lemma).

## The Measure-Theoretic Definition

<Callout type="definition" title="Conditional Expectation">
  Let $X$ be an integrable random variable on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ and $\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ a sub-$\sigma$-algebra. The **conditional expectation** $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is the (a.s. unique) random variable satisfying:
  1. $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable, and
  2. For every $G \in \mathcal{G}$: $\displaystyle\int_G E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]\,dP = \int_G X\,dP$.
</Callout>

Condition 1 says the conditional expectation is "knowable from $\mathcal{G}$." Condition 2, the **defining condition**, says it has the same integral as $X$ over every $\mathcal{G}$-measurable set. Existence follows from the Radon--Nikodym theorem; uniqueness is a.s. (any two versions agree except on a null set).

<Callout type="tip" title="Writing $E[X \\mid Y]$">
  We write $E[X \mid Y] = E[X \mid \sigma(Y)]$. This is the conditional expectation given the information generated by $Y$. By the Doob--Dynkin lemma, $E[X \mid Y] = g(Y)$ for some Borel function $g$. The function $g$ satisfies $g(y) = E[X \mid Y = y]$ for $\mu_Y$-a.e. $y$, recovering the elementary notion.
</Callout>

### Reduction to the Discrete Case

When $\mathcal{G}$ is generated by a finite partition $\{B_1, \ldots, B_k\}$ of $\Omega$ (each $P(B_i) > 0$), the conditional expectation is constant on each piece:

$$E[X \mid \mathcal{G}](\omega) = \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{E[X \mathbf{1}_{B_i}]}{P(B_i)} \cdot \mathbf{1}_{B_i}(\omega).$$

This is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable (constant on partition cells) and satisfies the defining condition: for $G = B_j$, $\int_{B_j} E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]\,dP = E[X \mathbf{1}_{B_j}]/P(B_j) \cdot P(B_j) = E[X \mathbf{1}_{B_j}] = \int_{B_j} X\,dP$.

## Tower Property

<Callout type="theorem" title="Tower Property (Law of Iterated Expectation)">
  If $\mathcal{H} \subseteq \mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$, then
  $$E[E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] \mid \mathcal{H}] = E[X \mid \mathcal{H}] \quad \text{a.s.}$$
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $Z = E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ and $W = E[Z \mid \mathcal{H}]$. We verify that $W$ satisfies the defining condition for $E[X \mid \mathcal{H}]$.

1. *$\mathcal{H}$-measurability:* $W = E[Z \mid \mathcal{H}]$ is $\mathcal{H}$-measurable by definition.

2. *Defining condition:* For any $H \in \mathcal{H}$, since $\mathcal{H} \subseteq \mathcal{G}$, we have $H \in \mathcal{G}$. Therefore:
   $$\int_H W\,dP = \int_H Z\,dP = \int_H E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]\,dP = \int_H X\,dP.$$
   The first equality is the defining condition for $W = E[Z \mid \mathcal{H}]$ (using $H \in \mathcal{H}$). The second equality is the defining condition for $Z = E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ (using $H \in \mathcal{G}$).

By uniqueness, $W = E[X \mid \mathcal{H}]$ a.s. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Special Case: $\\mathcal{H} = \\{\\emptyset, \\Omega\\}$">
  Taking $\mathcal{H} = \{\emptyset, \Omega\}$ (the trivial $\sigma$-algebra) in the tower property gives
  $$E[E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]] = E[X],$$
  since $E[X \mid \{\emptyset, \Omega\}] = E[X]$ (a constant). This is the **law of total expectation**.
</Callout>

## Taking Out What Is Known

<Callout type="theorem" title="Taking Out Known Factors">
  If $Y$ is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable and bounded (or $E[|XY|] < \infty$), then
  $$E[XY \mid \mathcal{G}] = Y \cdot E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] \quad \text{a.s.}$$
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $Z = Y \cdot E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$.

1. *$\mathcal{G}$-measurability:* Both $Y$ and $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ are $\mathcal{G}$-measurable, so their product is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable.

2. *Defining condition:* For any $G \in \mathcal{G}$, we need $\int_G Z\,dP = \int_G XY\,dP$. First consider $Y = \mathbf{1}_A$ for $A \in \mathcal{G}$. Then $Z = \mathbf{1}_A \cdot E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ and:
   $$\int_G Z\,dP = \int_{G \cap A} E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]\,dP = \int_{G \cap A} X\,dP = \int_G X\mathbf{1}_A\,dP = \int_G XY\,dP.$$
   The second equality uses the defining condition (since $G \cap A \in \mathcal{G}$). By linearity, the result extends to $\mathcal{G}$-measurable simple functions, then to nonnegative $\mathcal{G}$-measurable $Y$ by monotone convergence, and to general $Y$ by decomposing $Y = Y^+ - Y^-$. $\blacksquare$

## $L^2$ Projection Interpretation

<Callout type="theorem" title="Conditional Expectation as Best $L^2$ Predictor">
  Among all $\mathcal{G}$-measurable random variables $Z$ with $E[Z^2] < \infty$, the conditional expectation $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ uniquely minimizes the mean squared error:
  $$E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] = \operatorname{arg\,min}_{Z \in L^2(\mathcal{G})} E[(X - Z)^2].$$
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $Y = E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ and let $Z$ be any $\mathcal{G}$-measurable random variable in $L^2$. Write:

$$E[(X - Z)^2] = E[(X - Y + Y - Z)^2] = E[(X - Y)^2] + 2E[(X - Y)(Y - Z)] + E[(Y - Z)^2].$$

The cross term vanishes: since $Y - Z$ is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable, the "taking out known factors" property gives:

$$E[(X - Y)(Y - Z)] = E[E[(X - Y)(Y - Z) \mid \mathcal{G}]] = E[(Y - Z) \cdot E[X - Y \mid \mathcal{G}]].$$

But $E[X - Y \mid \mathcal{G}] = E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] - Y = Y - Y = 0$. Therefore:

$$E[(X - Z)^2] = E[(X - Y)^2] + E[(Y - Z)^2] \geq E[(X - Y)^2],$$

with equality if and only if $Y - Z = 0$ a.s. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Geometric Interpretation">
  $L^2(\mathcal{G})$ is a closed subspace of the Hilbert space $L^2(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$. Conditional expectation $E[\cdot \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is the orthogonal projection onto this subspace. The "error" $X - E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is orthogonal to every $\mathcal{G}$-measurable random variable -- this is exactly the defining condition rewritten as an inner product condition.
</Callout>

## Conditional Jensen's Inequality

<Callout type="theorem" title="Conditional Jensen's Inequality">
  If $\varphi$ is convex and $E[|X|], E[|\varphi(X)|] < \infty$, then
  $$\varphi(E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]) \leq E[\varphi(X) \mid \mathcal{G}] \quad \text{a.s.}$$
</Callout>

**Proof.** Since $\varphi$ is convex, for every point $y$ there exists a supporting line: $\varphi(x) \geq \varphi(y) + \lambda(y)(x - y)$ for all $x$, for some slope $\lambda(y)$. Taking $y = E[X \mid \mathcal{G}](\omega)$ and $x = X(\omega)$:

$$\varphi(X) \geq \varphi(E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]) + \lambda(E[X \mid \mathcal{G}])(X - E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]).$$

Take conditional expectation $E[\cdot \mid \mathcal{G}]$ of both sides. By monotonicity of conditional expectation:

$$E[\varphi(X) \mid \mathcal{G}] \geq \varphi(E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]) + \lambda(E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]) \cdot E[X - E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] \mid \mathcal{G}].$$

The last term is $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] - E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] = 0$ (tower property). Note that $\lambda(E[X \mid \mathcal{G}])$ is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable, so it factors out. We are left with $E[\varphi(X) \mid \mathcal{G}] \geq \varphi(E[X \mid \mathcal{G}])$. $\blacksquare$

<Problem title="Verifying the tower property in a discrete example">
  Let $\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ with $P(\{i\}) = 1/4$ for each $i$. Let $\mathcal{G} = \sigma(\{1,2\}, \{3,4\})$ and $\mathcal{H} = \{\emptyset, \Omega\}$. Define $X(\omega) = \omega^2$ (so $X(1) = 1, X(2) = 4, X(3) = 9, X(4) = 16$).

  (a) Compute $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ explicitly.

  (b) Compute $E[E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] \mid \mathcal{H}]$ and verify it equals $E[X \mid \mathcal{H}] = E[X]$.

  (c) Compute $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]^2$ and $E[X^2 \mid \mathcal{G}]$. Verify that conditional Jensen's inequality holds with $\varphi(x) = x^2$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** $\mathcal{G}$ partitions $\Omega$ into $B_1 = \{1,2\}$ and $B_2 = \{3,4\}$.

  On $B_1$: $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] = E[X \mathbf{1}_{B_1}]/P(B_1) = (1/4 \cdot 1 + 1/4 \cdot 4)/(1/2) = (5/4)/(1/2) = 5/2$.

  On $B_2$: $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] = E[X \mathbf{1}_{B_2}]/P(B_2) = (1/4 \cdot 9 + 1/4 \cdot 16)/(1/2) = (25/4)/(1/2) = 25/2$.

  So $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}](\omega) = 5/2$ for $\omega \in \{1,2\}$ and $25/2$ for $\omega \in \{3,4\}$.

  **(b)** $E[E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] \mid \mathcal{H}] = E[E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]] = (5/2)(1/2) + (25/2)(1/2) = 5/4 + 25/4 = 30/4 = 15/2$.

  $E[X] = (1 + 4 + 9 + 16)/4 = 30/4 = 15/2$. These agree, confirming the tower property.

  **(c)** $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]^2$: on $B_1$, $(5/2)^2 = 25/4$; on $B_2$, $(25/2)^2 = 625/4$.

  $E[X^2 \mid \mathcal{G}]$: on $B_1$, $(1/4 \cdot 1 + 1/4 \cdot 16)/(1/2) = (17/4)/(1/2) = 17/2$; on $B_2$, $(1/4 \cdot 81 + 1/4 \cdot 256)/(1/2) = (337/4)/(1/2) = 337/2$.

  Check Jensen: on $B_1$, $25/4 = 6.25 \leq 17/2 = 8.5$. On $B_2$, $625/4 = 156.25 \leq 337/2 = 168.5$. Both hold. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem title="Independence and conditional expectation">
  Let $X$ and $Y$ be independent integrable random variables. Prove that $E[X \mid Y] = E[X]$ a.s.

  *Hint:* Verify that the constant $E[X]$ satisfies the defining condition of $E[X \mid \sigma(Y)]$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  We claim $E[X \mid \sigma(Y)] = E[X]$ (the constant random variable). Verify the two conditions:

  1. *$\sigma(Y)$-measurability:* A constant is measurable with respect to any $\sigma$-algebra.

  2. *Defining condition:* For any $G \in \sigma(Y)$, we need $\int_G E[X]\,dP = \int_G X\,dP$, i.e., $E[X] \cdot P(G) = E[X \mathbf{1}_G]$.

  Since $G \in \sigma(Y)$, we can write $G = \{Y \in B\}$ for some Borel set $B$. Then $\mathbf{1}_G = \mathbf{1}_{\{Y \in B\}} = h(Y)$ for $h = \mathbf{1}_B$. By independence of $X$ and $Y$:

  $$E[X \mathbf{1}_G] = E[X \cdot h(Y)] = E[X] \cdot E[h(Y)] = E[X] \cdot P(G).$$

  The factorization $E[X \cdot h(Y)] = E[X] \cdot E[h(Y)]$ holds because $X$ and $h(Y)$ are independent (as measurable functions of independent random variables).

  By uniqueness, $E[X \mid \sigma(Y)] = E[X]$ a.s. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
