## The Two-Semester Architecture

The following table traces how each concept generalized from Probability I to Probability II.

| Probability I | Probability II |
|---|---|
| Axioms, finite sample spaces | Convergence modes, Borel-Cantelli |
| Discrete/continuous distributions | Transforms (MGF, CF) as universal tools |
| $E[X]$, $\text{Var}(X)$, moment calculations | Moment inequalities (Markov, Chebyshev, Jensen) |
| Independence, joint distributions | Concentration inequalities (Chernoff, Hoeffding) |
| Conditional probability/expectation | Conditional expectation as projection, martingales |
| Indicator tricks, LOTUS | Generating function proofs, convolution |
| Informal "large $n$" intuition | WLLN, SLLN, CLT with rigorous proofs |

The progression follows a clear logic: **compute** (Prob I) then **bound and approximate** (Prob II). Exact distributions give way to asymptotic and tail-bound reasoning.

## Capstone Proof Chains

The major theorems of this course form interconnected chains. Understanding the logical dependencies is as important as the proofs themselves.

### Chain 1: The Law of Large Numbers

$$\text{Markov} \implies \text{Chebyshev} \implies \text{WLLN} \implies \text{Consistency of } \bar{X}_n.$$

The SLLN requires additional tools: fourth-moment bounds plus Borel-Cantelli (in our proof), or Kolmogorov's truncation method (for the general case with only $E[|X_1|] < \infty$).

### Chain 2: The Central Limit Theorem

$$\text{CF properties} \implies \text{Taylor expansion of } \varphi \implies \text{CF of } Z_n \to e^{-t^2/2} \implies \text{Levy's theorem} \implies \text{CLT}.$$

The CLT then feeds directly into statistics: asymptotic normality of estimators, confidence intervals, and the delta method.

### Chain 3: Concentration

$$\text{Markov on } e^{\theta X} \implies \text{Chernoff bound} \implies \text{Hoeffding's lemma (convexity)} \implies \text{Hoeffding's inequality}.$$

<Callout type="tip" title="Unifying Theme">
Every major result ultimately rests on **Markov's inequality** -- the simplest tail bound, applied to progressively more cleverly chosen functions of $X$. Chebyshev applies Markov to $(X - \mu)^2$. Chernoff applies Markov to $e^{\theta X}$. Even the SLLN proof applies Markov to $S_n^4$.
</Callout>

## What Measure Theory Fixes

Our two-semester development made several compromises. Measure theory resolves each one.

### The Event Space Problem

<Callout type="definition" title="$\\sigma$-Algebras">
A **$\sigma$-algebra** $\mathcal{F}$ on $\Omega$ is a collection of subsets containing $\emptyset$, closed under complements and countable unions. The probability space is the triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$.
</Callout>

Why not just use all subsets? Vitali's theorem (1905) shows that on $[0,1]$, no translation-invariant, countably additive measure can assign a value to every subset. The $\sigma$-algebra restricts us to "measurable" sets where probability is well-defined. On $\mathbb{R}$, the Borel $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ -- generated by open sets -- is the standard choice.

### The Integration Problem

<Callout type="definition" title="Lebesgue Integral">
The **Lebesgue integral** $\int_\Omega X\,dP$ is defined by approximating $X$ from below by simple functions $\sum a_i \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$, then taking the supremum. This unifies:

- **Discrete**: $E[X] = \sum_x x\,P(X = x)$ (counting measure)
- **Continuous**: $E[X] = \int x\,f(x)\,dx$ (Lebesgue measure with density $f$)
- **Mixed and singular**: distributions that are neither discrete nor continuous
</Callout>

The convergence theorems (Monotone Convergence, Dominated Convergence, Fatou's Lemma) are properties of the Lebesgue integral that we used implicitly throughout this course. In measure theory, they are proved rigorously.

### The Conditioning Problem

In Probability I, we defined $E[X|Y = y]$ using conditional densities. But what if $Y$ is a constant with probability one? What if $(X, Y)$ has no joint density?

<Callout type="theorem" title="Conditional Expectation (Measure-Theoretic)">
Given a sub-$\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$, the **conditional expectation** $E[X|\mathcal{G}]$ is the (a.s. unique) $\mathcal{G}$-measurable random variable satisfying:

$$\int_G E[X|\mathcal{G}]\,dP = \int_G X\,dP \quad \text{for all } G \in \mathcal{G}.$$

Its existence is guaranteed by the **Radon-Nikodym theorem**.
</Callout>

This definition makes $E[X|\mathcal{G}]$ a random variable, not a number -- resolving the fundamental conceptual issue. It is the **orthogonal projection** of $X$ onto $L^2(\mathcal{G})$, connecting probability to Hilbert space geometry.

## Advanced Convergence Theory

### Martingale Convergence

With measure-theoretic conditional expectation, **martingales** $(M_n, \mathcal{F}_n)$ are sequences satisfying $E[M_{n+1}|\mathcal{F}_n] = M_n$. The martingale convergence theorem states: if $\sup_n E[|M_n|] < \infty$, then $M_n \to M_\infty$ a.s. for some integrable $M_\infty$.

This gives alternative proofs of the SLLN and powers results like the optional stopping theorem for bounded stopping times.

### Weak Convergence and Tightness

Convergence in distribution ($X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$) is formalized as **weak convergence** of probability measures: $\mu_n \Rightarrow \mu$ if $\int f\,d\mu_n \to \int f\,d\mu$ for all bounded continuous $f$.

<Callout type="definition" title="Tightness">
A family of probability measures $\{\mu_\alpha\}$ on $\mathbb{R}$ is **tight** if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a compact set $K$ such that $\mu_\alpha(K) \geq 1 - \varepsilon$ for all $\alpha$.
</Callout>

**Prokhorov's theorem**: a family of probability measures on $\mathbb{R}$ is relatively compact (every sequence has a weakly convergent subsequence) if and only if it is tight. This is the key tool for proving CLT-type results in general settings.

## The Road Ahead

A measure-theoretic probability course (often titled "Probability Theory" or "Stochastic Processes") typically covers:

1. **Measure spaces and integration**: construction of Lebesgue measure, $L^p$ spaces, convergence theorems
2. **Probability foundations**: Kolmogorov extension theorem (existence of infinite product measures), $0$-$1$ laws
3. **Conditioning**: regular conditional distributions, disintegration of measures
4. **Martingale theory**: convergence, optional stopping, uniformly integrable martingales
5. **Limit theorems**: Lindeberg-Feller CLT, stable laws, large deviations
6. **Stochastic processes**: Brownian motion as the continuous-time limit of random walks (Donsker's invariance principle)

<Callout type="warning" title="What Changes, What Stays">
The *results* you have learned (LLN, CLT, Hoeffding) remain exactly the same. Measure theory changes the *language* and *proof techniques*, replacing ad hoc arguments with systematic tools. The intuition you have built is the most important asset you carry forward.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem title="Exercise 15.1: Tracing the Dependencies">
Starting from Markov's inequality, trace the logical chain of dependencies that leads to the CLT-based confidence interval $\bar{X}_n \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sigma/\sqrt{n}$. List each theorem/tool used and how it depends on the previous one.

<Solution>
1. **Markov's inequality**: $P(X \geq a) \leq E[X]/a$ for nonneg $X$. (Foundational, uses only monotonicity of expectation.)
2. **Characteristic functions exist**: $|\varphi_X(t)| = |E[e^{itX}]| \leq 1$ (boundedness of $e^{itX}$).
3. **CF differentiability**: $\varphi^{(k)}(0) = i^k E[X^k]$ (dominated convergence, which in measure theory follows from the Lebesgue integral).
4. **Taylor expansion of CF**: $\varphi(t) = 1 + i\mu t - \sigma^2 t^2/2 + o(t^2)$ (from Step 3).
5. **CLT proof**: $[\varphi(t/\sqrt{n})]^n \to e^{-t^2/2}$ (from Step 4 and the limit $(1+a/n)^n \to e^a$).
6. **Levy continuity theorem**: pointwise convergence of CFs implies convergence in distribution (deep result using tightness/Prokhorov).
7. **CLT conclusion**: $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$.
8. **Confidence interval**: invert the CLT to get $P(\mu \in \bar{X}_n \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sigma/\sqrt{n}) \approx 1 - \alpha$.

Separately, Markov $\implies$ Chebyshev $\implies$ WLLN $\implies$ $S_n \xrightarrow{P} \sigma$, and then Slutsky's theorem justifies replacing $\sigma$ by $S_n$.
</Solution>
</Problem>
