# Invariant Subspaces

The central strategy of linear algebra is decomposition: break a complicated operator into simpler
pieces acting on smaller spaces. The right notion of "piece" is an invariant subspace -- a subspace
that the operator maps into itself. When the whole space decomposes as a direct sum of invariant
subspaces, the matrix of the operator becomes block diagonal, and we can study each block independently.

## $T$-Invariant Subspaces

<Callout type="definition" title="Invariant Subspace">
  Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear operator on a vector space $V$. A subspace $W \subseteq V$ is
  **$T$-invariant** (or simply **invariant under $T$**) if
  $$T(W) \subseteq W, \quad \text{i.e., } Tw \in W \text{ for all } w \in W.$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="Invariant Does Not Mean Fixed">
  $T$-invariance of $W$ means $T$ maps $W$ *into* $W$, not that $T$ fixes every element of $W$. The
  operator may move vectors around within $W$, but no vector in $W$ ever gets sent outside of $W$.
  Think of $W$ as a "closed room" under the action of $T$.
</Callout>

**Trivial examples.** For any operator $T \colon V \to V$:
- $\{0\}$ is $T$-invariant (since $T(0) = 0$).
- $V$ is $T$-invariant (since $T(V) \subseteq V$ by definition).

These are called the **trivial invariant subspaces**. The interesting question is whether $T$ has
nontrivial ones.

## The Kernel and Image Are Always Invariant

<Callout type="theorem" title="ker and im Are T-Invariant">
  Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear operator. Then:
  1. $\ker T$ is $T$-invariant.
  2. $\operatorname{im} T$ is $T$-invariant.
  More generally, for any positive integer $k$, the subspaces $\ker T^k$ and $\operatorname{im} T^k$ are $T$-invariant.
</Callout>

**Proof.** (1) Let $v \in \ker T$. Then $T(v) = 0 \in \ker T$, so $\ker T$ is $T$-invariant.

(2) Let $w \in \operatorname{im} T$, so $w = Tv$ for some $v \in V$. Then $T(w) = T(Tv) = T^2(v) \in \operatorname{im} T$ since $T^2(v)$ is in the image of $T$ (it equals $T$ applied to $Tv$). So $\operatorname{im} T$ is $T$-invariant.

For the general case: if $v \in \ker T^k$, then $T^k(Tv) = T(T^k v) = T(0) = 0$, so $Tv \in \ker T^k$. If $w \in \operatorname{im} T^k$, then $w = T^k u$ for some $u$, and $Tw = T^{k+1}u = T^k(Tu) \in \operatorname{im} T^k$. $\blacksquare$

## Restriction to an Invariant Subspace

<Callout type="definition" title="Restriction Operator">
  Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear operator and $W \subseteq V$ a $T$-invariant subspace. The
  **restriction of $T$ to $W$**, denoted $T|_W$, is the linear operator
  $$T|_W \colon W \to W, \quad T|_W(w) = T(w).$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Restriction Is Well-Defined">
  If $W$ is $T$-invariant, then $T|_W$ is a well-defined linear operator on $W$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** We need two things:

1. **Well-defined as a map $W \to W$:** For any $w \in W$, we need $T|_W(w) = T(w) \in W$. This
holds precisely because $W$ is $T$-invariant.

2. **Linearity:** For $w_1, w_2 \in W$ and $c \in \mathbb{F}$,

$$T|_W(cw_1 + w_2) = T(cw_1 + w_2) = cT(w_1) + T(w_2) = cT|_W(w_1) + T|_W(w_2).$$

This follows from the linearity of $T$ on $V$, restricted to inputs from $W$. $\blacksquare$

The restriction is a genuinely smaller operator: $T|_W$ lives on the lower-dimensional space $W$.
If we can decompose $V$ into invariant subspaces, we replace the study of one big operator with
the study of several smaller ones.

## Direct Sum Decompositions and Block Diagonal Form

<Callout type="definition" title="Direct Sum of Subspaces">
  Subspaces $W_1, \ldots, W_k$ of $V$ form a **direct sum decomposition** of $V$, written
  $$V = W_1 \oplus W_2 \oplus \cdots \oplus W_k,$$
  if every $v \in V$ can be written *uniquely* as $v = w_1 + w_2 + \cdots + w_k$ with $w_i \in W_i$.
  This is equivalent to: (i) $V = W_1 + W_2 + \cdots + W_k$, and (ii) $W_i \cap (W_1 + \cdots + W_{i-1} + W_{i+1} + \cdots + W_k) = \{0\}$ for each $i$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Direct Sum of Invariant Subspaces Gives Block Diagonal Form">
  Let $T \colon V \to V$ and suppose $V = W_1 \oplus W_2 \oplus \cdots \oplus W_k$ where each $W_i$ is
  $T$-invariant. Choose a basis $\mathcal{B}_i$ for each $W_i$ and let $\mathcal{B} = \mathcal{B}_1 \cup \cdots \cup \mathcal{B}_k$.
  Then the matrix of $T$ with respect to $\mathcal{B}$ is block diagonal:
  $$[T]_\mathcal{B} = \begin{pmatrix} [T|_{W_1}]_{\mathcal{B}_1} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & [T|_{W_2}]_{\mathcal{B}_2} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & [T|_{W_k}]_{\mathcal{B}_k} \end{pmatrix}.$$
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $b \in \mathcal{B}_i$ be a basis vector for $W_i$. Since $W_i$ is $T$-invariant, $Tb \in W_i$.
So $Tb$ is a linear combination of vectors in $\mathcal{B}_i$ only. In the full matrix $[T]_\mathcal{B}$,
this means the column corresponding to $b$ has nonzero entries only in the rows corresponding to $\mathcal{B}_i$
(the $i$-th block). All other entries in that column are zero, producing the block diagonal structure. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Block Diagonal = Decoupled">
  A block diagonal matrix means the operator on each $W_i$ is completely independent of the others.
  The characteristic polynomial factors as
  $$p_T(\lambda) = p_{T|_{W_1}}(\lambda) \cdot p_{T|_{W_2}}(\lambda) \cdots p_{T|_{W_k}}(\lambda).$$
  Powers, determinants, traces, and all spectral properties can be computed block-by-block.
</Callout>

## Eigenspaces Are the Simplest Invariant Subspaces

<Callout type="definition" title="Eigenspace">
  For $\lambda \in \mathbb{F}$, the **eigenspace** of $T$ corresponding to $\lambda$ is
  $$E_\lambda = \ker(T - \lambda I) = \{v \in V : Tv = \lambda v\}.$$
</Callout>

Each eigenspace $E_\lambda$ is $T$-invariant: if $v \in E_\lambda$, then $T(v) = \lambda v \in E_\lambda$
(since $E_\lambda$ is a subspace and $\lambda v$ is a scalar multiple of an element of $E_\lambda$).

Moreover, the restriction $T|_{E_\lambda}$ is the simplest possible operator: scalar multiplication by
$\lambda$. Its matrix (in any basis of $E_\lambda$) is $\lambda I$. This is why diagonalization is the
best case: when $V = E_{\lambda_1} \oplus \cdots \oplus E_{\lambda_k}$, each block is a scalar matrix.

## Quotient Spaces (Light Treatment)

When a subspace $W$ is $T$-invariant, we can also study the "leftover" via the quotient.

<Callout type="definition" title="Quotient Space">
  Let $W$ be a subspace of $V$. The **quotient space** $V/W$ is the set of cosets
  $$V/W = \{v + W : v \in V\}$$
  with vector space operations $(v_1 + W) + (v_2 + W) = (v_1 + v_2) + W$ and $c(v + W) = cv + W$.
  The dimension is $\dim(V/W) = \dim V - \dim W$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="definition" title="Induced Operator on Quotient">
  If $W$ is $T$-invariant, the **induced operator** $\bar{T} \colon V/W \to V/W$ is defined by
  $$\bar{T}(v + W) = T(v) + W.$$
  This is well-defined: if $v + W = v' + W$ (i.e., $v - v' \in W$), then $T(v) - T(v') = T(v - v') \in W$
  (by $T$-invariance of $W$), so $T(v) + W = T(v') + W$.
</Callout>

The quotient space captures the action of $T$ "modulo $W$." Together, $T|_W$ and $\bar{T}$ carry
all the information about $T$, but they do so in a way that separates the action on $W$ from the
action on the complement. The characteristic polynomial factors accordingly:

$$p_T(\lambda) = p_{T|_W}(\lambda) \cdot p_{\bar{T}}(\lambda).$$

## Existence of Nontrivial Invariant Subspaces

A fundamental question: does every operator have a nontrivial invariant subspace? Over $\mathbb{R}$,
the answer is no (e.g., a $2 \times 2$ rotation by an irrational angle has no 1-dimensional invariant
subspace). Over $\mathbb{C}$, the situation is much better.

<Callout type="theorem" title="Existence Over C">
  Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{C}$ with $\dim V \geq 1$, and let
  $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear operator. Then $T$ has at least one eigenvalue, and hence at least one
  nontrivial invariant subspace (namely, any eigenspace).
</Callout>

**Proof.** The characteristic polynomial $p(\lambda) = \det(T - \lambda I)$ has degree $n = \dim V \geq 1$.
By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, $p$ has at least one root $\lambda_0 \in \mathbb{C}$. Then
$\det(T - \lambda_0 I) = 0$, so $T - \lambda_0 I$ is not invertible, meaning $\ker(T - \lambda_0 I) \neq \{0\}$.
The eigenspace $E_{\lambda_0}$ is a nontrivial $T$-invariant subspace. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Why C Matters">
  Over $\mathbb{C}$, we can always start peeling off invariant subspaces one eigenvalue at a time. This
  is why the Jordan Normal Form exists over $\mathbb{C}$ but not always over $\mathbb{R}$. Over $\mathbb{R}$,
  the characteristic polynomial may have irreducible quadratic factors with no real roots, leading to
  $2$-dimensional invariant subspaces (corresponding to rotation-scaling blocks) instead of $1$-dimensional
  eigenspaces.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem title="Identifying invariant subspaces">
  Let $T \colon \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ be defined by $T(x, y, z) = (2x + y, 3y, x + z)$.
  (a) Is $W_1 = \operatorname{span}\{(1, 0, 0)\}$ invariant under $T$?
  (b) Is $W_2 = \{(x, y, z) : y = 0\}$ invariant under $T$?
  (c) Find $\ker T$ and verify it is $T$-invariant.
  (d) Find at least one nontrivial $T$-invariant subspace.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** $T(1, 0, 0) = (2, 0, 1)$. Since $(2, 0, 1) \notin \operatorname{span}\{(1, 0, 0)\}$, $W_1$ is NOT $T$-invariant.
  **(b)** If $y = 0$, then $T(x, 0, z) = (2x, 0, x + z)$. The second component is $0$, so $T(x, 0, z) \in W_2$. Therefore $W_2$ IS $T$-invariant.
  **(c)** Solving $T(x, y, z) = (0, 0, 0)$: from $3y = 0$ we get $y = 0$. From $2x + y = 0$ we get $x = 0$. From $x + z = 0$ we get $z = 0$. So $\ker T = \{0\}$, the trivial subspace (which is always invariant).
  **(d)** From part (b), $W_2 = \{(x, 0, z) : x, z \in \mathbb{R}\}$ is a nontrivial 2-dimensional $T$-invariant subspace. The restriction $T|_{W_2}(x, z) = (2x, x + z)$ has matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ in the basis $\{(1,0,0), (0,0,1)\}$.
  Another: $W_3 = \operatorname{span}\{(0, 1, 0)\}$. Check: $T(0, 1, 0) = (1, 3, 0) \notin W_3$, so this does NOT work.
  We can also try eigenspaces. The matrix of $T$ is $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. Eigenvalue $\lambda = 3$: $\ker(A - 3I)$ gives a nontrivial invariant subspace. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem title="Proving the block diagonal structure theorem">
  Let $V = W_1 \oplus W_2$ where both $W_1$ and $W_2$ are $T$-invariant, with $\dim W_1 = 2$ and $\dim W_2 = 3$.
  (a) Explain why the matrix of $T$ in a basis $\mathcal{B}_1 \cup \mathcal{B}_2$ (where $\mathcal{B}_i$ is a basis for $W_i$) is block diagonal. Be precise about which entries are zero and why.
  (b) If $T|_{W_1}$ has eigenvalues $\{1, 2\}$ and $T|_{W_2}$ has eigenvalues $\{3, 3, 5\}$, what is the characteristic polynomial of $T$?
  (c) Is $T$ necessarily diagonalizable? Justify.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** Let $\mathcal{B}_1 = \{v_1, v_2\}$ and $\mathcal{B}_2 = \{v_3, v_4, v_5\}$. The matrix $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ has its $j$-th column equal to the coordinate vector of $Tv_j$ in the basis $\mathcal{B}$.
  For $j = 1, 2$: $v_j \in W_1$ and $W_1$ is $T$-invariant, so $Tv_j \in W_1$. This means $Tv_j$ is a linear combination of $v_1, v_2$ only. The coordinates of $Tv_j$ in $\mathcal{B}$ have zeros in positions $3, 4, 5$.
  For $j = 3, 4, 5$: $v_j \in W_2$ and $W_2$ is $T$-invariant, so $Tv_j \in W_2$. This means $Tv_j$ is a linear combination of $v_3, v_4, v_5$ only. The coordinates of $Tv_j$ in $\mathcal{B}$ have zeros in positions $1, 2$.
  The resulting matrix has the form $\begin{pmatrix} A_1 & 0 \\ 0 & A_2 \end{pmatrix}$ where $A_i = [T|_{W_i}]_{\mathcal{B}_i}$.
  **(b)** $p_T(\lambda) = p_{T|_{W_1}}(\lambda) \cdot p_{T|_{W_2}}(\lambda) = (\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 2)(\lambda - 3)^2(\lambda - 5)$.
  **(c)** Not necessarily. We know $\operatorname{am}(1) = 1$, $\operatorname{am}(2) = 1$, $\operatorname{am}(5) = 1$, and $\operatorname{am}(3) = 2$. The eigenvalues $1, 2, 5$ have algebraic multiplicity $1$, so their geometric multiplicities are also $1$. For $\lambda = 3$, we need $\operatorname{gm}(3) = 2$ for diagonalizability. If $T|_{W_2}$ restricted to eigenvalue $3$ has a Jordan block (e.g., $T|_{W_2}$ has matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$), then $\operatorname{gm}(3) = 1 < 2$ and $T$ is not diagonalizable. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>

<Problem title="Quotient space computation">
  Let $T \colon \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ have matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ and let $W = \operatorname{span}\{e_1\}$.
  (a) Verify that $W$ is $T$-invariant and describe $T|_W$.
  (b) Describe the quotient space $\mathbb{R}^3/W$ and the induced operator $\bar{T}$.
  (c) Compute the characteristic polynomials of $T$, $T|_W$, and $\bar{T}$, and verify the factorization $p_T = p_{T|_W} \cdot p_{\bar{T}}$.
</Problem>

<Solution>
  **(a)** $T(e_1) = (1, 0, 0) = 1 \cdot e_1 \in W$, so $W$ is $T$-invariant. The restriction $T|_W \colon W \to W$ is multiplication by $1$, i.e., $T|_W = \operatorname{id}_W$.
  **(b)** $\mathbb{R}^3/W$ consists of cosets $\{(x, y, z) + W\}$. Since $W = \operatorname{span}\{e_1\}$, two vectors are in the same coset iff they differ by a multiple of $e_1$, i.e., they have the same $y$ and $z$ coordinates. We can identify $\mathbb{R}^3/W$ with $\mathbb{R}^2$ via $(x, y, z) + W \mapsto (y, z)$.
  Under this identification, $\bar{T}((y, z)) = (2y + z, 2z)$, which has matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.
  **(c)** $p_T(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I) = (1 - \lambda)(2 - \lambda)^2$.
  $p_{T|_W}(\lambda) = 1 - \lambda$.
  $p_{\bar{T}}(\lambda) = \det\begin{pmatrix} 2 - \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & 2 - \lambda \end{pmatrix} = (2 - \lambda)^2$.
  Check: $(1 - \lambda)(2 - \lambda)^2 = p_{T|_W}(\lambda) \cdot p_{\bar{T}}(\lambda)$. $\checkmark$ $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
