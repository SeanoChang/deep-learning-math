# Singular Value Decomposition

The Spectral Theorem applies to self-adjoint (or normal) operators. But what about an arbitrary linear map $T : V \to W$ between possibly different spaces, with no symmetry at all? The Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) answers this question completely: every matrix can be decomposed into a rotation, a stretch along coordinate axes, and another rotation.

## What Does a Matrix Do Geometrically?

Consider a linear map $A : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$. It maps the unit sphere $S^{n-1} \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ to an ellipsoid in $\mathbb{R}^m$. The SVD makes this precise: there exist orthonormal bases for the domain and codomain in which $A$ acts as a simple coordinate-wise scaling.

The key idea: even though $A$ itself may not be symmetric, the matrices $A^T A$ and $AA^T$ are always symmetric and positive semidefinite.

## The Gram Matrices $A^T A$ and $AA^T$

<Callout type="theorem" title="A^T A is Positive Semidefinite">
  For any $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, the matrix $A^T A$ is symmetric and positive semidefinite. Moreover, $\operatorname{rank}(A^T A) = \operatorname{rank}(A)$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Symmetry: $(A^T A)^T = A^T (A^T)^T = A^T A$.

Positive semidefiniteness: for any $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$,

$$x^T (A^T A) x = (Ax)^T (Ax) = \|Ax\|^2 \geq 0.$$

Rank equality: $\ker(A^T A) = \ker(A)$. Indeed, if $A^T Ax = 0$, then $x^T A^T Ax = \|Ax\|^2 = 0$, so $Ax = 0$. Conversely, $Ax = 0$ trivially implies $A^T Ax = 0$. Since the null spaces are equal, the rank-nullity theorem gives $\operatorname{rank}(A^T A) = \operatorname{rank}(A)$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="The Same Holds for $AA^T$">
  By identical reasoning, $AA^T \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ is symmetric positive semidefinite with $\operatorname{rank}(AA^T) = \operatorname{rank}(A)$.
</Callout>

## Singular Values

<Callout type="definition" title="Singular Values">
  Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with $\operatorname{rank}(A) = r$. The **singular values** of $A$ are the numbers
  $$\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$$
  where $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$ and $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_r > 0$ are the positive eigenvalues of $A^T A$ (equivalently, of $AA^T$).
</Callout>

The singular values measure how much $A$ stretches vectors along its principal directions. The largest singular value $\sigma_1$ equals the operator norm $\|A\|$.

## The SVD Theorem

<Callout type="theorem" title="Singular Value Decomposition">
  Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with $\operatorname{rank}(A) = r$. Then there exist:
  - An orthogonal matrix $U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ (left singular vectors),
  - An orthogonal matrix $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ (right singular vectors),
  - A matrix $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with $\Sigma_{ii} = \sigma_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, r$ and all other entries zero,
  such that
  $$A = U \Sigma V^T.$$
</Callout>

**Proof (from the Spectral Theorem).**

**Step 1 (Right singular vectors).** $A^T A$ is symmetric positive semidefinite. By the Real Spectral Theorem, there exists an orthonormal basis $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ of $\mathbb{R}^n$ consisting of eigenvectors:

$$A^T A v_i = \lambda_i v_i, \quad \lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_r > 0 = \lambda_{r+1} = \cdots = \lambda_n.$$

Set $V = [v_1 \mid \cdots \mid v_n]$.

**Step 2 (Singular values and left singular vectors).** Define $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$ for $i = 1, \ldots, r$. For $i = 1, \ldots, r$, define:

$$u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} Av_i.$$

We verify these are orthonormal:

$$\langle u_i, u_j \rangle = \frac{1}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} \langle Av_i, Av_j \rangle = \frac{1}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} v_i^T A^T A v_j = \frac{\lambda_j}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} \langle v_i, v_j \rangle = \frac{\lambda_j}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} \delta_{ij} = \delta_{ij}.$$

**Step 3 (Extend to a full basis).** Extend $\{u_1, \ldots, u_r\}$ to an orthonormal basis $\{u_1, \ldots, u_m\}$ of $\mathbb{R}^m$. Set $U = [u_1 \mid \cdots \mid u_m]$.

**Step 4 (Verify $A = U\Sigma V^T$).** For $i = 1, \ldots, r$: $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$. For $i = r+1, \ldots, n$: $Av_i = 0$ (since $v_i \in \ker A$). This is exactly the statement $AV = U\Sigma$, i.e., $A = U\Sigma V^T$. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Geometric Interpretation: Rotation-Stretch-Rotation">
  The SVD says every linear map is the composition:
  1. **$V^T$:** Rotate/reflect the domain to align with the right singular vectors.
  2. **$\Sigma$:** Stretch along each coordinate axis by $\sigma_i$ (and possibly embed into a higher/lower dimension).
  3. **$U$:** Rotate/reflect the result to align with the left singular vectors.
  No matter how complicated $A$ is, its action is always "rotate, stretch, rotate."
</Callout>

## The Compact (Reduced) SVD

The full SVD uses $m \times m$ and $n \times n$ orthogonal matrices, but the essential information is in the rank-$r$ part. The **compact SVD** is:

$$A = U_r \Sigma_r V_r^T = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$

where $U_r = [u_1 \mid \cdots \mid u_r] \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times r}$, $\Sigma_r = \operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r) \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times r}$, and $V_r = [v_1 \mid \cdots \mid v_r] \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$.

The outer product form $A = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$ writes $A$ as a sum of rank-1 matrices, ordered by importance ($\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots$).

## Relationship Between Left and Right Singular Vectors

<Callout type="theorem" title="Singular Vectors as Eigenvectors">
  The right singular vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ are eigenvectors of $A^T A$:
  $$A^T A v_i = \sigma_i^2 v_i.$$
  The left singular vectors $u_1, \ldots, u_m$ are eigenvectors of $AA^T$:
  $$AA^T u_i = \sigma_i^2 u_i.$$
</Callout>

**Proof of the second claim.** For $i \leq r$:

$$AA^T u_i = AA^T \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma_i} Av_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} A(A^T A v_i) = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} A(\sigma_i^2 v_i) = \sigma_i A v_i = \sigma_i^2 u_i.$$

For $i > r$: $u_i \in (\operatorname{col} A)^{\perp} = \ker A^T$, so $AA^T u_i = 0 = 0 \cdot u_i$. $\blacksquare$

## The Eckart-Young Theorem: Best Low-Rank Approximation

<Callout type="theorem" title="Eckart-Young Theorem">
  Let $A = U\Sigma V^T$ be the SVD with singular values $\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$. For $k \leq r$, define the rank-$k$ truncation:
  $$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i u_i v_i^T.$$
  Then $A_k$ is the best rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ in both the Frobenius and operator norms:
  $$\min_{\operatorname{rank}(B) \leq k} \|A - B\|_F = \|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2}.$$
  $$\min_{\operatorname{rank}(B) \leq k} \|A - B\|_2 = \|A - A_k\|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}.$$
</Callout>

**Proof sketch (Frobenius norm).** Since $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal, the Frobenius norm is invariant under orthogonal transformations: $\|A\|_F = \|\Sigma\|_F$. For any rank-$k$ matrix $B$:

$$\|A - B\|_F^2 = \|U\Sigma V^T - B\|_F^2 = \|\Sigma - U^T BV\|_F^2.$$

The matrix $U^T BV$ has rank at most $k$. The problem reduces to finding the best rank-$k$ approximation to the diagonal matrix $\Sigma$, which is clearly the truncation keeping the $k$ largest diagonal entries. Thus:

$$\|A - A_k\|_F^2 = \sigma_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2. \quad \blacksquare$$

<Callout type="tip" title="Why Eckart-Young Matters">
  This theorem is the theoretical backbone of **dimensionality reduction**:
  - **PCA** (Principal Component Analysis) is SVD applied to the centered data matrix.
  - **Image compression**: keep the top-$k$ singular values to approximate an image.
  - **Latent semantic analysis**: approximate a term-document matrix.
  The singular values tell you exactly how much information each component carries.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<Problem title="Prove: Nonzero Singular Values of A and A^T Coincide">
  Show that $A$ and $A^T$ have the same nonzero singular values. (Hint: relate the eigenvalues of $A^T A$ and $AA^T$.)
<Solution>
  **Proof.** The nonzero singular values of $A$ are the square roots of the nonzero eigenvalues of $A^T A$. The nonzero singular values of $A^T$ are the square roots of the nonzero eigenvalues of $(A^T)^T A^T = AA^T$.
  We show $A^T A$ and $AA^T$ have the same nonzero eigenvalues. If $A^T A v = \lambda v$ with $\lambda \neq 0$ and $v \neq 0$, set $u = Av \neq 0$ (since $\lambda \neq 0$ implies $v \notin \ker A$). Then:
  $$AA^T u = AA^T(Av) = A(A^T A v) = A(\lambda v) = \lambda (Av) = \lambda u.$$
  So $\lambda$ is also an eigenvalue of $AA^T$. By symmetry (replacing $A$ with $A^T$), every nonzero eigenvalue of $AA^T$ is an eigenvalue of $A^T A$. The nonzero eigenvalues coincide, so the nonzero singular values of $A$ and $A^T$ are identical. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

<Problem title="SVD of a Rank-1 Matrix">
  Let $A = uv^T$ where $u \in \mathbb{R}^m$ and $v \in \mathbb{R}^n$ are nonzero vectors. Find the singular values, left singular vectors, and right singular vectors of $A$ explicitly.
<Solution>
  **Solution.** Since $A = uv^T$ has rank 1, there is exactly one nonzero singular value.
  Compute $A^T A = (uv^T)^T(uv^T) = v u^T u v^T = \|u\|^2 v v^T$. This is a rank-1 matrix with eigenvector $v$ and eigenvalue $\|u\|^2 \|v\|^2$ (since $vv^T \cdot v = \|v\|^2 v$). All other eigenvalues are 0.
  Therefore: the single nonzero singular value is $\sigma_1 = \|u\| \cdot \|v\|$.
  The right singular vector is $\hat{v} = v / \|v\|$.
  The left singular vector is $\hat{u} = Av / (\sigma_1 \|v\|^{-1} \cdot \|v\|) = uv^Tv / (\sigma_1) = u\|v\|^2 / (\|u\|\|v\|) = u\|v\| / (\|u\|\|v\|) \cdot \|v\| / \|v\|$. Simplifying: $\hat{u} = u/\|u\|$.
  So the compact SVD is: $A = \sigma_1 \hat{u} \hat{v}^T = (\|u\|\|v\|) \cdot \frac{u}{\|u\|} \cdot \frac{v^T}{\|v\|}$, which is indeed $uv^T$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>
