# Classification of Real Symmetric Forms: Sylvester's Law

We have seen that every real quadratic form can be diagonalized by a change of basis, yielding
$Q = d_1 y_1^2 + d_2 y_2^2 + \cdots + d_n y_n^2$ for some real coefficients $d_i$. A natural question
arises: how much can we simplify further? Can different diagonalizations produce different numbers of
positive and negative coefficients? Sylvester's Law of Inertia gives a decisive answer: **no**. The
numbers of positive, negative, and zero coefficients are invariants of the quadratic form, independent
of the choice of diagonalization. This **signature** is the complete invariant for classification under
congruence.

## Positive and Negative Definiteness

<Callout type="definition" title="Definiteness of Quadratic Forms">
  Let $Q(v) = B(v, v)$ be a real quadratic form on $\mathbb{R}^n$ with symmetric matrix $A$.
  - $Q$ (or $A$) is **positive definite** if $Q(v) > 0$ for all $v \neq 0$.
  - $Q$ (or $A$) is **positive semidefinite** if $Q(v) \geq 0$ for all $v$.
  - $Q$ (or $A$) is **negative definite** if $Q(v) < 0$ for all $v \neq 0$.
  - $Q$ (or $A$) is **negative semidefinite** if $Q(v) \leq 0$ for all $v$.
  - $Q$ (or $A$) is **indefinite** if $Q$ takes both positive and negative values.
</Callout>

**Examples.**

- $Q(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2 + x_3^2$: positive definite (clearly $Q(x) > 0$ for $x \neq 0$).
- $Q(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2$: positive semidefinite on $\mathbb{R}^3$ (taking $x_3$ as a free variable makes $Q(0,0,1) = 0$).
- $Q(x) = x_1^2 - x_2^2$: indefinite ($Q(1,0) = 1 > 0$ but $Q(0,1) = -1 < 0$).
- $Q(x) = -x_1^2 - 2x_2^2$: negative definite.

## Sylvester's Law of Inertia

<Callout type="definition" title="Signature">
  Let $Q$ be a real quadratic form on $\mathbb{R}^n$. After diagonalization, write
  $Q = d_1 y_1^2 + \cdots + d_n y_n^2$. The **signature** of $Q$ is the triple $(n_+, n_-, n_0)$ where:
  - $n_+$ = number of positive coefficients $d_i > 0$,
  - $n_-$ = number of negative coefficients $d_i < 0$,
  - $n_0$ = number of zero coefficients $d_i = 0$.
  Note $n_+ + n_- + n_0 = n$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Sylvester's Law of Inertia">
  The signature $(n_+, n_-, n_0)$ of a real quadratic form $Q$ is an invariant: it does not depend on
  the choice of diagonalization. Two real symmetric matrices are congruent if and only if they have the
  same signature.
</Callout>

**Proof.** We first show that the signature is well-defined (independent of the diagonalization), then
that it is a complete invariant.

**Well-definedness.** Suppose $Q$ has two diagonalizations:

$$Q = a_1 y_1^2 + \cdots + a_n y_n^2 = b_1 z_1^2 + \cdots + b_n z_n^2$$

where $a_i, b_j \in \mathbb{R}$. After reordering, assume $a_1, \ldots, a_p > 0$, $a_{p+1}, \ldots, a_n \leq 0$
in the first diagonalization, and $b_1, \ldots, b_q > 0$, $b_{q+1}, \ldots, b_n \leq 0$ in the second.
We need to show $p = q$.

Suppose for contradiction that $p > q$. Let $\{y_1, \ldots, y_n\}$ and $\{z_1, \ldots, z_n\}$ be
the respective coordinate systems (each related to the original coordinates by an invertible linear
transformation).

Consider the subspaces:
$$W_+ = \operatorname{span}\{y_1, \ldots, y_p\}, \quad W_{\leq 0} = \operatorname{span}\{z_{q+1}, \ldots, z_n\}.$$

We have $\dim W_+ = p$ and $\dim W_{\leq 0} = n - q$. Since $p > q$, we get
$\dim W_+ + \dim W_{\leq 0} = p + (n - q) > n$, so by the dimension formula
$W_+ \cap W_{\leq 0} \neq \{0\}$.

Pick a nonzero $v \in W_+ \cap W_{\leq 0}$. Since $v \in W_+$, we can write $v$ in the $y$-coordinates
with $y_{p+1} = \cdots = y_n = 0$, so $Q(v) = \sum_{i=1}^p a_i y_i^2 > 0$ (since $v \neq 0$ means
at least one $y_i \neq 0$, and all $a_i > 0$).

Since $v \in W_{\leq 0}$, we can write $v$ in the $z$-coordinates with $z_1 = \cdots = z_q = 0$,
so $Q(v) = \sum_{j=q+1}^n b_j z_j^2 \leq 0$.

This contradicts $Q(v) > 0$ and $Q(v) \leq 0$ simultaneously. Hence $p \leq q$. By symmetry of the
argument, $q \leq p$, so $p = q$. The same argument applied to $-Q$ shows that $n_-$ is also invariant,
and $n_0 = n - n_+ - n_-$ follows. $\blacksquare$

**Complete invariant.** Suppose $A$ and $A'$ are symmetric matrices with the same signature $(n_+, n_-, n_0)$.
By diagonalizing both:

$$P_1^T A P_1 = \operatorname{diag}(a_1, \ldots, a_n), \quad P_2^T A' P_2 = \operatorname{diag}(a_1', \ldots, a_n')$$

with the same number of positive, negative, and zero entries. By further scaling
(e.g., replacing $y_i$ by $y_i / \sqrt{|a_i|}$ for $a_i \neq 0$), both can be reduced to

$$\operatorname{diag}(\underbrace{1, \ldots, 1}_{n_+}, \underbrace{-1, \ldots, -1}_{n_-}, \underbrace{0, \ldots, 0}_{n_0}).$$

Since both $A$ and $A'$ are congruent to this canonical diagonal matrix, they are congruent to each other
(by transitivity of congruence). $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="The Canonical Diagonal Form">
  Every real symmetric matrix is congruent to a matrix of the form $\operatorname{diag}(I_{n_+}, -I_{n_-}, 0_{n_0})$.
  This is the simplest representative in each congruence class. Over $\mathbb{R}$, the diagonal entries
  can always be normalized to $\pm 1$ or $0$ by scaling.
</Callout>

## Characterizing Positive Definiteness

<Callout type="theorem" title="Sylvester's Criterion (Leading Principal Minors)">
  A real symmetric matrix $A \in M_n(\mathbb{R})$ is positive definite if and only if all its
  **leading principal minors** are positive. That is, defining
  $$\Delta_k = \det(A_k), \quad A_k = \text{top-left } k \times k \text{ submatrix of } A,$$
  $A$ is positive definite if and only if $\Delta_1 > 0, \Delta_2 > 0, \ldots, \Delta_n > 0$.
</Callout>

**Proof sketch.** ($\Rightarrow$) If $A$ is positive definite, then every principal submatrix $A_k$ is
also positive definite (restrict the form to the subspace spanned by the first $k$ basis vectors).
A positive definite matrix has all positive eigenvalues, hence positive determinant. So $\Delta_k > 0$.

($\Leftarrow$) We prove by induction on $n$ that all leading principal minors positive implies
positive definiteness.

*Base case ($n = 1$):* $\Delta_1 = a_{11} > 0$, and $Q(x) = a_{11} x^2 > 0$ for $x \neq 0$. Positive definite.

*Inductive step:* The first $k$ leading principal minors of $A_{n-1}$ (the top-left $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ submatrix)
are $\Delta_1, \ldots, \Delta_{n-1}$, all positive. By the inductive hypothesis, $A_{n-1}$ is positive
definite. By a Schur complement argument, $A$ is positive definite if and only if $A_{n-1}$ is positive
definite and $\det(A) / \det(A_{n-1}) > 0$. Since $\Delta_{n-1} > 0$ and $\Delta_n > 0$, this ratio is
positive. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="theorem" title="Equivalent Conditions for Positive Definiteness">
  For a real symmetric matrix $A$, the following are equivalent:
  1. $A$ is positive definite.
  2. All eigenvalues of $A$ are positive.
  3. All leading principal minors are positive (Sylvester's criterion).
  4. $A = R^T R$ for some invertible matrix $R$ (Cholesky-type factorization).
  5. The signature of $A$ is $(n, 0, 0)$.
</Callout>

## Positive Definite Symmetric Bilinear Forms Are Inner Products

<Callout type="theorem" title="Inner Product Characterization">
  A symmetric bilinear form $B$ on a real vector space $V$ is an **inner product** if and only if $B$
  is positive definite (i.e., $B(v, v) > 0$ for all $v \neq 0$).
  *Proof.* An inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ on a real vector space is, by definition, a
  symmetric bilinear form that is positive definite. Conversely, any symmetric bilinear form $B$ that is
  positive definite satisfies all the inner product axioms:
  1. **Bilinearity** in each argument: by hypothesis.
  2. **Symmetry:** $B(u,v) = B(v,u)$: by hypothesis.
  3. **Positive definiteness:** $B(v,v) > 0$ for $v \neq 0$: by hypothesis.
  4. $B(0,0) = 0$: by bilinearity, $B(0,v) = B(0 \cdot 0, v) = 0 \cdot B(0,v) = 0$.
  Hence $B$ is an inner product. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

This theorem reveals the geometric content of positive definiteness: a positive definite symmetric
bilinear form equips the vector space with a notion of length ($\|v\| = \sqrt{B(v,v)}$) and angle
($\cos \theta = B(u,v)/(\|u\|\|v\|)$). Indefinite forms do not define a geometry in this sense --
$B(v,v)$ can be negative, so $\sqrt{B(v,v)}$ is not defined.

## Classification of Small Forms

**$2 \times 2$ case.** A symmetric $2 \times 2$ matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{pmatrix}$
has signature determined by:

- **Positive definite** ($n_+ = 2$): $a > 0$ and $\det(A) = ac - b^2 > 0$.
- **Negative definite** ($n_- = 2$): $a < 0$ and $\det(A) = ac - b^2 > 0$.
- **Indefinite** ($n_+ = 1, n_- = 1$): $\det(A) = ac - b^2 < 0$.
- **Positive semidefinite, rank 1** ($n_+ = 1, n_0 = 1$): $\det(A) = 0$ and $a \geq 0$.
- **Zero** ($n_0 = 2$): $A = 0$.

**$3 \times 3$ case.** For $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & d & e \\ d & b & f \\ e & f & c \end{pmatrix}$,
Sylvester's criterion gives:
- Positive definite: $a > 0$, $ab - d^2 > 0$, $\det(A) > 0$.
- Negative definite: $a < 0$, $ab - d^2 > 0$, $\det(A) < 0$.

The full classification has signatures $(3,0,0)$, $(2,1,0)$, $(1,2,0)$, $(0,3,0)$, plus the
semidefinite and degenerate cases.

## Exercises

<Problem title="Prove Sylvester's Law for 2x2 forms">
  Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{pmatrix}$ be a real symmetric matrix with $a \neq 0$.
  (a) Show that by a congruence transformation, $A$ can be reduced to
  $\operatorname{diag}(a, (ac - b^2)/a)$.
  (b) Use this to prove that the signature of $A$ depends only on the signs of $a$ and $ac - b^2$
  (which is $\det(A)$), and deduce that the signature is a congruence invariant.
<Solution>
  **(a)** We complete the square. The quadratic form is $Q(x, y) = ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$.
  Since $a \neq 0$:
  $$Q = a\left(x + \frac{b}{a}y\right)^2 + \left(c - \frac{b^2}{a}\right) y^2 = a u^2 + \frac{ac - b^2}{a} y^2$$
  where $u = x + (b/a)y$. The change of variables is $\begin{pmatrix} u \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & b/a \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$, i.e., $P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ b/a & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ gives $x = Pv$ and:
  $$P^T A P = \begin{pmatrix} a & 0 \\ 0 & (ac - b^2)/a \end{pmatrix}.$$
  **(b)** The diagonal entries are $d_1 = a$ and $d_2 = (ac - b^2)/a = \det(A)/a$. The signature is:
  - $(2, 0, 0)$ if $a > 0$ and $\det(A) > 0$ (both positive).
  - $(0, 2, 0)$ if $a < 0$ and $\det(A) > 0$ (both negative, since $\det(A)/a < 0$... wait, let's recheck).
  Actually: $d_2 = \det(A)/a$.
  - If $a > 0$ and $\det(A) > 0$: $d_1 > 0, d_2 > 0$, signature $(2,0,0)$.
  - If $a > 0$ and $\det(A) < 0$: $d_1 > 0, d_2 < 0$, signature $(1,1,0)$.
  - If $a < 0$ and $\det(A) > 0$: $d_1 < 0, d_2 < 0$, signature $(0,2,0)$.
  - If $a < 0$ and $\det(A) < 0$: $d_1 < 0, d_2 > 0$, signature $(1,1,0)$.
  - If $\det(A) = 0$: $d_2 = 0$, signature $(1,0,1)$ or $(0,1,1)$ depending on sign of $a$.
  Since $a$ and $\det(A)$ are properties of $A$ that determine the diagonal entries' signs, and the
  signature depends only on these signs, the signature is invariant under congruence. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

<Problem title="Prove: positive definite symmetric bilinear form is an inner product">
  Let $B \colon \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a symmetric bilinear form that is
  positive definite. Verify all four axioms of an inner product:
  1. Linearity in the first argument.
  2. Symmetry: $B(u, v) = B(v, u)$.
  3. Positive definiteness: $B(v, v) \geq 0$ with equality iff $v = 0$.
<Solution>
  **Axiom 1 (Linearity in first argument):** By definition of bilinear form,
  $B(\alpha u + \beta w, v) = \alpha B(u, v) + \beta B(w, v)$ for all $u, w, v \in V$ and
  $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$.
  **Axiom 2 (Symmetry):** $B(u, v) = B(v, u)$ by the hypothesis that $B$ is symmetric.
  **Axiom 3 (Positive definiteness):** By hypothesis, $B(v, v) > 0$ for all $v \neq 0$. Also
  $B(0, 0) = B(0 \cdot v, 0) = 0 \cdot B(v, 0) = 0$ by bilinearity. So $B(v, v) \geq 0$ with equality
  if and only if $v = 0$.
  Note: linearity in the second argument follows from axioms 1 and 2:
  $B(u, \alpha v + \beta w) = B(\alpha v + \beta w, u) = \alpha B(v, u) + \beta B(w, u) = \alpha B(u, v) + \beta B(u, w)$.
  All axioms are satisfied, so $B$ is an inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>
