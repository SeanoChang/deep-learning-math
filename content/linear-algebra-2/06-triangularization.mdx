# Triangularization

Not every linear operator is diagonalizable, but over an algebraically closed field every operator can
be brought into upper triangular form. This is a powerful structural theorem: it tells us that even when
an eigenbasis fails to exist, we can still choose a basis in which the matrix of $T$ has all its
eigenvalues displayed on the diagonal and zeros below it. Triangularization is the gateway to the
Jordan canonical form.

## Upper Triangular Matrices

<Callout type="definition" title="Upper Triangular Matrix">
  A matrix $A \in M_n(\mathbb{F})$ is **upper triangular** if $A_{ij} = 0$ whenever $i > j$. That is,
  all entries below the main diagonal are zero:
  $$A = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \cdots & a_{1n} \\ 0 & a_{22} & \cdots & a_{2n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & a_{nn} \end{pmatrix}.$$
</Callout>

<Callout type="definition" title="Triangularizable Operator">
  A linear operator $T \colon V \to V$ on a finite-dimensional vector space is **triangularizable** if
  there exists an ordered basis $\mathcal{B}$ of $V$ such that $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ is upper triangular.
  Equivalently, a matrix $A$ is **triangularizable** if there exists an invertible $P$ with $P^{-1}AP$
  upper triangular.
</Callout>

Upper triangular matrices are pleasant to work with. Their key algebraic properties are immediate:

<Callout type="theorem" title="Properties of Upper Triangular Matrices">
  Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ upper triangular matrix with diagonal entries $a_{11}, a_{22}, \ldots, a_{nn}$. Then:
  1. $\det(A) = a_{11} a_{22} \cdots a_{nn}$.
  2. The eigenvalues of $A$ are exactly the diagonal entries $a_{11}, \ldots, a_{nn}$ (counted with multiplicity).
  3. The characteristic polynomial is $\chi_A(\lambda) = (a_{11} - \lambda)(a_{22} - \lambda) \cdots (a_{nn} - \lambda)$.
  4. $A$ is invertible if and only if all diagonal entries are nonzero.
  *Proof.* The determinant of a triangular matrix is the product of its diagonal entries -- this follows
  from cofactor expansion along the first column (all entries below the $(1,1)$ position are zero) and
  induction. The matrix $A - \lambda I$ is also upper triangular with diagonal entries $a_{ii} - \lambda$,
  so $\chi_A(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I) = \prod_{i=1}^n (a_{ii} - \lambda)$. The roots of this polynomial
  are precisely the diagonal entries. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## The Triangularization Theorem

<Callout type="theorem" title="Schur's Triangularization Theorem">
  Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space over an algebraically closed field $\mathbb{F}$ (for example,
  $\mathbb{F} = \mathbb{C}$), and let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear operator. Then $T$ is triangularizable:
  there exists an ordered basis $\mathcal{B}$ of $V$ such that $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ is upper triangular.
  Equivalently: if $A \in M_n(\mathbb{C})$, there exists an invertible $P \in M_n(\mathbb{C})$ with
  $P^{-1}AP$ upper triangular.
</Callout>

**Proof (by induction on $n = \dim V$).**

*Base case ($n = 1$):* Every $1 \times 1$ matrix is upper triangular.

*Inductive step:* Assume the result holds for all operators on spaces of dimension less than $n$.
Let $T \colon V \to V$ with $\dim V = n$. Since $\mathbb{F}$ is algebraically closed, the characteristic
polynomial $\chi_T(\lambda)$ has a root $\lambda_1 \in \mathbb{F}$. Thus $T$ has an eigenvalue $\lambda_1$
with corresponding eigenvector $v_1$.

Let $W = \operatorname{span}\{v_1\}$ and consider the quotient space $V/W$, which has dimension $n - 1$.
Define $\overline{T} \colon V/W \to V/W$ by $\overline{T}(v + W) = Tv + W$. This is well-defined because
$W$ is $T$-invariant: if $w \in W$, then $w = cv_1$ for some scalar $c$, and $Tw = c\lambda_1 v_1 \in W$.

By the inductive hypothesis, $\overline{T}$ is triangularizable on $V/W$. There exists a basis
$\{\overline{v}_2, \ldots, \overline{v}_n\}$ of $V/W$ such that $[\overline{T}]$ is upper triangular.
Choose representatives $v_2, \ldots, v_n \in V$ with $v_i + W = \overline{v}_i$.

The set $\mathcal{B} = \{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n\}$ is a basis of $V$. We claim $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ is
upper triangular. Indeed:
- $Tv_1 = \lambda_1 v_1$, so the first column has $\lambda_1$ on top and zeros below.
- For $j \geq 2$, $Tv_j \equiv \sum_{i=2}^{j} a_{ij} v_i \pmod{W}$ because $[\overline{T}]$ is upper
  triangular. Thus $Tv_j = a_{1j} v_1 + a_{2j} v_2 + \cdots + a_{jj} v_j$ for some scalars, with all
  coefficients of $v_{j+1}, \ldots, v_n$ equal to zero.

Therefore $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ is upper triangular. $\blacksquare$

<Callout type="tip" title="Why Algebraically Closed?">
  The proof requires that $\chi_T(\lambda)$ has a root in $\mathbb{F}$. Over $\mathbb{R}$, the polynomial
  $\lambda^2 + 1$ has no real roots, so the rotation matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$
  is not triangularizable over $\mathbb{R}$. Over $\mathbb{C}$, every polynomial splits, so every operator
  is triangularizable.
</Callout>

## Reading Eigenvalues from the Triangular Form

<Callout type="theorem" title="Diagonal Entries Are Eigenvalues">
  If $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ is upper triangular with diagonal entries $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, then
  the eigenvalues of $T$ are exactly $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$ (with algebraic multiplicities given
  by the number of times each value appears on the diagonal).
  *Proof.* The characteristic polynomial of $T$ equals the characteristic polynomial of any matrix
  representative:
  $$\chi_T(\lambda) = \det([T]_\mathcal{B} - \lambda I) = \prod_{i=1}^n (\lambda_i - \lambda).$$
  The roots are $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, and the algebraic multiplicity of any $\mu$ equals
  $|\{i : \lambda_i = \mu\}|$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

This gives an immediate corollary that is used constantly:

<Callout type="theorem" title="Trace and Determinant from Eigenvalues">
  Let $T \colon V \to V$ be an operator on a complex vector space with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$
  (counted with algebraic multiplicity). Then:
  $$\operatorname{tr}(T) = \lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n, \qquad \det(T) = \lambda_1 \cdots \lambda_n.$$
</Callout>

## Nilpotent Operators

<Callout type="definition" title="Nilpotent Operator">
  A linear operator $N \colon V \to V$ is **nilpotent** if $N^k = 0$ for some positive integer $k$.
  The smallest such $k$ is the **index of nilpotency** (or **nilpotency index**).
</Callout>

**Examples.**
- The zero operator is nilpotent with index 1.
- The shift operator $N(e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n) = (0, e_1, \ldots, e_{n-1})$ satisfies $N^n = 0$.
- The matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ is nilpotent with index 2.

<Callout type="theorem" title="Nilpotent Operators Have Eigenvalue 0 Only">
  If $N \colon V \to V$ is nilpotent, then the only eigenvalue of $N$ is $0$.
  *Proof.* Suppose $Nv = \lambda v$ for some $v \neq 0$. Then $N^k v = \lambda^k v$. If $N^k = 0$, then
  $\lambda^k v = 0$, so $\lambda^k = 0$ (since $v \neq 0$), hence $\lambda = 0$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Triangular Form of Nilpotent Operators">
  Let $N$ be a nilpotent operator on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ over any field $\mathbb{F}$.
  Then:
  1. $N$ is triangularizable (over any field, not just algebraically closed ones).
  2. The triangular matrix has all zeros on the diagonal.
  3. $N^{\dim V} = 0$; that is, the nilpotency index is at most $\dim V$.
  *Proof.*
  (1) The characteristic polynomial of $N$ is $\chi_N(\lambda) = (-\lambda)^n = (-1)^n \lambda^n$, which
  splits over any field with the single root $\lambda = 0$. The proof of the triangularization theorem
  only needs the characteristic polynomial to split, so $N$ is triangularizable.
  (2) By the reading-eigenvalues theorem, the diagonal entries of the upper triangular form are the eigenvalues
  of $N$, all of which are $0$.
  (3) An upper triangular matrix with all zeros on the diagonal is **strictly upper triangular**. A strictly
  upper triangular $n \times n$ matrix $A$ satisfies $A^n = 0$ (the product pushes nonzero entries further
  above the diagonal at each step, and after $n$ steps everything vanishes). Since $N$ is similar to such
  a matrix, $N^n = 0$. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="Nilpotent Power Vanishing">
  The bound $N^n = 0$ is tight: the $n \times n$ shift matrix (ones on the superdiagonal, zeros elsewhere)
  has nilpotency index exactly $n$. But for specific nilpotent operators, the index can be any value from
  $1$ to $n$.
</Callout>

## Invariant Subspaces and Triangularization

The key mechanism in the triangularization proof is the existence of a $T$-invariant subspace of
dimension 1 (the eigenspace). More generally:

<Callout type="definition" title="T-Invariant Subspace">
  A subspace $W \subseteq V$ is **$T$-invariant** if $T(W) \subseteq W$, i.e., $Tw \in W$ for all $w \in W$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="theorem" title="Triangularization via Invariant Flag">
  $T$ is triangularizable if and only if there exists a **complete flag** of $T$-invariant subspaces:
  $$\{0\} = W_0 \subset W_1 \subset W_2 \subset \cdots \subset W_n = V$$
  where $\dim W_i = i$ for each $i$.
  *Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) If $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ is upper triangular with basis $\mathcal{B} = \{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$,
  set $W_i = \operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_i\}$. The upper triangularity of $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ means
  $Tv_j \in \operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_j\} = W_j$ for each $j$, so each $W_i$ is $T$-invariant.
  ($\Leftarrow$) Given the flag, choose $v_i \in W_i \setminus W_{i-1}$ for each $i$. Then $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$
  is a basis, and $Tv_i \in W_i = \operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_i\}$ gives the upper triangular form. $\blacksquare$
</Callout>

## Computational Example

Let us triangularize a concrete $3 \times 3$ matrix over $\mathbb{C}$.

**Example.** Consider

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & -2 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The characteristic polynomial is $\chi_A(\lambda) = -(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 2)^2$. The eigenvalues
are $\lambda_1 = 1$ and $\lambda_2 = 2$ (with algebraic multiplicity 2).

For $\lambda_1 = 1$: $\ker(A - I) = \operatorname{span}\{(-2, 1, 1)^T\}$, so $\operatorname{gm}(1) = 1$.

For $\lambda_2 = 2$: $\ker(A - 2I) = \operatorname{span}\{(0, 1, 0)^T\}$, so $\operatorname{gm}(2) = 1 < 2 = \operatorname{am}(2)$.

Since $\operatorname{gm}(2) \neq \operatorname{am}(2)$, $A$ is **not diagonalizable**. But it is
triangularizable over $\mathbb{C}$ (and in fact over $\mathbb{R}$ too, since the characteristic polynomial
splits over $\mathbb{R}$).

<PythonPlayground

## Exercises

<Problem title="Prove: Strictly upper triangular matrices are nilpotent">
  Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ strictly upper triangular matrix (upper triangular with all diagonal entries
  equal to zero). Prove that $A^n = 0$.
  *Hint:* Consider the standard basis $e_1, \ldots, e_n$ and show by induction that $A^k$ maps
  $\operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_j\}$ into $\operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_{j-k}\}$ (with
  the convention that this is $\{0\}$ when $j - k \leq 0$).
<Solution>
  **Proof.** Let $V_j = \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_j\}$ for $j = 1, \ldots, n$, and $V_0 = \{0\}$.
  Since $A$ is strictly upper triangular, $Ae_j \in \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_{j-1}\} = V_{j-1}$
  for each $j$ (the $j$-th column of $A$ has zeros at and below position $j$).
  **Claim:** $A^k(V_j) \subseteq V_{j-k}$ for all $k \geq 0$ and $j$, where $V_m = \{0\}$ for $m \leq 0$.
  *Proof of claim by induction on $k$:*
  - $k = 0$: $A^0 = I$, and $I(V_j) = V_j \subseteq V_j$. Holds.
  - $k \to k+1$: By the inductive hypothesis, $A^k(V_j) \subseteq V_{j-k}$. Applying $A$:
  $A^{k+1}(V_j) = A(A^k(V_j)) \subseteq A(V_{j-k}) \subseteq V_{j-k-1}$.
  Taking $k = n$ and $j = n$: $A^n(V_n) \subseteq V_{n-n} = V_0 = \{0\}$. Since $V_n = \mathbb{F}^n$,
  this says $A^n v = 0$ for all $v$, so $A^n = 0$. $\blacksquare$
</Solution>
</Problem>

<Problem title="Triangularization over R: when does it work?">
  Determine whether each of the following real matrices is triangularizable over $\mathbb{R}$. Justify
  your answer.
  (a) $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$
  (b) $B = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$
  (c) $C = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$
<Solution>
  **(a)** The characteristic polynomial is $\chi_A(\lambda) = (1 - \lambda)^2 + 1 = \lambda^2 - 2\lambda + 2$.
  The discriminant is $4 - 8 = -4 < 0$, so $\chi_A$ has no real roots. Therefore $A$ has no real eigenvalues
  and is **not triangularizable over $\mathbb{R}$**. (It is triangularizable over $\mathbb{C}$, with
  eigenvalues $1 \pm i$.)
  **(b)** The matrix $B$ is already upper triangular, so it is trivially **triangularizable over $\mathbb{R}$**.
  Its eigenvalues are $2, 2$.
  **(c)** The matrix $C$ is already upper triangular with eigenvalues $1, 1, 4$. It is
  **triangularizable over $\mathbb{R}$**. $\square$
</Solution>
</Problem>
