## Why Limits Matter for Deep Learning

Before we can talk about derivatives, gradients, or optimization — the core tools of training neural networks — we need limits. Every derivative is defined as a limit. Every gradient descent step relies on the assumption that functions behave predictably as we take infinitesimally small steps.

<Callout type="tip" title="The Big Picture">
Limits formalize the idea of "approaching." When we compute a gradient, we're asking: what happens to the loss as we nudge a weight by an infinitesimal amount? That's a limit.
</Callout>

## Definition of a Limit

<Callout type="definition" title="Limit">
We say that $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that:

$$
0 < |x - a| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon
$$
</Callout>

In plain English: $f(x)$ gets arbitrarily close to $L$ as $x$ gets close to $a$ (but doesn't equal $a$).

### Intuition

Think of it as a game:
1. Someone challenges you with an $\varepsilon$-tolerance around $L$
2. You must find a $\delta$-neighborhood around $a$ where all function values stay within $\varepsilon$ of $L$
3. If you can always win this game, the limit exists

## Limit Laws

For limits $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ and $\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = M$:

$$
\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] = L + M
$$

$$
\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = L \cdot M
$$

$$
\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M} \quad \text{if } M \neq 0
$$

$$
\lim_{x \to a} [cf(x)] = cL \quad \text{for any constant } c
$$

These laws let us break complex limits into simpler pieces — exactly what we'll do when differentiating composite functions later.

## Continuity

<Callout type="definition" title="Continuous Function">
A function $f$ is **continuous at $a$** if:

$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)
$$

This requires three things: (1) $f(a)$ exists, (2) the limit exists, (3) they're equal.
</Callout>

### Why Continuity Matters in ML

Most activation functions in neural networks are continuous (or piecewise continuous):

- **ReLU**: $f(x) = \max(0, x)$ — continuous everywhere, but not differentiable at $x = 0$
- **Sigmoid**: $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}$ — smooth and continuous everywhere
- **Tanh**: $\tanh(x)$ — smooth and continuous everywhere

The **Intermediate Value Theorem** (a consequence of continuity) guarantees that a continuous loss function takes on every value between any two points — which is partly why continuous optimization works.

## Important Limits

Several limits appear repeatedly in calculus and ML:

$$
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1
$$

$$
\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x - 1}{x} = 1
$$

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = e
$$

The last one defines Euler's number $e \approx 2.718$, which appears everywhere in ML: exponentials, softmax, cross-entropy loss.

## Squeeze Theorem

<Callout type="theorem" title="Squeeze Theorem">
If $g(x) \leq f(x) \leq h(x)$ near $a$, and $\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = \lim_{x \to a} h(x) = L$, then:

$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L
$$
</Callout>

This is surprisingly useful in ML theory — for example, proving that certain loss bounds converge.

<Problem title="Prove: lim(x→3) (2x + 1) = 7 using the ε-δ definition">
  Use the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition to prove that $\lim_{x \to 3} (2x + 1) = 7$.

  **Hint:** Start by working backwards — assume $|f(x) - 7| < \varepsilon$ and find what $\delta$ you need.

  <Solution>
    **Proof.** Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be given. Choose $\delta = \varepsilon / 2$.

    If $0 < |x - 3| < \delta$, then:

    $$|f(x) - 7| = |(2x + 1) - 7| = |2x - 6| = 2|x - 3| < 2\delta = 2 \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon$$

    Therefore $\lim_{x \to 3}(2x + 1) = 7$. $\blacksquare$
  </Solution>
</Problem>

## Connection to Deep Learning

Limits are the theoretical bedrock of:

1. **Derivatives** — defined as $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$
2. **Gradient descent** — assumes smooth (continuous + differentiable) loss landscapes
3. **Convergence guarantees** — SGD convergence proofs use limits of sequences
4. **Softmax stability** — understanding $\lim_{x \to \infty} e^x$ helps prevent numerical overflow

In the next lesson, we'll use limits to define derivatives — the core operation of backpropagation.
