## Why Derivatives Are Everything in Deep Learning

Every time a neural network learns, it computes derivatives. **Backpropagation is just the chain rule applied systematically.** Understanding derivatives deeply means understanding how neural networks train.

<Callout type="tip" title="The Core Idea">
A derivative tells you how sensitive a function's output is to changes in its input. In ML, this means: how much does the loss change when we adjust a weight?
</Callout>

## Definition

<Callout type="definition" title="Derivative">
The **derivative** of $f$ at $x = a$ is:

$$
f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h}
$$

provided this limit exists. Equivalently:

$$
f'(a) = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}
$$
</Callout>

Geometrically, $f'(a)$ is the **slope of the tangent line** to $f$ at $x = a$.

## Differentiation Rules

### Power Rule

$$
\frac{d}{dx} x^n = nx^{n-1}
$$

### Sum and Constant Multiple Rules

$$
\frac{d}{dx}[f(x) + g(x)] = f'(x) + g'(x)
$$

$$
\frac{d}{dx}[cf(x)] = cf'(x)
$$

### Product Rule

$$
\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)g(x)] = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)
$$

### Quotient Rule

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] = \frac{f'(x)g(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2}
$$

## The Chain Rule

<Callout type="theorem" title="Chain Rule">
If $y = f(g(x))$, then:

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)
$$

In Leibniz notation, if $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$:

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}
$$
</Callout>

The chain rule is the **single most important rule for deep learning**. Here's why:

A neural network is a composition of functions:

$$
\hat{y} = f_L(f_{L-1}(\cdots f_2(f_1(\mathbf{x})) \cdots))
$$

The loss is:

$$
\mathcal{L} = \text{Loss}(\hat{y}, y)
$$

To compute $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w}$ for any weight $w$ buried deep in the network, we apply the chain rule repeatedly — that's **backpropagation**.

### Chain Rule Example

Let $f(x) = (3x^2 + 1)^5$. Find $f'(x)$.

**Solution:** Let $u = 3x^2 + 1$, so $f = u^5$.

$$
f'(x) = 5u^4 \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = 5(3x^2 + 1)^4 \cdot 6x = 30x(3x^2 + 1)^4
$$

## Derivatives of Key Functions in ML

| Function | Derivative | ML Usage |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| $e^x$ | $e^x$ | Softmax, exponential families |
| $\ln x$ | $1/x$ | Log-likelihood, cross-entropy |
| $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$ | $\sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x))$ | Sigmoid activation |
| $\tanh(x)$ | $1 - \tanh^2(x)$ | Tanh activation |
| $\text{ReLU}(x) = \max(0,x)$ | $1 \text{ if } x > 0,\; 0 \text{ if } x < 0$ | Most common activation |

<Callout type="info" title="Sigmoid Derivative Trick">
The sigmoid's derivative has a beautiful form: $\sigma'(x) = \sigma(x)(1 - \sigma(x))$. This makes computation efficient during backpropagation — you only need the forward pass output to compute the gradient.
</Callout>

### Deriving the Sigmoid Derivative

$$
\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}} = (1 + e^{-x})^{-1}
$$

By the chain rule:

$$
\sigma'(x) = -(1 + e^{-x})^{-2} \cdot (-e^{-x}) = \frac{e^{-x}}{(1 + e^{-x})^2}
$$

Now notice that $1 - \sigma(x) = \frac{e^{-x}}{1 + e^{-x}}$, so:

$$
\sigma(x)(1 - \sigma(x)) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}} \cdot \frac{e^{-x}}{1+e^{-x}} = \frac{e^{-x}}{(1+e^{-x})^2} = \sigma'(x) \quad \checkmark
$$

<Problem title="Derive the gradient of cross-entropy loss">
  Let $\mathcal{L} = -[y \ln \hat{y} + (1-y)\ln(1-\hat{y})]$ where $\hat{y} = \sigma(z)$.

  Show that $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z} = \hat{y} - y$.

  **Hint:** Use the chain rule and the sigmoid derivative identity.

  <Solution>
    For binary cross-entropy with sigmoid output $\hat{y} = \sigma(z)$:

    $$\mathcal{L} = -[y \ln \hat{y} + (1-y)\ln(1-\hat{y})]$$

    $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial z}$$

    First: $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \hat{y}} = -\frac{y}{\hat{y}} + \frac{1-y}{1-\hat{y}}$

    Second: $\frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial z} = \hat{y}(1 - \hat{y})$

    Combining:

    $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z} = \left(-\frac{y}{\hat{y}} + \frac{1-y}{1-\hat{y}}\right) \hat{y}(1-\hat{y}) = \hat{y} - y$$

    This remarkably simple result ($\hat{y} - y$) is why sigmoid + cross-entropy is such a popular combination.
  </Solution>
</Problem>

## Key Takeaways

1. **Derivatives measure sensitivity** — $\frac{\partial \text{Loss}}{\partial w}$ tells us how to adjust weight $w$
2. **The chain rule enables backprop** — decompose complex derivatives into local ones
3. **Activation function derivatives matter** — they affect gradient flow (vanishing/exploding gradients)
4. **Numerical differentiation** is a great sanity check for analytical gradients
